## 0. Executive Summary (the one-page version)

**Master brand:** **Divine Ipseity** — with PunchUp, WithDepth, Systematic Sins, and Faction of Truth as sub-lines/sub-brands underneath it.

**What it stands for (one sentence):** *Preserving the human race by dismantling the systems that put production above personhood.*

**Archetype:** Outlaw–Everyman primary; Sage–Magician secondary. The outlaw-prophet with a library. Revolutionary and philosophical. Loud about systems, gentle with people.

**The creator:** Anonymous. The brand voice is the identity. No face, no name attached. This is a philosophical and political choice — consistent with "definition is limitation," consistent with the Outlaw archetype, and protective given the politically charged content.

**The audience:** Young adults (roughly 18–32) inheriting a broken system. Curious. Leftist-leaning or leftist-curious. Tired, sometimes idealistic, sometimes paralyzed, often lonely. They want a framework for *why* things feel the way they feel — and a community of people who see it too.

**The business model:** A tiered, phased, zero-profit-capped structure. Founder compensation capped at ~4% of revenue (your formula). Workers paid fairly first. All excess reinvested into mission. Apparel (PunchUp) is the initial revenue engine. Education (WithDepth) layers on. Media and product come later.

**The growth model:** Because the creator is anonymous and starting from zero, growth depends on (a) earned reach through writing quality and philosophical distinctness, (b) collaboration with adjacent creators and publications, (c) community-first architecture from day one, and (d) identifiable apparel that acts as physical brand distribution.

**The first 90 days:** Pick the name formally. Build the owned-land layer (Substack + Discord + simple site). Publish weekly long-form essays. Drop first apparel capsule. Seed a founding community of ~100 thoughtful people before chasing any scale.

Everything below is the expanded version.

## 1. Brand Architecture — The Divine Ipseity System

### 1.1 Why Divine Ipseity as the master name

Of your candidates, **Divine Ipseity** is the one that does the most work at once:

- It carries philosophical weight (Ibn Arabi, Bahá'u'lláh — the lineage you've already rooted in) without locking you into any single religion.
- It's *self-defining in its obscurity* — the audience has to learn what "ipseity" means (selfhood, the state of being oneself), and that learning is itself the first experience of the brand. Most successful philosophical brands work this way (think "Stoicism," "Absurdism," "Acid Communism"). The barrier-to-entry is a filter that attracts the right audience and repels the wrong one.
- It scales across sub-products because it's abstract enough to parent anything: apparel, writing, video, software, community.
- It's a **Sage-Magician name wrapped around an Outlaw-Everyman mission** — which is exactly your archetype pair. The friction between the mystical-sounding master brand and the street-level sub-brands (PunchUp, Systematic Sins) creates a brand tension that is itself memorable.
- It's search-defensible. Low existing competition means you can own the term.

**Trade-offs to acknowledge:**
- High initial friction. People won't be able to pronounce it, spell it, or Google it without effort. This is a real cost in the first 18 months.
- It leans Sage-Magician harder than Outlaw-Everyman, which means your *sub-brands* and *content* have to do the Outlaw-Everyman work. That's a feature if you execute it; a bug if you drift mystical and lose the populism.

**The alternative worth naming:** If you want a more populist master brand, **PunchUp** is the other strong option. Shorter, more kinetic, more Outlaw. I still recommend Divine Ipseity because (a) it has more range as an umbrella, (b) PunchUp works *better* as a sub-brand than a parent, and (c) the anonymous-philosopher frame benefits from the mystique.

### 1.2 Sub-brand architecture

A clean, legible system:

```
                    DIVINE IPSEITY
                   (master brand — philosophy, umbrella identity)
                           │
        ┌──────────────────┼──────────────────┬────────────────┐
        │                  │                  │                │
     PUNCHUP           WITHDEPTH          FACTION           SYSTEMATIC
     (apparel,         (long-form         OF TRUTH          SINS
     physical         educational         (community,       (recurring
     artifacts)       essays, video)      membership)       content series)
```

Function by sub-brand:

**PunchUp** — The physical/apparel arm. T-shirts, hoodies, patches, pins, posters. Each drop is mission-tied. The product is a wearable flag that turns members into walking distribution. Revenue engine in year 1–2.

**WithDepth** — The educational arm. Long-form YouTube essays, Substack deep-dives, eventually courses/cohorts. Operates with the Sage voice — patient, evidentiary, no-shortcut explanations. This is where the "10-minute systems explanation" content lives.

**Faction of Truth** — The community arm. Paid or free-tiered membership layer where discussion happens, reading groups meet, and members co-create. This is where retention compounds.

**Systematic Sins** — A recurring *content series* (not a business line). Each installment names a specific systemic failure (e.g., "Systematic Sin #3: The manufactured middle class"). Operates across all formats — short-form, long-form, apparel graphics. This is the Outlaw voice's home.

**Other names in your notes — where they fit:**
- **Isonomia** — reserve as the name of your community's constitution / charter. The document that governs how Faction of Truth operates.
- **Solon** — character name for the punchup character (the figure that is punching up in the logo)
- **Unlmted** — a tagline or t-shirt graphic (*"Definition is limitation. Be unlimited."*), not a brand line.

### 1.3 Naming rules going forward

Once this is set:
- Nothing new gets a new *brand* unless it's a genuine product category. Nothing is named for ego; everything is named for clarity.
- Every piece of content tags the sub-brand: *"PunchUp drop: Systematic Sins volume one"*, or *"WithDepth video: Systematic Sin #4"*.
- The word "ipseity" appears in the About page, the first newsletter, the manifesto — early and often, so the audience learns it.

## 2. Brand Identity

### 2.1 Positioning statement

Filling the three-sentence framework from the research:

> **I help** young adults who sense the system is failing them but can't name how
> **develop** a coherent philosophical and political understanding that translates into everyday action and real community
> **without** demanding their allegiance, selling them easy answers, or trading their attention for ad revenue.

One-sentence version (for bios): *Divine Ipseity is a philosophical and political project for the people inheriting a broken system — built to preserve the human, not the machine.*

The *word* in the mind: **Preservation** (of the human being, of autonomy, of dignity). That's the word to own. Every piece of content should be traceable to preservation-of-the-human.

### 2.2 Archetype expression

**Outlaw–Everyman (primary)**
- *Outlaw* shows up as: naming power by name, refusing false neutrality, rejecting the comfort of the establishment, centering the working class, critiquing systems rather than shaming individuals caught in them.
- *Everyman* shows up as: accessibility of language, warmth toward the audience, "we" rather than "I / them," permission for the audience to be tired or uncertain without being lectured.

**Sage–Magician (secondary)**
- *Sage* shows up as: citations, evidence, historical and philosophical grounding, willingness to say "I don't know," corrections and updates published proudly.
- *Magician* shows up as: the transformative framing — *"this is what changes when you see it this way,"* naming the invisible, revealing hidden structures. Vision + action together.

**What the archetype combination rules *out*:**
- No cynicism or doom-posting. The Magician promises transformation; cynicism is a Magician betrayal.
- No gatekeeping. The Everyman forbids it.
- No rage-bait. The Sage forbids it.
- No performative radicalism. The Outlaw is *actually* oppositional to systems, not cosplaying opposition.

### 2.3 Voice

The voice of Divine Ipseity is **prophetic-but-patient**. Prophetic in the Old Testament sense — naming what is, demanding change — not the televangelist sense. Patient in the philosopher's sense — willing to show the work, willing to be wrong, willing to let the reader arrive at the conclusion themselves.

Specific voice rules I recommend:

- **Second person, plural first person, selective third person.** "You." "We." "They" only when identifying systems or the ruling class. Avoid "I" — you are anonymous. The voice is the brand, not a person.
- **Clear but not dumb.** The reader is treated as intelligent. Hard concepts get explained, not avoided.
- **Warmth toward individuals, sharpness toward systems.** Never insult the working-class person trapped in the system. Always insult the system.
- **Bible-adjacent cadence, without religious framing.** Your own notes already do this ("Are you not exhausted?"). It works because it's prophetic without being preachy. Use it.
- **Citations are part of the voice.** Show the book. Link the study. Quote the source. This compounds authority and models the epistemic standard you want to propagate.
- **Humor is sparing and dry.** The Jester is not in the archetype stack. Occasional irony lands harder than constant quips.
- **Never punch down.** Literally. It is the brand's most recognizable rule.

### 2.4 Visual direction

Since you're anonymous, visual identity has to carry more weight than usual. The visual system IS the face.

Recommended direction:

- **Typography-forward.** A strong serif or blackletter for headings (evokes prophetic, old-text weight) paired with a clean sans-serif for body. Specifically: try pairing **Playfair Display** or **EB Garamond** headline with **Inter**  body. Blackletter used *sparingly* (for Systematic Sins masthead only — it's too heavy as a general face).
- **A disciplined two-color palette.** Cadmium-adjacent **deep red** (revolutionary, but muted — think oxblood rather than stop-sign red) with a near-**black ink** and **ivory/off-white** background. One accent: a **sand dollar** for the Divine Ipseity master mark only. This gives the master brand a shimmer of the sacred, and the sub-brands the austerity of political print.

- **Wordmark-first logo.** Divine Ipseity set as a custom wordmark — serifed, understated, ornament only if it earns it. No glyph or icon at launch. Add a sigil later once the brand has mass. (Toy with the second I in Divine connecting to the leading I in Ipseity
- **Consistency of frame.** Every post uses the same aspect ratios, the same margin rules, the same type stack. Visual fluency = cognitive fluency = trust (see Part I of the deep-dive research doc).

For PunchUp apparel: **bolder, rougher, more riso-print-punk energy.** The apparel is allowed to be louder than the master brand. That visual contrast between the measured parent and the aggressive child is itself brand expression.

### 2.5 The Kapferer prism for Divine Ipseity

Running the full six-facet audit so you can check future work against it:

| Facet | Divine Ipseity |
|---|---|
| **Physique** | Serif type, oxblood + ink + ivory palette, archival imagery, no face |
| **Personality** | Prophetic, patient, principled, warm, sharp on systems |
| **Culture** | Pro-human, anti-extractive; preservation over production; community over competition; philosophy over dogma |
| **Relationship** | Mentor-peer hybrid: we are learning with you, not above you — but we've read the books and will cite them |
| **Reflection** | How the audience sees themselves: *"Thoughtful. Awake. Part of something real."* Not edgy rebels; thinking people who refuse complacency |
| **Self-image** | Who the audience actually is: 18–32, disillusioned but not disengaged, online-native, working-class or downwardly-mobile middle-class, varying literacy with philosophy/politics |

Any future brand decision that violates one of these cells is probably the wrong decision.

## 3. Content Model

### 3.1 The four content pillars (adapted from your development notes)

Your notes already worked out the pillar logic; here's a tightened version with a percentage target.

**Pillar 1 — SYSTEMS (40% of output).**
Educational, evidentiary, patient. How economies work, how geopolitics works, how capitalism organizes behavior, how power concentrates. This is the WithDepth pillar. Expertise, Sage voice. Examples: *"How the 'middle class' was manufactured,"* *"A five-minute explainer on how rent extracts wealth from labor,"* *"Marx vs. Lenin — what each actually said and where they differ."*

**Pillar 2 — PHILOSOPHY (25% of output).**
The worldview layer. How the ideas connect. How seeing differently changes acting. This is where Ipseity, divine self-hood, and the big-picture philosophical positions live. Magician voice. Examples: *"Definition is limitation — and why it matters politically,"* *"The idol and the idle: why externalizing agency is the original sin."*

**Pillar 3 — PROPHETIC / POINT-OF-VIEW (25% of output).**
The Systematic Sins pillar. Named critique. Current-events response from the brand's position. Outlaw voice. Examples: *"Systematic Sin #7: The myth of the ethical landlord,"* *"Are you not exhausted? A letter to the complacent."* This is where the recurring format lives.

**Pillar 4 — PROCESS & PRACTICE (10% of output).**
Selectively behind-the-work — but anonymous-friendly. Instead of "I," use "we" or "the project." Reading logs, source walkthroughs, how a specific essay was researched, what we changed our mind on this quarter. Everyman voice. Examples: *"What we read this month,"* *"Corrections: three things we got wrong in Q1,"* *"The reading list behind Systematic Sin #4."*


### 3.4 Anonymous-creator parasocial strategy

The research flagged a real concern: anonymous/virtual creators tend to have weaker parasocial bonds because similarity, identification, and perceived authenticity drop. You can *substantially* close that gap by:

1. **Writing the voice as a person.** Don't institutional-speak. A brand that uses "we" but still has a clear personality reads as a small trustworthy collective, not a PR machine.
2. **Strategic self-disclosure that doesn't identify you.** You can share *emotional* and *intellectual* vulnerability without identifying facts. "This essay took three weeks to write. Two of those weeks were spent wrestling with a worry that the argument is wrong." That's parasocial-building without doxxing.
3. **Name the voice.** Consider giving the brand an author handle — **"The Editor,"** **"The Scribe,"** **"The Publisher of Divine Ipseity,"** something that implies a specific (if anonymous) human. This gives the audience a character to bond with.
4. **Respond to individuals.** Under the brand handle, reply to comments, quote readers, mention regulars by their handles. Acknowledgement is the single highest-ROI retention move (from the research) and it works for anonymous brands too.
5. **Publish on a predictable rhythm.** The cadence itself becomes parasocial — the reader starts to feel the author's weekly presence.
6. **Don't hide that you're anonymous.** A short manifesto about *why* the project is anonymous (Outlaw discipline, safety, refusal of personality-cults, philosophical consistency with "definition is limitation") converts the anonymity from a weakness into a feature. The audience opts into the mystery deliberately.

### 3.5 Game and long-horizon content ideas (from your notes)

Your notes mentioned a game that learns to challenge you, and a "humanism game" that trains empathy by making you blend in. Both are excellent — park them as year-2+ projects, not launch items. The game as a Divine Ipseity product is a Creator-Product model move and belongs in Phase 3 of the business plan below.

## 4. Outreach & Growth Strategy

Outreach for an anonymous, zero-audience, philosophically-charged brand works differently from a face-forward creator. Direct cold DMs ("hi, I'm the founder of X") are neither feasible nor on-brand. The growth strategy relies on earned reach, strategic collaboration, owned-community flywheel, and the physical brand distribution that PunchUp enables.

### 4.1 The five growth channels, prioritized

**1. Earned reach through essay quality (highest priority, months 1–12).**
One exceptional long-form essay that goes around the philosophical/political corner of the internet outperforms a year of throat-clearing content. The goal in the first six months is not a quantity target; it's **three essays that get quote-tweeted, reposted on Substack Notes, or syndicated by a larger publication.** Everything else is preparation for those.

**2. Strategic guest-and-host exchanges (months 2–18).**
- Pitch essays to established publications: *Jacobin*, *Current Affairs*, *The Baffler*, *Protean*, *The Drift*, *The New Inquiry*, *Dissent*, *Aeon*. Even one acceptance is a massive audience leverage point and lends authority.
- Offer **anonymous guest voices** to podcasts whose hosts are sympathetic. Many hosts will take a voiced-but-anonymous guest — you control vocal identification (filters, pitch shift if wanted). *Chapo Trap House*, *Trashfuture*, *Know Your Enemy*, *Bad Faith*, *The Majority Report*, *Behind the Bastards*, *Some More News*, *Tom Scott Goes To* — not literal targets but a taste of the tonal range. Find the 30 podcasts in this space that match your target audience and build a pitch sheet.
- Host *your own* essay-form interviews once you have enough brand equity (month 6+). Interviewing someone notable is a growth move for both parties.

**3. Adjacent-creator collaboration (months 3–18).**
- Cross-posts with other Substacks (easy on the platform).
- Joint drops with other small political/philosophical apparel projects.
- "Reading group" co-hosts — pick a text and co-read with another creator's audience.
- Comment and reply thoughtfully on larger creators' work under the brand handle. Good replies *are* a discovery channel on X/Bluesky — people read comments, they check who wrote the best ones.

**4. Apparel as distribution (months 3+).**
Every PunchUp item is a small mobile advertisement. Design the first capsule to be legible from across a room: striking typography, short provocative phrases, a discreet Divine Ipseity mark. Every person who wears it is a walking outpost of the brand. This is the single most underrated growth channel you have available as an anonymous creator: **physical objects don't need to reveal who made them**, and they introduce the brand to people who'd never see a social post.

**5. Community flywheel (month 3+).**
Once you have ~100 subscribers, open Faction of Truth. Early members are evangelists. The flywheel research showed community-acquired users spend 25% more and churn 18% less than platform-acquired ones. Start the community smaller than you think it should be — 100 engaged members beats 1,000 lurkers.

### 4.2 What NOT to do (for this brand specifically)

- **No paid ads in year one.** Paid ads to sell a philosophical project look exactly like what you're critiquing. They'll also erode trust with a left-aligned audience who have seen every "revolutionary" startup in their Instagram feed.
- **No engagement bait.** "Like if you agree," cliffhanger hooks that punish readers for wanting context, rage-bait takes you don't believe. These trade trust for reach — a direct violation of the brand's principles.
- **No influencer-style sponsorships** (inbound or outbound) until the brand has clear independence. You can accept them later if you transparently disclose and the sponsor genuinely aligns, but not in the first year. Early dependency shapes the brand forever.
- **No face reveals on a whim.** If you ever do reveal, do it once, deliberately, and with reason — ideally tied to a major brand moment. Don't leak identity accidentally.
