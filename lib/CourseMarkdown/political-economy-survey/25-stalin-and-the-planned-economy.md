Between 1928 and 1953, the Soviet Union under Joseph Stalin (1878–1953) transformed itself from a recovering agrarian economy into the second-largest industrial power on earth. The transformation was carried out at staggering human cost. Whether the cost was *necessary* for the industrialization, *separable* from it, or part of *what made it possible* is the central debate of twentieth-century Soviet history. There is no consensus answer.

This module describes what happened, what it cost, and how it worked. The judgments belong to you.

## The end of NEP

By 1928, the Soviet Union under NEP had recovered from the civil war but had not modernized. Russia in 1928 still looked, in most respects, like Russia in 1913 — an agrarian economy with a peasantry that produced enough food for itself but did not deliver large surpluses to the cities. Stalin and his supporters argued that this situation was unsustainable. The Soviet Union was surrounded by hostile capitalist powers (the Western interventions of 1918–22 had taught this lesson). It needed to industrialize rapidly or it would be conquered.

The capital for industrialization had to come from somewhere. The Bolsheviks had inherited no colonies, no developed financial system, no foreign investment. The only large pool of resources in the country was the peasantry — perhaps a hundred million peasants who together produced the food the cities needed and could, if forced, deliver more.

The political and theoretical debate over what to do split the Bolshevik leadership in the late 1920s.

The *right opposition* — led by Nikolai Bukharin — argued for continuing NEP. Let the peasantry grow richer; let small industry grow alongside; tax the surplus to fund slow but sustainable industrial growth. This was a model close to what would later be called market socialism.

The *left opposition* — led by Trotsky and then by Yevgeni Preobrazhensky — argued for *primitive socialist accumulation*: extracting surplus from the peasantry through high agricultural prices set by the state, taxes, and forced sales, to fund rapid industrial growth.

Stalin, in 1928, broke with both camps and adopted a position more extreme than either. The peasantry would not be taxed; it would be *collectivized*. Private peasant agriculture would be abolished. Land would be merged into large state-controlled farms (*sovkhozy*) and supposedly cooperative collective farms (*kolkhozy*). Grain would be delivered to the state at administered prices. Resistance would be crushed.

## Collectivization

The collectivization drive began in late 1929. By 1934, roughly seventy percent of Soviet peasant households had been forced into collective farms; by 1937, ninety-three percent. The process was, in the technical sense, a war.

Peasants resisted. They slaughtered their livestock rather than surrender it to the kolkhoz. Soviet cattle, sheep, and pig populations fell by roughly half between 1929 and 1934. Peasants hid grain. They fought local officials. They emigrated to the cities in vast numbers, often illegally, fleeing the requisitioning teams.

The state response was systematic. The wealthier peasants — the *kulaks* — were designated as enemies. Stalin announced "the liquidation of the kulaks as a class" in late 1929. Between 1930 and 1933, perhaps 1.8 million peasants were deported to forced-labor settlements in Siberia and Kazakhstan. Hundreds of thousands were executed outright. The rest of the peasantry was given a choice between joining the collective farm or being treated as a kulak.

The agricultural collapse that followed produced the great Soviet famine of 1932–1933. Estimates of deaths vary; recent scholarship suggests somewhere between five and eight million people starved to death across the Soviet Union, with concentrations in Ukraine, Kazakhstan, the Volga region, and the North Caucasus. The Ukrainian famine — known in Ukrainian as the *Holodomor* — is recognized by Ukraine and many other governments as a genocide; that classification is still contested by some historians.

The grain that was *not* eaten by starving peasants was, in many cases, taken to the cities or exported — the Soviet Union continued to export grain throughout 1932 and 1933, making the famine a product, in significant part, of deliberate state policy.

## Industrialization

The grain extracted from the countryside fed urban workers, who built the factories. The First Five-Year Plan (1928–1932) called for, and largely achieved, the construction from scratch of entirely new heavy industries — steel, machine tools, electricity generation, tractors, chemicals. Whole new industrial cities were built: Magnitogorsk in the Urals (steel), Stalingrad (tractors), Cheliabinsk, Komsomolsk-on-Amur. The Dnipro hydroelectric plant became the largest in Europe. The Moscow Metro opened in 1935.

The official statistics on Soviet growth in this period are astonishing — Soviet industrial output is variously estimated to have grown by 12% to 20% per year during the First Five-Year Plan, depending on how one accounts for inflation and product mix. Even discounting heavily for Soviet statistical manipulation and the easy gains of building from a very low base, the achievement was real. The Soviet Union became, between 1928 and 1940, an industrial power.

The cost in human terms is harder to summarize but worth attempting. In addition to the famine deaths:

- Roughly 800,000 to 1 million people were executed by the Soviet state between 1929 and 1953, the great majority during the Great Terror of 1936–38.
- Roughly 18 million people passed through the *Gulag* — the network of forced-labor camps — between 1929 and 1953, of whom perhaps 1.5 to 1.8 million died in the camps.
- An additional several million were deported as ethnic suspects (Volga Germans, Crimean Tatars, Chechens, Koreans) and lost hundreds of thousands more to deportation conditions.
- The Great Terror of 1936–38 destroyed the senior leadership of the Communist Party itself, executing or imprisoning a majority of the party's central committee, most of the senior Red Army officer corps, and tens of thousands of mid-level officials and intellectuals.

The historian Stephen Kotkin's three-volume biography of Stalin and the work of the *Memorial* society in Russia (forcibly dissolved by the Putin government in 2021) have done the most detailed reconstruction of these numbers. The estimates have ranges; the underlying scale is not in serious dispute among professional historians.

## The mechanics of the planned economy

How did Soviet central planning actually work?

The planning system rested on a hierarchy of state agencies. At the top, *Gosplan* (the State Planning Committee) set five-year economic targets in physical units — so many tons of steel, so many tractors, so many railway cars. These targets were broken down into annual targets and disaggregated to industrial ministries, which further disaggregated them to *trusts* and finally to individual enterprises.

Each enterprise received an annual plan specifying its output targets and the inputs it would be allocated. Prices existed but did not play a market-clearing role; they were set administratively by the state to facilitate accounting. There was no real market for capital goods; firms could not freely buy inputs from other firms but had to receive them through state allocation.

The system had two major chronic problems.

**The information problem.** Planning a complex economy by physical units requires the central planner to know, for every industry, what every other industry needs as inputs. This is an enormous informational task. Friedrich Hayek, the Austrian economist we will meet in Unit 5, argued in the 1930s and 1940s that no central authority could ever have enough information to do this. Hayek's argument has been debated for ninety years. The empirical record of Soviet planning is partly an experimental answer.

**The incentive problem.** Enterprise managers in the Soviet system had strong incentives to *minimize* the targets they accepted (so they could over-fulfill and earn bonuses) and to misreport their resource needs and capabilities. The result was systematic shortage of inputs, hoarding of resources, and substantial fraud in the reported numbers. The Soviet economy was, in important respects, run on lies.

Despite both problems, the system produced enormous quantities of goods. The Soviet Union industrialized faster than any major economy except Japan. It defeated Nazi Germany in the Second World War — a feat that required producing roughly 100,000 tanks, 130,000 aircraft, and 800,000 artillery pieces during the war years, on top of feeding the population. It built nuclear weapons within four years of the United States, put the first satellite in orbit, and put the first man in space.

These achievements were real, as were the empty shelves in Soviet stores, the chronic shortages of consumer goods, the breakdowns of distribution that left fields full of unharvested grain and warehouses full of unsold goods.

## The kulturkampf

A note before closing this module. The Stalinist transformation was not only economic. It included:

- A massive program of mass literacy and education that brought literacy from below 30 percent in 1917 to roughly 80 percent by 1939
- The construction of universal healthcare that, by the 1950s, had raised Soviet life expectancy from 32 years in 1900 to 67 years
- The construction of a substantial scientific and engineering establishment that would dominate Soviet life for the rest of its existence
- The forced Russification of non-Russian Soviet republics, with attacks on the Ukrainian, Tatar, Polish, Jewish, Baltic, and Caucasian intelligentsias
- The exile and execution of much of the original revolutionary generation
- The construction of a personality cult around Stalin that exceeded anything seen in Europe outside Hitler's Germany

The Soviet Union of 1953 was both a major industrial power that had defeated Nazism, achieved universal basic literacy, and built a working welfare state, and a totalitarian dictatorship that had killed millions of its own citizens and imprisoned millions more — both descriptions are accurate, and the historical accounting cannot reduce one to the other.

## What Stalin's industrialization teaches

This is the hardest material in the course to teach without sliding into partisan rhetoric. Here is the attempt:

**The industrialization was real and rapid.** Whatever else is true, the Soviet Union of 1953 was a vastly different country from the Russia of 1917, and most of the difference was the product of the planned economy under Stalin. The achievement was not propaganda. Friedrich von Hayek, the great anti-Soviet theorist, did not deny it.

**The cost was real and staggering.** Whatever else is true, the human cost of collectivization, the famine, the Terror, the Gulag, and the deportations was on a scale unmatched outside Nazi Germany. The achievement is inseparable, in the historical record, from the cost.

**The question of necessity is open.** Could the Soviet Union have industrialized rapidly without Stalin's specific methods? Could a continuation of NEP have produced sustainable industrial growth that left a smaller body count? These are counterfactuals, and counterfactuals do not have answers. Defenders of Stalin (still found in some Marxist circles) argue that the methods were necessary given external threats; critics argue that alternative methods existed and were rejected for political reasons. Both arguments have evidence; neither is conclusive.

**The Soviet model became the reference point.** Whatever else is true, the Stalinist model became, after 1945, the template that other communist regimes would follow. China, North Korea, Eastern Europe, Vietnam, Cuba — each adopted versions of central planning, single-party rule, and state ownership built on the Soviet model. The variants differed, sometimes substantially, but the basic shape was Soviet.

## Figures to know

- **Joseph Stalin** (Iosif Vissarionovich Dzhugashvili, 1878–1953)
- **Yevgeni Preobrazhensky** (1886–1937) — theorist of "primitive socialist accumulation"; executed in the Terror
- **Nikolai Bukharin** (1888–1938) — defender of NEP and gradual industrialization; executed in the Terror
- **Sergei Kirov** (1886–1934) — Stalin's lieutenant; assassinated in 1934 (probably on Stalin's orders), the assassination triggering the Great Terror
- **Lazar Kaganovich** (1893–1991) — Politburo member; key architect of collectivization in Ukraine
- **Anastas Mikoyan** (1895–1978) — long-serving Politburo member, survivor of the Stalin years; later first deputy premier
- **Wassily Leontief** (1905–1999) — Russian-born economist; later Nobel laureate; developed input-output analysis, which was used by both Soviet planners and Western economists
