## VIDEO SCRIPT — "The Iron Law of Wages and What It Got Right"

**Runtime target:** 22–25 minutes
**Visual treatment:** Heavy use of nineteenth-century engravings of factory life, period wage tables, modern wage-stagnation graphs.

---

### COLD OPEN — 0:00–0:50

[On screen: row of nineteenth-century cotton mill engravings, then a modern Amazon warehouse photograph.]

**Narrator (VO):**
In 1817, David Ricardo published a book that explained why wages, no matter how much an economy grew, would always tend toward the minimum a worker needed to survive. He called this the *natural price* of labor. The German socialist Ferdinand Lassalle later renamed it the *iron law of wages*. The name stuck because it sounded grim and inevitable.

By the early twentieth century, the iron law of wages was widely considered to have been disproven. Wages in industrialized countries rose dramatically. The American factory worker of 1950 lived a life that would have been unimaginable to a Manchester mill operative of 1850. Ricardo, the consensus said, had simply been wrong.

This is partly true and partly misleading. Today we are going to look at what Ricardo actually argued, why it failed as a prediction, and why the structural insight underneath it is still useful for understanding why some periods produce wage growth and others — like the period most of us have lived in — produce wage stagnation.

[Title card: THE IRON LAW OF WAGES — WHAT RICARDO GOT RIGHT.]

---

### CHAPTER 1 — WHAT RICARDO ACTUALLY SAID — 0:50–4:30

[On screen: cover of the *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*, 1817.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Let us reconstruct Ricardo's argument step by step, because most people who quote him have not read him.

Step one. Ricardo accepted from Malthus that population would grow when it could. If wages rose above subsistence, working-class families would have more children, and more of those children would survive infancy. Population would grow.

Step two. Ricardo accepted from Smith that the labor force is a kind of supply. More workers means lower wages. Wage labor is bought and sold in a market, and the price is set, like any price, by the balance of supply and demand.

Step three. Combining these two: if wages rise, population grows, the labor supply expands, and wages fall back to subsistence. The system has a *natural* tendency to return to the minimum.

[On screen: simple diagram showing the wage-population feedback loop.]

This is the iron law of wages. It is not a moral claim about workers. It is not a claim that wages are *fair* at subsistence. It is a claim about the *equilibrium* of a market system in which the labor supply responds to wages through population growth.

Now — what would have to be true for this prediction to be correct?

First, the labor supply has to respond to wages through population. Higher wages, more children, more workers.

Second, food production has to be a hard constraint. If subsistence is a fixed bundle of food and other necessities, the cost of producing that bundle has to set the floor for wages.

Third, there cannot be major exit options for the working class — no significant emigration to less crowded places, no successful unionization that raises wages above the market clearing rate, no welfare-state transfer payments that supplement wages.

In Ricardo's time, all three of those conditions held — at least roughly. By the late nineteenth century, none of them did. Let us see why.

---

### CHAPTER 2 — WHY THE PREDICTION FAILED — 4:30–9:30

[On screen: maps showing nineteenth-century European emigration to the Americas.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Reason one. Mass emigration. Between 1840 and 1914, somewhere around 55 million Europeans emigrated to the Americas. That is an enormous bleed-off of labor supply from the European labor market. It is hard to drive wages to subsistence when your surplus workers are getting on boats to Pennsylvania.

Reason two. Industrial productivity grew faster than population. Ricardo's framework assumed agriculture would face diminishing returns and that food would become increasingly expensive. In fact, mechanization, fertilizer, and the opening of the American Great Plains made food cheaper. The subsistence floor moved down — and worker productivity, in industrial sectors, rose dramatically. There was room for wages to rise without eating into profits.

[On screen: graph showing British real wages 1750–1900, slow then rising sharply after 1850.]

Reason three. The fertility transition. This is the one Ricardo and Malthus did not see coming. Starting in France in the eighteenth century, then spreading through Europe over the next century and a half, populations stopped responding to higher wages with more children. They responded with *fewer* children. The expectation that children would survive infancy, plus widely available contraception by the early twentieth century, plus the rising cost of educating each child for an industrial economy, plus the entry of women into the labor force — all of these decoupled fertility from short-run wage dynamics. The labor-supply feedback loop that the iron law depended on was broken.

Reason four. Collective action. Workers in industrial economies, slowly and against ferocious resistance, organized into unions, formed labor parties, won the franchise, and used political power to win minimum wages, labor laws, and welfare-state transfers. The *bargaining power* of labor stopped being purely a function of market supply and demand. It became, in part, a function of political organization.

By 1950, in Western Europe and North America, the iron law was a historical curiosity. Wages had risen to several multiples of subsistence. The working class lived in detached houses with cars and refrigerators. The dismal prediction looked refuted.

---

### CHAPTER 3 — WHAT RICARDO GOT RIGHT — 9:30–14:00

[On screen: graph of US median real wage 1970–2020, roughly flat.]

**Narrator (VO):**
And yet.

Between roughly 1973 and 2020 — half a century — real median wages for American workers were essentially flat. The chart you are looking at is one of the most discussed facts in modern political economy. Productivity in the US economy roughly doubled in this period. Real wages did not move. The gains flowed somewhere — to capital, to the top decile of earners, to corporate profits — but not to the median worker.

How can this be? The iron law was supposedly disproven.

The answer, I think, is that Ricardo got something right that is bigger than his specific mechanism. The right thing is this: the wage you can negotiate depends on the *exit options* available to you. If you can credibly threaten to walk away — because there is more demand for your labor than supply, because there is a union behind you, because there is a welfare state that will catch you, because there is an emigration option that will accept you — then the wage can rise. If you cannot credibly walk away, the wage settles at the minimum you will accept.

Ricardo's specific mechanism — population growth driving labor supply — is a special case of this broader principle. When that mechanism failed, the principle did not. Anything that weakens the worker's exit options pushes wages down.

[On screen: graph of US union membership 1950–2020, declining from 35% to 10%.]

In the United States since the 1970s, the things that supported worker exit options have eroded substantially. Union density has fallen. Manufacturing has been offshored, weakening the bargaining position of the workers who remained. Welfare-state protections have been weakened. Anti-trust enforcement against employer collusion in labor markets has been negligible. The result is that median wages have stagnated while productivity continues to rise.

Ricardo did not predict the specific causes of this. But the structural intuition — that wages settle where the worker has no further fallback — has turned out to be more durable than the post-war consensus that thought it had been disproven.

---

### CHAPTER 4 — THE LANDLORD PROBLEM, REVISITED — 14:00–18:30

[On screen: graph of housing costs as a share of US household income, 1960–2020, rising sharply.]

**Narrator (VO):**
There is another Ricardian observation that has aged remarkably well, and almost nobody talks about it.

Ricardo argued that as an economy grows, *rent* — the income of the owners of non-reproducible assets — captures an increasing share of the surplus. He was talking, in 1817, about agricultural land. The mechanism: as population grows, you have to use marginal land. The owners of better land collect the difference. They produce nothing extra; they just own.

In the twenty-first century, the most important non-reproducible asset in most workers' lives is not farmland. It is urban housing. The land underneath a one-bedroom apartment in San Francisco or central London or central Toronto or Berlin or Sydney is non-reproducible. You cannot manufacture another acre of central Brooklyn. And the owners of that land, like Ricardo's landlords, collect a rising share of the surplus the economy produces — in the form of rent and home-price appreciation — without producing anything in return.

The contemporary economist Joseph Stiglitz has called this *rentier capitalism*. The contemporary historian Adam Tooze refers to it as *the asset economy*. The British economist Brett Christophers, in his 2020 book *Rentier Capitalism*, traces how an increasing share of corporate profits in advanced economies derives not from productive activity but from rents — on real estate, on patents, on platforms, on regulatory privileges.

Ricardo saw the structural mechanism two centuries early. He did not see the form it would take in the twenty-first century — that monopoly rents on intellectual property would matter more than rents on farmland — but the principle is the same. When economic growth happens, *someone* captures the surplus. If the someone is the owner of a non-reproducible asset, the surplus does not flow to wages.

---

### CHAPTER 5 — THE LESSON — 18:30–22:00

[On screen: cover image of Ricardo's *Principles*, then dissolve to modern graph of wealth shares.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The iron law of wages, taken as a strict prediction, is wrong — real wages can and do rise far above subsistence under the right conditions.

But the structural insight that Ricardo introduced — that the distribution of economic output between landlords, capitalists, and workers is governed by *system properties*, not by individual choices or moral merit — is one of the most durable observations in the history of economics. Whether wages rise or stagnate depends not on whether workers deserve more, but on what structural conditions support their bargaining power.

If you want wages to rise:
— make labor scarce relative to demand
— or strengthen collective organization
— or strengthen the welfare-state floor
— or strip rentiers of their ability to capture growth
— or expand worker exit options through portable benefits, easier mobility, mutualized insurance

If you want wages to stagnate, do the opposite. We have spent the last fifty years, mostly, doing the opposite.

This is not a critique of capitalism per se. It is a recognition of the structural framework Ricardo handed us. The iron law was wrong because it assumed conditions that did not hold. But the underlying logic — wages are a function of bargaining power, bargaining power is a function of exit options, exit options are a function of policy and institutional choices — has held up.

Reading Ricardo well does not turn you into a Ricardian. It gives you a framework for asking the right question. The right question is not *what wage is fair?* The right question is *what conditions produce what wage?* — that is the work.
