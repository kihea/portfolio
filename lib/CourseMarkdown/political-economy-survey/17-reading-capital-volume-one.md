*Das Kapital, Volume I* was published in German in 1867. Volume II and Volume III, edited from Marx's notebooks by Engels after Marx's death, were published in 1885 and 1894. The book is the central text of Marx's economic theory and the densest, most demanding work in the history of political economy.

This module gives you the conceptual structure of Volume I. Reading Marx in the original is part of the course; this is the scaffolding that makes the reading possible. You do not need to read Volume I cover to cover (although you should, eventually). For this module, the chapters that matter are 1, 4, 6, 7, 10, and 25. The Penguin Classics translation by Ben Fowkes is the standard.

## The starting point: the commodity

Marx begins with the *commodity* — anything produced for exchange rather than for direct use. A commodity has two aspects:

**Use-value.** What the commodity does, materially. A coat keeps you warm. Wheat fills your stomach. A book contains words you can read. Use-value is qualitative, particular, and necessary — a commodity that satisfied no use would not be exchanged for anything.

**Exchange-value.** The ratio at which one commodity exchanges for another. A coat exchanges for two tons of iron, or for twenty bushels of wheat, or for whatever amount of any other commodity. Exchange-value is quantitative, abstract, and the puzzle Marx wants to explain.

The puzzle: how is it that completely different things — coats and iron and wheat — can be exchanged in determinate ratios? They must share some common quality that allows the comparison. They cannot share a use-value, since their use-values are completely different. What is the common substance?

Marx's answer: *abstract human labor*. Whatever the specific work that produced the coat or the iron or the wheat, all of it is, abstracting from the particular, the same thing — human labor-time. A commodity's exchange-value is determined by the *socially necessary labor-time* required to produce it. *Socially necessary* means: under the average conditions of production, with the average skill, and at the average intensity of effort that the society in question is currently capable of. If you spend three days making a chair that society could make in three hours, you have not created three days of value; you have wasted two and a quarter days.

This is Marx's version of the *labor theory of value*. Notice the differences from Ricardo. Marx's theory is about *abstract* labor (the homogeneous human effort, not the particular skilled work) and *socially necessary* labor (a moving average that changes as technology and organization change). The theory is much more sophisticated than the simple "labor content equals value" formula Marx is often given credit for.

## Why this matters

The labor theory of value is the foundation on which Marx will build his entire critique. If commodities exchange according to the labor they contain, and if profit exists, then profit must come from somewhere within the labor process — not from market manipulation, not from buying cheap and selling dear, but from a structural feature of how labor produces value under capitalism.

The marginal revolution of the 1870s — Jevons, Menger, Walras — abandoned the labor theory of value in favor of a subjective theory: a thing's value is whatever someone is willing to pay for it, given preferences and scarcity. Modern mainstream economics rests on this subjective foundation. The labor theory of value is, by mainstream economic standards, an outdated nineteenth-century doctrine.

This is the place where you have to decide what you think. Modern economics has dismissed Marx on technical grounds — the *transformation problem* (how labor-values get converted into market prices) is mathematically intractable in the way Marx posed it, and Piero Sraffa's *Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities* (1960) reformulated the theory in ways that avoided some of the difficulties but raised others. Marxist economists have continued to defend, modify, and reconstruct the theory throughout the twentieth century. There is no consensus.

For our purposes: the labor theory of value is Marx's analytic device for asking *where does profit come from?* Even if you reject the formal mathematics, the question Marx is asking — and the answer he gives — illuminates the structure of the wage relation in a way the subjective theory does not. Treat the theory as a lens. The lens reveals something. Whether or not it is the only lens is a separate question.

## Commodity fetishism

The most philosophical passage in the first chapter of *Capital* is on what Marx calls *the fetishism of commodities*. It builds directly on Feuerbach's structure of projection.

Commodities, Marx observes, look like they have value in themselves. A coat costs a certain amount of money; it seems as though the value lives in the coat, as a natural property of it, the way warmth lives in the coat. But this is an illusion. Value is not a property of the coat. It is a *social relation* — between the coat-maker, the coat-buyer, and all the other producers and exchangers whose labor sets the socially necessary labor-time. The coat is the visible token of an invisible web of social relationships.

Under capitalism, Marx argues, these social relations between producers appear as material relations between things. We say "this commodity is worth that commodity" instead of "the labor of these workers stands in such-and-such relation to the labor of those workers." The social relation is *fetishized* — projected into the object — and then bowed down to as if it were a natural fact.

This is the structure of Feuerbach's analysis of religion, transplanted to economic life. Just as believers project human qualities onto God and then experience God as ruling them, capitalist subjects project social relations onto commodities and then experience commodity prices as ruling them. The cure, again, is to *see through* the projection — to see that the apparent power of commodities is the alienated power of social labor.

Commodity fetishism is one of Marx's most influential conceptual moves. It has been picked up and extended by the Frankfurt School, by Guy Debord (*The Society of the Spectacle*, 1967), and by the entire tradition of cultural Marxism that analyzes how everyday consumer life reproduces capitalist social relations.

## Money and the M-C-M' circuit

Once commodities exchange according to labor-values, money emerges as the universal commodity that mediates exchange. Gold and silver, in Marx's account, become money because they have certain physical properties (divisibility, durability) and because they are themselves commodities with their own labor-value — so they can express the value of other commodities in a stable way.

In ordinary commodity exchange, the circuit is C-M-C: you have a commodity (your labor, your produce), you sell it for money, you use the money to buy what you need. The point of the circuit is the final commodity. Money is an intermediary.

Under capitalism, a new circuit appears: M-C-M'. You start with money, buy commodities (raw materials, labor power, machinery), produce goods, sell them for more money. The point of this circuit is the *more*. M' has to be greater than M. The whole economy reorganizes itself around the production of money out of money — what Marx calls *capital*.

But how is this possible? If commodities exchange at their value, you should not be able to end up with more money than you started with. The answer to this puzzle — *the* answer, the analytic core of *Capital* — is in the special commodity Marx calls *labor-power*.

## Labor-power

Marx makes a distinction that classical political economy had blurred: between *labor* and *labor-power*.

*Labor* is the actual activity of working — what you do when you go to work; *labor-power* is the *capacity* to work, your ability to perform that activity over a given period of time.

What the capitalist buys, in Marx's analysis, is not labor but labor-power. The wage is the price of labor-power. And labor-power, like any commodity, has a value determined by what it costs to produce — that is, by what it costs to feed, clothe, house, educate, and raise the worker who is going to provide the labor-power.

Here is the crucial move. The value of labor-power is what it costs to keep the worker alive and functioning. The *value the worker creates in a working day* is something different. If it costs four hours of socially necessary labor-time to produce the bundle of goods that keep the worker going for a day, the worker is paid the equivalent of four hours of value (a subsistence wage in Ricardian terms). But the worker can be made to work — and is made to work — for ten hours, or twelve. The extra six or eight hours of value the worker produces during the working day, beyond what was paid for in the wage, is *surplus value* — the source of profit — and the next module is devoted to it.

For this module, the key point is: the labor theory of value, properly understood, is not just a price theory but a framework for asking where capitalist profit comes from. And Marx's answer — that profit comes from the difference between the value of labor-power (the wage) and the value labor produces (the output) — is unique to him and central to everything that follows.

## How to read Volume I

If you are reading *Capital* alongside this course, here is a recommended approach:

— **Chapter 1** ("The Commodity"). Read carefully. The argument is laid out in stages — first the two-sidedness of the commodity, then the substance of value, then the magnitude of value, then commodity fetishism. Read each stage once; reread Section 4 on fetishism.

— **Chapter 4** ("The General Formula for Capital"). The M-C-M' circuit. Short, clear, essential.

— **Chapter 6** ("The Buying and Selling of Labor-Power"). Where Marx makes the distinction between labor and labor-power. The famous closing line: "We therefore take leave for a time of this noisy sphere, where everything takes place on the surface and in view of all men, and follow [the worker and the capitalist] into the hidden abode of production, on whose threshold there stares us in the face 'No admittance except on business.'"

— **Chapter 7** ("The Labor Process and the Process of Valorization"). The mechanics of producing surplus value.

— **Chapter 10** ("The Working Day"). One of the great passages in Marx — a historical account of the struggle over the length of the working day in nineteenth-century England. The chapter is mostly empirical: factory reports, mine inspections, Parliamentary hearings. It is the most readable section of *Capital* and worth reading even by people who never read the rest.

— **Chapter 25** ("The General Law of Capitalist Accumulation"). The famous discussion of the *reserve army of labor* and the long-run dynamics of capitalist development. Difficult but rewarding.

— Skim Chapters 26–33 ("Primitive Accumulation"). Marx's historical account of how the capitalist labor market was created — the enclosures, the displacement of peasants, the colonial extraction. The argument we encountered through Polanyi in Unit 1 is, originally, here.

## Figures to know

- **Karl Marx** (1818–1883) — *Capital*, *Grundrisse*, *Theories of Surplus Value*.
- **Friedrich Engels** (1820–1895) — editor of *Capital* Volumes II and III.
- **Piero Sraffa** (1898–1983) — Italian-British economist; *Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities* (1960); reformulated classical value theory in ways that influenced both Marxists and post-Keynesians.
- **Michio Morishima** (1923–2004) — Japanese mathematical economist; *Marx's Economics* (1973); the most thorough technical analysis of Marx's economic theory.
- **Ben Fowkes** — translator of the standard English Penguin edition.
