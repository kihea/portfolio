The generation after Ricardo took classical political economy in two directions. The French liberals, building on Jean-Baptiste Say, pushed the case for free markets and minimal government further than Smith or Ricardo ever had. The English reformers, led by John Stuart Mill, pulled back — accepting much of the economic mechanism but arguing that political economy, properly understood, had to be subordinate to ethical and social ends. This split would shape every subsequent debate.

## Jean-Baptiste Say and Say's Law

Jean-Baptiste Say (1767–1832) was a French manufacturer, publisher, and academic. His *Treatise on Political Economy* (1803) became the standard French-language textbook of classical economics. His most famous contribution — *Say's Law* — was an attempt to settle a question Ricardo and Malthus had argued about: could there be a general overproduction crisis?

Malthus had argued yes. There could be moments when too many goods were produced relative to demand, causing prices to crash, businesses to fail, and workers to be thrown out of jobs. Say and Ricardo denied this. Say's argument, in its sharpest form: *supply creates its own demand*. To produce a good is to give the producer an income equal to the value of that good, which the producer can then use to demand other goods. Aggregate production and aggregate demand are therefore the same thing. There can be sectoral imbalances — too many shoes, not enough hats — but the total market cannot be glutted.

Say's Law was a touchstone of classical economics. Marx attacked it ferociously. Keynes would attack it again, more successfully, in 1936. The 1929 collapse and the Great Depression that followed are commonly read as the most decisive empirical refutation of Say's Law in economic history. Production capacity sat idle. Workers wanted to work but could not. Goods on shelves were not bought. The argument that supply could not, in aggregate, exceed demand was contradicted by the visible evidence.

But Say's Law has a kernel of truth that returns in modern macroeconomics: in the long run, an economy's production possibilities and its demand are connected, because incomes are generated by production. Whether *short-run gluts* were possible was the argument — and the answer, established by 1936 and confirmed in every business cycle since, is yes.

## Frédéric Bastiat and the case against intervention

The French liberal tradition produced one more figure worth knowing. Frédéric Bastiat (1801–1850) was not an economic theorist of the first rank, but he was a pamphleteer of enormous influence. His short essay *That Which Is Seen and That Which Is Not Seen* (1850) introduced what is now called the *broken window fallacy* — the error of crediting some economic activity (window-repair, after a vandal breaks a window) without subtracting the economic activity that did not happen because the resources were redirected (the shoes the window-owner did not buy). The essay is still given to undergraduate economics students.

Bastiat's broader project was a defense of free trade and minimal government against the rising protectionism and socialism of mid-century France. His satirical *Petition of the Candlemakers* — asking the French government to ban the sun to protect the domestic candle industry from unfair foreign competition — is the canonical mockery of protectionism. The piece is funnier than most of nineteenth-century economics and is sometimes the only Bastiat anyone reads.

The French liberal tradition Bastiat exemplified would, in the twentieth century, feed into the work of Friedrich Hayek and Milton Friedman. The line of descent is direct: Say → Bastiat → Walras (the marginal revolution) → Pareto → Mises → Hayek → Friedman.

## John Stuart Mill: the reformer

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) was the most important economic thinker of the generation between Ricardo and Marx. He was the son of James Mill, a leading utilitarian and Ricardo's close friend. He was raised on Greek and Latin from age three and Ricardian economics from his early teens. The result was the most formidably learned mind of nineteenth-century Britain, joined to a temperament willing to subject inherited doctrine to constant revision.

Mill's *Principles of Political Economy* (1848) was the standard economics textbook in English for nearly fifty years. It is unique among classical works for its commitment to a particular distinction:

> "The laws and conditions of the production of wealth, partake of the character of physical truths. There is nothing optional or arbitrary in them ... It is not so with the distribution of wealth. That is a matter of human institution solely. The things once there, mankind, individually or collectively, can do with them as they like."

This is the crucial Millian move. Production is governed by natural laws — diminishing returns, capital accumulation, the productivity gains of the division of labor. But distribution is a *political and ethical choice*. There is no natural law saying landlords must keep their rents, that capitalists must keep their profits, that wages must remain at subsistence. Society can collectively decide who gets what, and the only constraint is that whatever is decided must not damage the production process so badly that there is nothing left to distribute.

This is liberalism in a different key from Say or Bastiat. Mill accepted the market as an efficient producer. He did not accept the market's distribution as morally binding. He argued for inheritance taxation, for cooperative ownership of firms, for women's economic equality (in *The Subjection of Women*, 1869), and — in his later years — for what he called "socialism," by which he meant something close to what we would now call market socialism or worker cooperatives.

Mill on the stationary state is a famous passage worth quoting:

> "I am inclined to believe that it would be, on the whole, a very considerable improvement on our present condition. ... It is scarcely necessary to remark that a stationary condition of capital and population implies no stationary state of human improvement. There would be as much scope as ever for all kinds of mental culture, and moral and social progress; as much room for improving the Art of Living, and much more likelihood of its being improved, when minds ceased to be engrossed by the art of getting on."

Mill, in other words, was already a critic of growth-as-such. He believed political economy should serve human flourishing, and that endlessly growing material production might at some point cease to do that. The contemporary degrowth and post-growth movements have a direct nineteenth-century ancestor in Mill.

## Mill on freedom

Mill's economic positions were inseparable from his broader liberalism. *On Liberty* (1859) is the canonical statement: that the only legitimate ground for restricting individual action is to prevent harm to others, and that even this principle does not justify the tyranny of majority opinion against individual nonconformity. The harm principle has been argued about for 165 years. It has not been improved upon as the starting position for liberal political theory.

For political economy, Mill's *On Liberty* matters because it set up a tension that has structured liberal economics ever since: individual freedom is the ultimate value, but the actual conditions of working-class life under industrial capitalism — illiteracy, malnutrition, long hours, the impossibility of meaningful choice — do not look like freedom. Mill spent the rest of his career trying to reconcile his liberalism with his increasingly socialist sympathies, and never quite finished the project.

## Harriet Taylor Mill

A neglected figure who is essential. Harriet Taylor Mill (1807–1858) was John Stuart Mill's intellectual partner for more than two decades — first while she was married to John Taylor, then as Mill's wife after Taylor's death. Mill credited her, on the title pages of his books, as substantially co-author of *On Liberty* and a major influence on the *Principles of Political Economy*. The credit was for many years dismissed as romantic exaggeration. More recent scholarship — beginning with Jo Ellen Jacobs's 1994 edition of Taylor's *Complete Works* — has substantially rehabilitated her standing.

Taylor's *Enfranchisement of Women* (1851) was one of the earliest substantial arguments for women's full political and economic equality. Her influence on Mill's socialist sympathies and his views on women is well-documented in the surviving correspondence. She is one of the lesser-known but historically important figures the syllabus promised to include, and she earns the inclusion.

## The Manchester School and free trade

While Mill was writing, the most consequential application of classical economics in nineteenth-century politics was the campaign against the Corn Laws. The *Manchester School* — Richard Cobden, John Bright, and the Anti-Corn Law League — translated Ricardian theory into a mass movement. The League raised funds, organized lectures, lobbied Parliament, and made free trade into a moral cause. Cobden, the textile manufacturer turned politician, was the most effective political organizer of the campaign. Bright, the great orator, made the moral case.

The Corn Laws were repealed in 1846. The economic effects were ambiguous (food prices did fall; British agriculture suffered substantially), but the political effect was enormous. Britain became, for the next seventy years, the world's foremost champion of free trade — and used its naval and imperial power to push other countries to adopt free trade on British terms. The "free trade" of nineteenth-century globalization was, like the "free market" of the enclosure period, a political project requiring substantial state power to maintain.

## Classical political economy at its peak

By 1850, classical political economy had produced:

- A theory of value (the labor theory, in Smith's, Ricardo's, and Mill's versions)
- A theory of distribution (rent, wages, profit, structured by class)
- A theory of growth (capital accumulation, division of labor, the stationary state)
- A theory of trade (comparative advantage, the gains from free exchange)
- A political program (free trade, limited but real state action, gradual reform)

It had also produced two unresolved tensions. The first was about *crises*: could there be general overproduction (Malthus) or could there not (Say, Ricardo)? The second was about *distribution*: was the market's allocation morally binding (the French liberals) or merely conventional (Mill)?

The next two units take up where classical political economy left off. Marx attacked it from the left, arguing that its categories obscured a structural injustice at the heart of the wage relation. The marginal revolution — Jevons, Menger, Walras, in the 1870s — attacked it from the technical side, replacing the labor theory of value with a subjective theory of utility. By 1900, classical political economy had been displaced from the mainstream of economics. Its insights were absorbed, modified, and partly forgotten; you are now equipped to read Marx.

## Figures to know

- **Jean-Baptiste Say** (1767–1832) — *Treatise on Political Economy* (1803); Say's Law.
- **Frédéric Bastiat** (1801–1850) — *That Which Is Seen and That Which Is Not Seen*; the broken-window fallacy.
- **John Stuart Mill** (1806–1873) — *Principles of Political Economy* (1848); *On Liberty* (1859); *The Subjection of Women* (1869).
- **Harriet Taylor Mill** (1807–1858) — *Enfranchisement of Women* (1851); co-author with J. S. Mill on multiple works.
- **Richard Cobden** (1804–1865), **John Bright** (1811–1889) — the Manchester School; Anti-Corn Law League.
