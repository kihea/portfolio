## VIDEO SCRIPT — "How Neoliberalism Won"

**Runtime target:** 22–26 minutes
**Visual treatment:** Footage of Mont Pelerin Society meetings, Thatcher and Reagan speeches, archival photographs of the Chicago School, deindustrializing British towns, late-1970s gas lines, modern financial trading floors.

---

### COLD OPEN — 0:00–1:00

[On screen: a black-and-white photograph of the Mont Pelerin hotel where the founding meeting was held in 1947. Slow zoom.]

**Narrator (VO):**
In April 1947, thirty-nine men met at a hotel in Switzerland to plan how to defeat what was then the dominant economic ideology of the Western world. They were a small group. They had no political power. The people they were arguing against — Keynesian economists, social-democratic politicians, the architects of the post-war welfare state — controlled almost every elected government in Europe and North America.

Thirty-five years later, the people in that room had won. The economic order they had wanted to dismantle was being dismantled — by elected governments, by central banks, by international institutions, by the people who had once been their opponents and were now their converts. By the early twenty-first century, the program their grandchildren inherited — privatization, deregulation, tax cuts on capital, weakened labor, free trade — was so dominant that the word for it was simply *common sense*.

Today we look at how that happened. Not as conspiracy or accident. As a sustained, organized political project that produced one of the most successful intellectual operations in modern history.

[Title card: HOW NEOLIBERALISM WON.]

---

### CHAPTER 1 — WHAT NEOLIBERALISM IS — 1:00–4:30

[On screen: a slow series of photos of Friedrich Hayek, Milton Friedman, James Buchanan.]

**Narrator (VO):**
First, some vocabulary. The word *neoliberalism* gets thrown around carelessly. Let us define it precisely.

The classical liberalism of the nineteenth century was a political program organized around individual rights, limited government, and free trade. Neoliberalism, as it emerged in the mid-twentieth century, was something more specific. It was a positive program for the *active construction* of market relations across more of social life, supported by a strong state designed to maintain market conditions even when democratic majorities would prefer otherwise.

The British geographer David Harvey defines neoliberalism as "a theory of political economic practices that proposes that human well-being can best be advanced by liberating individual entrepreneurial freedoms and skills within an institutional framework characterized by strong private property rights, free markets, and free trade. The role of the state is to create and preserve an institutional framework appropriate to such practices."

Notice the strong state. This is what distinguishes neoliberalism from libertarianism. Neoliberals do not want a small state; they want a state strong enough to create and maintain markets where markets do not naturally exist, and to prevent democratic majorities from interfering with market outcomes. Margaret Thatcher famously called for "the strong state and the free economy." Friedrich Hayek defended constitutional constraints on democracy precisely so that democratic majorities could not undo market arrangements.

The program was, and is, coherent — not a slogan or a vibe, but a specific theory of how a modern society should be organized.

### CHAPTER 2 — THE LONG MARCH OF IDEAS — 4:30–10:00

[On screen: archival footage of Hayek lecturing, then dissolves to photos of think tanks, journal covers, etc.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The 1947 meeting was the start of a project that would take three decades to bear fruit. The Mont Pelerin Society itself was a discussion group; it had no political power. The political power came later, built piece by piece through institutions.

In 1955, the Institute of Economic Affairs was founded in London — a think tank that would, by the 1970s, be feeding the Thatcher government its policy proposals. In 1962, Milton Friedman published *Capitalism and Freedom*, the popular statement of the program. In 1971, the *Powell Memorandum* was written by future Supreme Court justice Lewis Powell — a memo to the U.S. Chamber of Commerce arguing that American business was losing the intellectual war and needed to organize, fund think tanks, train cadres, and engage in the long game. American conservative foundations — the John M. Olin Foundation, the Bradley Foundation, the Scaife Foundation, the Koch network — followed his prescription. The Heritage Foundation was founded in 1973, the Cato Institute in 1977, the Federalist Society in 1982.

The University of Chicago became the academic stronghold. Friedman, Stigler, Becker, Coase — eight Nobel laureates emerged from the Chicago department in a generation. The legal scholarship of *law and economics*, originated by Aaron Director and developed by Richard Posner, spread through American law schools. By the 1990s, both the American Bar Association and the American Economic Association had been substantially reshaped by the movement.

This was not conspiracy. It was patient, public, sustained intellectual organizing of the kind that left-wing critics have long admired in older socialist parties. The neoliberal movement built infrastructure that the Keynesian and social-democratic establishment, comfortable in its mid-century dominance, did not bother to match. By the time the political crisis came in the 1970s, the neoliberals had a worked-out program, trained operatives, and a media ecosystem ready to deploy it. Their opponents did not.

There is a lesson here for any political movement: ideas without infrastructure die, and infrastructure outlives any individual generation. Build patiently.

### CHAPTER 3 — WHAT BROKE IN THE 1970s — 10:00–14:30

[On screen: 1970s footage — gas station lines, layoff announcements, headlines about inflation.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The neoliberal program would have stayed marginal without the crisis of the 1970s. The post-war Keynesian model — full employment macroeconomic policy, strong unions, expanding welfare states, capital controls — had held together for thirty years. Then it cracked.

Productivity growth slowed in every major industrial country after 1973. Why is debated, but the basic shift is established. Through the post-war boom, productivity in the U.S. and Western Europe had grown around three percent a year. After 1973, it slowed to one percent. The pie was no longer growing fast enough to satisfy everyone.

Inflation rose as oil prices quadrupled in 1973 and doubled again in 1979, wage demands accelerated, and profits were squeezed. Stagflation — rising inflation and rising unemployment at the same time — was not supposed to happen in the Keynesian framework, but it was happening.

Center-left governments tried wage-price controls, incomes policies, tripartite bargaining. None of these worked durably. The political center began to look for an alternative. The neoliberals had one ready.

The contemporary economist James Meadway has called this period the *Keynesian end-game*. The post-war model had run out of road. The institutional response — the policy turn — could have gone in several directions. It could have produced an expansion of democratic economic planning, as the Swedish Meidner Plan had proposed. It could have produced a continental shift toward worker control, as left currents in France, Italy, and the U.K. were arguing. It produced neoliberalism instead. The neoliberals had spent thirty years preparing for this moment. Their opponents had not.

### CHAPTER 4 — POLITICAL CONSOLIDATION — 14:30–19:00

[On screen: Thatcher and Reagan campaign footage, then Volcker testimony.]

**Narrator (VO):**
In 1979, Margaret Thatcher won the British general election. In 1980, Ronald Reagan won the U.S. presidency. In 1979, Paul Volcker took over the U.S. Federal Reserve. Within five years, these three figures had implemented the core of the neoliberal program: tax cuts on capital and high earners, privatization, deregulation, broken unions, tight monetary policy, free trade.

Two things made the transition durable.

First, the *crisis framing*. By 1979, voters were prepared to accept painful change because they believed the existing model had failed. Inflation was eating savings; unemployment was rising; the streets were full of strikes. Thatcher and Reagan offered a story — *the state is the problem; we will free the market* — that was simple, intelligible, and matched the experience of crisis. Whatever else they did, they spoke clearly when other politicians were equivocating.

Second, the *political reorganization*. Both Thatcher and Reagan moved their parties decisively to the right and refused to negotiate with the labor movements that had been junior partners in the post-war consensus. Thatcher's victory in the 1984–85 miners' strike was the symbolic end of the British post-war labor compact. Reagan's firing of the PATCO air traffic controllers in 1981 signaled the end of the American one. The institutional infrastructure of organized labor — the precondition for the wage demands that had eroded post-war profit rates — was substantially dismantled.

What happened next was that *the center moved*. The successor governments — Bush, Major, Clinton, Blair, Schröder — accepted the neoliberal framework even when they came from nominally left-wing parties. Bill Clinton declared in 1996 that "the era of big government is over" and signed welfare reform. Tony Blair's *Third Way* in the late 1990s tried to humanize neoliberalism without challenging it. Gerhard Schröder's Hartz reforms in Germany weakened labor protections. The new political center across the West was a softened neoliberalism, with some social safety net retained but with the basic architecture — open capital flows, weakened unions, financialized economies, privatized utilities — accepted.

By the 2000s, neoliberalism was not a movement — it was the air people breathed. The financial crisis of 2008 would shake this but would not, as we will see in two modules, end it.

### CHAPTER 5 — WHY THE WIN WAS SO COMPLETE — 19:00–23:00

[On screen: slow montage — 1990s globalization imagery, dot-com headlines, EU expansion maps.]

**Narrator (VO):**
There are several reasons the neoliberal turn became as dominant as it did.

The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 removed the only major alternative model from the global stage. For forty years, the existence of the Soviet bloc had served as a left-wing reference point — a flawed and often horrifying one, but a real existence-proof that an alternative to capitalist organization was possible. After 1991, the alternative was visibly dead. Francis Fukuyama's *The End of History?* — the 1989 essay arguing that liberal capitalism had decisively won and would now spread universally — was widely mocked when its predictions did not fully materialize, but it captured the actual mood of the 1990s.

The mass-media ecosystem changed. The deregulation of broadcasting and the consolidation of media ownership produced a press that was, on average, friendlier to the neoliberal program than its predecessor. The new business press — *The Economist*, the *Wall Street Journal* opinion pages, the Financial Times — was largely aligned with the movement.

Globalization made non-neoliberal models structurally harder to maintain. Once capital could move freely across borders, a country that tried to impose high taxes on capital or strong labor regulations would lose investment to less restrictive jurisdictions. The mobility of capital disciplined democracies. The political theorist Wendy Brown has called this the *de-democratization* of economic decision-making: the transfer of central economic choices out of democratic politics and into the hands of central bankers, trade bodies, and bond markets.

Technology accelerated the same dynamic. Containerized shipping made manufacturing offshoring economically viable; digital communications made financial trading global and instantaneous; the internet made labor itself, in some sectors, geographically arbitrary. The infrastructure of the 1990s economy was, in many ways, built for the neoliberal program.

By 2007, the program seemed permanent: markets had been opened, capital was free, inflation was tamed. The Great Moderation, as the period was called, looked stable. The financial system was producing innovative new instruments — collateralized debt obligations, mortgage-backed securities, credit default swaps — that, the architects of the system said, had eliminated systemic risk. They were wrong. The next module covers what happened next.

### CLOSING — 23:00–25:00

[On screen: a long shot of the Mont Pelerin hotel today, with mountains behind.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The point of this video is not to celebrate or denounce but to understand.

Neoliberalism won because its proponents organized for decades while its opponents took their dominance for granted. It won because the post-war model genuinely had problems that needed addressing and the alternatives on offer were either insufficient (the Keynesian center) or unable to develop coherent political traction (the democratic-socialist left). It won because the historical conjuncture — globalization, technological change, the Soviet collapse — favored its prescriptions.

It also produced specific failures that became visible only later. The financialization of the economy concentrated wealth at the top. The decline of organized labor weakened the political voice of working-class people. The deregulation of finance produced the 2008 collapse. The opening of capital flows produced repeated crises in developing countries. The privatization of public services often produced worse outcomes at higher cost. The hollowing-out of mid-skill jobs fed the political backlash that has driven much of the politics of the last decade.

The neoliberal era is not finished — it is contested, and whether what comes next is a renewed left, a new authoritarian right, a fragmented multipolar mess, or something we cannot yet name is one of the questions this course will leave open.

What you can take from the story is the institutional lesson. Movements that organize patiently, build infrastructure, train cadres, fund think tanks, and shift the academic center of gravity will, eventually, win. Movements that rely on the obvious correctness of their position to carry them into power will lose — and this is not a partisan observation; it applies in every direction.

That is the work. Next module, what happened to the world the neoliberals built.
