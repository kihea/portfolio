There are two ways to live inside an economic system. One is to use the words you inherited — *market*, *wage*, *property*, *the economy* — without ever asking where they came from or who built them to mean what they mean. The other is to learn the history of those words, the arguments that produced them, and the systems they have been used to describe and to defend — and this course is the second way.

## What "political economy" means

The phrase is older than the field. Before it was *economics*, it was *political economy* — a term coined by the French writer Antoine de Montchrestien in 1615 and made central by Adam Smith in 1776; the compound word was the point. The Greek *oikonomia* originally meant "household management." Adding *political* extended that management from the household to the polis — the city, the state, the community of citizens.

So political economy, at its origin, is the study of how human beings, in groups, decide who works, who owns, who eats, and who decides. It is a single subject that braids together three things modern academia usually separates:

- **Economics** — how production, exchange, and distribution work mechanically.
- **Politics** — who holds power, how, and over what.
- **Ethics** — what justifies the resulting arrangement.

The word *economics* (without the *political*) was popularized by Alfred Marshall in the 1890s as part of a deliberate effort to make the field look more like physics. Marshall and his allies wanted graphs, equations, and equilibrium curves. They wanted to study price formation the way a chemist studies reactions — value-free, predictive, technical. The split was successful: today most universities teach economics as a quantitative science and political economy as a niche subfield of history or political science.

This course refuses the split, because the split makes the subject impossible to understand. You cannot explain why a factory worker in Manchester in 1830 earned what she earned without knowing who owned the factory, who passed the Combination Acts, and who wrote the laws that made the union she might have joined a criminal conspiracy. The mechanics, the politics, and the ethics travel together. We will treat them that way.

## Why this matters for someone who is not an economist

You already live inside an economic system. It set the terms of the job you have or the job you cannot find. It decided how much rent you pay and to whom. It is the reason your country is at war with that country, the reason your phone costs what it costs, the reason your grandmother's pension was structured the way it was, the reason a hospital visit in your city costs what it does. Every consequential thing in your material life — and most of the consequential things in your emotional life — runs through choices made inside an economic order.

You do not have to like this or think it fair, but you should not be illiterate about it.

The political economists of the past three hundred years were people trying to answer questions like:

- Why is some labor paid and some unpaid?
- Why do some countries get richer than others?
- What makes a thing valuable — the labor that made it, the desire someone has for it, or what it costs to produce it?
- Is there a natural limit to how unequal a society can become before something gives?
- Can a society choose its economic system, or does the system choose itself?
- What does freedom mean inside a wage relationship?

These are not closed questions. The answers given by Adam Smith, Karl Marx, John Maynard Keynes, Friedrich Hayek, and Milton Friedman differ in fundamental ways. Each of them built a coherent and intelligent system. Each of them was disastrously wrong about some things and brilliantly right about others. By the end of this course you will know which is which.

## The shape of what is ahead

We will move forward in roughly chronological order, but the chronology is the spine, not the point. The point is to build a working vocabulary you can use to read any economic arrangement — past, present, or proposed — and ask the right questions.

We begin in pre-history with subsistence economies and gift exchange — partly because that is where the human story begins, and partly because the assumptions that anthropologists used to make about "primitive" economies were exactly the assumptions classical economists wrote into their theories of capitalism. To understand Smith, you have to see what Smith got wrong about hunter-gatherers.

Then feudalism, mercantilism, and the long birth of capitalism. Then Smith and his successors. Then Marx and his successors. Then the twentieth century, when political economy stopped being an academic question and became the basis on which whole governments were built and overthrown. Then neoliberalism, the economic philosophy that has dominated the past forty years. Then a synthesis.

The course ends, not with a verdict, but with you having enough vocabulary to render one yourself.

## A warning about the field

Political economy attracts people with strong views. Some of the readings ahead were written to persuade you. Some were written to defend a regime that killed people. Some were written to justify an arrangement the writer was profiting from. We will read all of them seriously, because the only way to think about these questions is to read the smartest people who answered them and follow their arguments. But we will also read the historians who came after, and the critics who got their hands dirty in the archives. The classroom rule is simple: take every thinker at his or her strongest, then ask what they could not see.

## What you will be able to do

By the end of forty hours, you will be able to:

1. Trace the genealogy of any major economic concept — *capital*, *labor*, *value*, *market*, *rent* — back to the argument that produced it.
2. Read the original sentences of Smith, Marx, Keynes, Hayek, and Friedman without translation help.
3. Describe how at least eight historical economic systems actually functioned — not in slogans, but in mechanics.
4. Tell the difference between a critique of capitalism, a defense of capitalism, and a description of capitalism — and know which one a given writer is doing.
5. Look at a contemporary phenomenon — gig work, the housing market, climate policy, AI — and read it through any of the major traditions on demand.

That is what "near-expert" means in this course. Not that you will know everything (no one does) but that you will be inside the conversation.

## How to read this course

Slow. The single most common mistake students make with political economy is reading it like a news article. These writers were arguing with each other for centuries. Every paragraph is loaded. Reread when you do not understand. Look up names. Sit with the disagreements before you resolve them.

Take notes by hand. There is research from cognitive science suggesting handwritten notes produce better retention and conceptual understanding than typing, but the more important reason is psychological: writing by hand slows you down enough that you cannot pretend to have understood something you have not.

Trust the order of the modules. We have spent a long time arranging them. Concepts that show up early get loaded with meaning by concepts that come later. If you skip ahead, the system will not punish you, but you will get less.

Take the quizzes seriously. They are not gatekeeping — they are calibration. If you cannot pass the quiz, you have not yet absorbed the unit, and you will be lost in the next one.

Now turn the page.
