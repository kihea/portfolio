The previous module introduced Marx's labor theory of value and the distinction between labor and labor-power. This module unpacks what Marx does with these tools — the theory of *surplus value*, which is the analytic heart of Volume I of *Capital*.

This is the single most important concept in Marx. Read it once carefully. If you do not understand it, reread. Subsequent modules will assume you have it.

## The setup

Recall the M-C-M' circuit. A capitalist begins with money (M). He uses it to buy commodities (C) — raw materials, machinery, and crucially, labor-power. Production happens. He ends up with finished commodities, which he sells for money (M'). For the circuit to be capitalism, M' must be greater than M.

Where does the surplus — the difference between M' and M — come from?

The puzzle: if all commodities exchange at their values, the capitalist has paid for everything he bought at its true value, and he sells everything at its true value. No commodity has been overvalued or undervalued in the exchange. So where does the surplus emerge?

Marx's answer is that one of the commodities the capitalist buys — labor-power — has a unique property. Its *use-value* is the capacity to produce *more value* than it itself costs.

## The two values of labor-power

The capitalist pays the value of labor-power, which is the value of the goods needed to reproduce the worker — food, clothing, housing, the basic costs of keeping the worker alive and capable of working tomorrow. Suppose, for the sake of a clean illustration, that producing this bundle of subsistence goods requires four hours of socially necessary labor.

So labor-power is worth four hours' value. The capitalist pays the worker the equivalent of four hours of value — a wage that allows the worker to buy the subsistence bundle.

But what does the capitalist *get* in exchange? The capacity to set the worker to work for an entire working day. And the working day is not four hours. It is eight, ten, twelve hours, depending on the period and the industry.

In the first four hours, the worker produces value equivalent to her wage — to what it costs to keep her alive. This is *necessary labor*. In the remaining hours of the working day, the worker continues producing value, but now she is no longer being paid for it. She is producing value over and above what was paid for. This excess is *surplus labor*, and the value it produces is *surplus value*.

The capitalist's profit, in Marx's analysis, is surplus value. It is the value produced by the worker in the hours of the day that go uncompensated.

## The rate of exploitation

Marx defines the *rate of surplus value* (or *rate of exploitation*) as the ratio of surplus labor to necessary labor. If the working day is twelve hours and four hours are necessary (covering the wage), then eight hours are surplus. The rate of exploitation is 8/4 = 200%.

This is a technical term in Marx's economics. It does not, in the technical sense, mean "the worker is treated badly" or "the worker is paid poorly." It means precisely: the ratio of unpaid labor to paid labor. A worker who is paid generously can still be exploited in the Marxian sense — perhaps especially so, since a high-productivity worker may produce far more value than her wage represents.

Notice the political force of this definition. Under feudalism, the serf worked some days of the week on her own strips and other days on the lord's demesne. The extraction of surplus labor was visible — the serf knew which days were hers and which were the lord's. Under capitalism, the same structural relation exists, but it is invisible. The worker is at her job for ten hours. Each hour looks identical to every other. The fact that some hours produce value she keeps and others produce value the capitalist keeps is not immediately apparent. The wage form obscures the structure of extraction. This is one of Marx's deepest claims about why capitalism feels different from feudalism even when, structurally, it accomplishes the same surplus extraction.

## Absolute and relative surplus value

Marx distinguishes two ways the capitalist can increase the rate of exploitation.

**Absolute surplus value.** Extend the working day. If the working day was ten hours and you make it twelve, and the necessary labor (the wage-equivalent) stays at four hours, then surplus labor has grown from six hours to eight hours. The rate of exploitation has risen.

This was the dominant strategy in the early industrial revolution. Working days of twelve, fourteen, sixteen hours were common in early-nineteenth-century English factories. Children worked alongside adults. The mortality rates were appalling. Marx devotes Chapter 10 of *Capital* — the most readable section of the book — to the long political struggle in nineteenth-century England to limit the working day. The Factory Acts of 1833, 1844, 1847, and 1850 progressively reduced the legal maximum working day for women and children and indirectly for adult men. Marx reads this struggle as a structural conflict over absolute surplus value.

**Relative surplus value.** Reduce the necessary labor portion of the working day by making the production of the worker's subsistence bundle cheaper. If technological improvements allow food, clothing, and housing to be produced more efficiently, the labor required to produce them falls — and necessary labor (which corresponds to that value) falls along with it. The working day stays the same length, but the share of it that is unpaid grows.

This was the dominant strategy in mature industrial capitalism, especially from the late nineteenth century onward. As manufacturing productivity rose, the cost of basic consumer goods fell. Real wages could rise — the worker could buy more goods than her grandmother — while the *rate of exploitation* also rose, because the labor required to produce her bundle of subsistence fell faster than her wages rose.

This is the most important Marxian insight for understanding why a modern American factory worker can earn what looks like a comfortable wage and still be, in Marx's technical sense, more exploited than her nineteenth-century counterpart. Her wage buys more goods. Her wage as a *share* of the value she produces, however, has shrunk.

## The class struggle implication

Marx's distinction between absolute and relative surplus value also explains the structure of nineteenth-century labor conflict.

Absolute surplus value pits worker against capitalist over the *length* of the working day. Every hour reduced from the working day is an hour of surplus labor lost to the capitalist. Workers in this era fought for the eight-hour day, the ten-hour day, the abolition of child labor. These were structural battles, and they were eventually won — in nineteenth-century Britain through legislation, in twentieth-century America largely through union contracts.

Relative surplus value pits worker against capitalist over the *productivity* of the working day — over technology, work intensity, mechanization. The capitalist's interest is to increase the output per hour, which (other things equal) reduces necessary labor and increases surplus labor. The worker's interest is to ensure that productivity gains are *shared* — that wages rise with productivity rather than only the rate of profit.

The remarkable feature of the post-1973 period in the United States is that productivity has continued to grow but wages have not. The gains have flowed entirely to capital. In Marxian terms: the rate of exploitation has risen dramatically. Whether you accept the underlying theory or not, the *fact* that the wage-to-productivity gap has grown sustained over fifty years is one of the central facts of modern political economy. You cannot read contemporary debates about inequality without understanding it, and Marx provides the most parsimonious analytic framework for describing it.

## Why this analysis is not the same as "the worker is paid too little"

It is worth stating clearly what Marx is and is not saying.

**Not saying:** That the wage relation is morally identical to slavery. Marx is clear that there is a real difference. The wage worker is formally free; she can quit, can change employers, can in some sense bargain. The slave cannot.

**Not saying:** That capitalists are evil people. Capitalists, in Marx's analysis, are *bearers* of structural roles. An individual capitalist who refused to extract surplus value would simply go bankrupt, and his more efficient competitors would take his place. The system selects for capitalists who maximize surplus extraction.

**Not saying:** That the wage is "less than it should be" in some moral sense that could be remedied within capitalism. Marx is making a structural claim, not an ethical one. The wage equals the value of labor-power. The value of labor-power is less than the value the worker produces. This is a structural feature of the system, not a violation of its rules.

**What he is saying.** That the appearance of capitalist exchange as fair — a free worker, an honest wage, a market price — conceals a structural extraction analogous to (but more obscured than) the extraction of feudal rent or slave labor. The form of the relation has changed; the function has not.

You do not have to accept this analysis, but you do have to engage with it, because most of twentieth-century critical thought rests on it.

## The mainstream economist's objections

Three standard objections to the theory of surplus value:

**Capital also produces value.** The capitalist provides not just money but organization, risk-bearing, and the productive use of machinery. Why is this not also a source of value? Marx's answer: machinery transfers its own value (the labor that produced it) to the product, but does not produce new value on top of it. Only living labor creates new value. This is a controversial claim. The post-Keynesian and Sraffian traditions have proposed alternative formulations.

**The transformation problem.** Marx's labor-values are not, mathematically, the same as market prices. The transformation between the two requires reallocation of surplus value across industries with different capital-labor ratios. Marx attempted this transformation in *Capital* Volume III. The mathematics of his attempt does not work — a fact known since Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk in 1896, refined by Ladislaus von Bortkiewicz in 1907, and re-examined by every generation of Marxist economists since. The Sraffian framework offers an alternative but raises its own difficulties.

**Empirical correlation between labor and price.** Critics argue that prices are not, in fact, well-correlated with labor content in modern economies. Defenders point to studies (Anwar Shaikh, Andrew Kliman, and others) that argue the empirical correlation is stronger than mainstream economists allow.

We will not resolve these debates. You should know they exist. Marxian economics is a continuing research program with both rigorous defenders and serious critics. The popular dismissal of Marx as "disproven" is no more accurate than the popular dismissal of Smith as "vindicated."

## What to take from this module

If you take one thing: the wage you receive is not the value you produce — the gap between them is what makes capitalism capitalism rather than feudalism, slavery, or subsistence. Whether you call that difference *surplus value*, *profit*, *return to capital*, or something else is partly a question of theoretical commitment. The structural fact — that the wage and the output diverge, and the gap goes somewhere — is independent of vocabulary.

## Figures to know

- **Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk** (1851–1914) — Austrian economist; *Karl Marx and the Close of His System* (1896); the first systematic critique of Marx's transformation problem.
- **Ladislaus von Bortkiewicz** (1868–1931) — Russian-Polish economist; refined the Böhm-Bawerk critique mathematically.
- **Anwar Shaikh** (b. 1945) — American economist; defender and reconstructor of classical-Marxian value theory; *Capitalism: Competition, Conflict, Crises* (2016).
- **Michio Morishima** (1923–2004) — Japanese economist; *Marx's Economics* (1973); the most mathematically rigorous Marxist economic theorist.
- **Diane Elson** (1946–2021) — British feminist economist; argued that Marx's value theory must be extended to include unpaid reproductive labor.

## Reflection prompt

Compute, roughly, your own rate of exploitation. Take your annual wage. Take your employer's revenue per employee (often available in public financial reports, or estimable). The ratio of the second to the first is, approximately, the value you produce relative to what you receive. The gap is, in Marxian terms, the rate of exploitation plus some overhead. Do not take the number as precise. Take it as a structural intuition. What does the order of magnitude tell you?
