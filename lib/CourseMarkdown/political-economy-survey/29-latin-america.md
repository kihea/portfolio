The twentieth-century Latin American left produced experiments that did not fit either the Soviet or the social-democratic mold. They were attempts to build democratic socialism in countries that had been kept poor by a combination of resource-extractive economies, oligarchic land tenure, and U.S. political-military intervention. Some succeeded, some were destroyed, all are essential to understanding political economy in the global south.

This module covers three episodes: Allende's Chile (1970–73), revolutionary Cuba (1959 onward), and the early-twenty-first-century *Pink Tide* of left governments across the region.

## Allende and Chilean democratic socialism (1970–1973)

Salvador Allende (1908–1973) was a Chilean physician and senator who had run for the presidency three times before finally winning in 1970 as the candidate of *Unidad Popular* — a coalition of socialists, communists, radicals, and Christian-left parties. He took office with 36.6 percent of the vote, no congressional majority, and a clear program: nationalize the copper mines, accelerate land reform, expand education and healthcare, and build democratic socialism through constitutional means.

The Allende government's first eighteen months produced substantial achievements. Copper — Chile's principal export, then largely owned by U.S. companies Anaconda and Kennecott — was nationalized by unanimous vote of the Chilean Congress in July 1971. Land reform accelerated, redistributing large *latifundia* to peasant cooperatives. Wages rose. Inflation, low in 1971, began to accelerate. By 1972, the government faced mounting economic strain: capital flight, falling copper prices on world markets, food shortages, and disruption of distribution networks.

A crucial part of the Allende story is the U.S. intervention. The Nixon administration — Henry Kissinger reportedly saying "I don't see why we need to stand by and watch a country go communist due to the irresponsibility of its own people" — moved to destabilize the government. Declassified documents have established:

- A $10 million CIA covert action campaign against Allende's election (before his victory)
- Economic pressure including freezing of U.S. credits and orchestrated capital flight
- Support for strikes, especially the truckers' strike of October 1972 that paralyzed distribution
- Coordination with Chilean military officers planning a coup

Project Cybersyn — a remarkable Allende-era experiment in computerized economic management designed by the British cybernetician Stafford Beer — attempted to provide the Allende government with real-time information on the state of the economy through a network of telex machines and a central operations room. The system was partially operational by 1973, when the coup destroyed it.

On September 11, 1973, the Chilean military under General Augusto Pinochet bombed La Moneda, the presidential palace. Allende died inside, almost certainly by suicide. The coup was followed by a seventeen-year military dictatorship in which somewhere between 3,000 and 4,000 people were killed or disappeared, tens of thousands tortured, and hundreds of thousands forced into exile.

Pinochet's regime then implemented a radical neoliberal economic program designed by the *Chicago Boys* — Chilean economists who had been trained at the University of Chicago under Milton Friedman in the 1960s. Chile became the first laboratory for the full neoliberal program: privatization of state enterprises, deregulation, opening to foreign capital, dismantling of unions, privatization of pensions and (partially) healthcare. The economic results were initially disastrous (the 1982 collapse) and ultimately, by some measures, strong, with Chile becoming Latin America's wealthiest economy by the 2000s — though with persistently high inequality and substantial sections of the population in poverty.

Allende's Chile is one of the most significant cases in twentieth-century political economy because it tested, under hard conditions, whether democratic socialism could be implemented in a developing country with U.S. opposition. The answer turned out to be no, in part because the U.S. would not let it be yes. The lesson cuts both ways: defenders of Allende argue that the experiment was destroyed before it could succeed; critics argue that the internal contradictions of the program — the inflation, the shortages, the political polarization — would have produced collapse anyway. Both arguments have evidence. Neither is definitive.

## Cuba: the longest-running revolution

Fidel Castro (1926–2016) and a small guerrilla force descended from the Sierra Maestra mountains in late 1958 and overthrew the Batista dictatorship in January 1959. The Cuban Revolution was not initially Marxist. Castro had been educated in the law and was a Cuban nationalist of the *partido ortodoxo* tradition. The radicalization came in stages — the U.S. economic embargo of 1960, the Bay of Pigs invasion in 1961, the alignment with the Soviet Union, the Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962 — and culminated in Castro's declaration in December 1961 that he was, and always had been, a Marxist-Leninist.

Cuban political economy after 1961 followed a roughly Soviet model with distinctive features:

**Universal healthcare and education.** Cuba achieved literacy rates above 99 percent by the 1970s. Cuban infant mortality is among the lowest in the Western Hemisphere. Cuban medical training programs export doctors throughout Latin America and Africa.

**Land reform.** Large estates were nationalized; small farms were left in private hands. Cuba differs from Soviet experience in that significant private agricultural production continued.

**Single-party rule.** The Cuban Communist Party has held a constitutional monopoly on political organization since 1965. Political opposition is illegal. The country has had two leaders — Fidel and his brother Raúl — for over six decades; in 2018, Miguel Díaz-Canel became the first non-Castro president.

**Dependence on external support.** The Cuban economy was substantially supported by Soviet subsidies, principally in the form of preferential trade arrangements for sugar, until 1991. The *Special Period* of the 1990s — the economic catastrophe that followed the Soviet collapse — produced widespread hunger and a roughly thirty percent contraction of GDP. Cuba survived through emergency rationing, urban agriculture, and Venezuelan oil subsidies under Hugo Chávez.

**The U.S. embargo.** In place since 1960, repeatedly tightened, the embargo has done enormous economic damage to Cuba while failing in its stated goal of regime change. Whether Cuba's economic dysfunctions are primarily caused by the embargo or by the Cuban government's own policies is the central argument about Cuban political economy; both factors are real.

The Cuban model is admired and criticized. Health and education achievements are real and substantial, as is the political repression. Whether the model is replicable in conditions of less foreign hostility, or sustainable in conditions of continued isolation, remains open.

## The Pink Tide

In the late 1990s and 2000s, a wave of left and center-left governments came to power across Latin America: Hugo Chávez in Venezuela (1999), Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva in Brazil (2002), Néstor and Cristina Kirchner in Argentina (2003 and 2007), Tabaré Vázquez in Uruguay (2005), Evo Morales in Bolivia (2006), Rafael Correa in Ecuador (2007), Daniel Ortega's return in Nicaragua (2007), Fernando Lugo in Paraguay (2008), José Mujica in Uruguay (2010), Andrés Manuel López Obrador in Mexico (2018), Gabriel Boric in Chile (2022).

The *Pink Tide* (so called because it was less red than the cold-war socialist tradition) was a heterogeneous phenomenon. Some governments were radical (Venezuela, Bolivia); some were moderate social-democratic (Brazil, Uruguay); some were populist nationalist (Argentina). They shared:

- An emphasis on reducing poverty and inequality through direct cash transfers and expanded social programs
- Resource nationalism — renegotiating contracts with foreign mining and oil companies for better state revenues
- Skepticism of Washington Consensus economic prescriptions
- Strong regional cooperation (UNASUR, ALBA, the Bank of the South)

The achievements were substantial. Brazil's *Bolsa Família* cash transfer program lifted tens of millions out of extreme poverty. Bolivia's nationalization of hydrocarbons substantially increased state revenue. Venezuelan oil wealth funded mass literacy and healthcare programs in the early Chávez years.

The contradictions were also substantial. The Pink Tide governments depended on commodity prices — oil for Venezuela and Ecuador, soy for Argentina, minerals for Bolivia, iron for Brazil — that boomed during the 2000s and collapsed after 2014. The economic models did not diversify away from commodity dependence; redistribution depended on commodity rents that proved cyclical. Venezuela under Maduro collapsed into hyperinflation, mass emigration, and authoritarianism. Brazil's Workers' Party (PT) was destabilized by corruption scandals and the controversial 2016 impeachment of Dilma Rousseff. Bolivia's MAS government was destabilized by the 2019 crisis.

By 2020, the Pink Tide had partially receded. A second wave (often called the *Marea Rosa 2.0*) began in 2018–22 with Mexico's AMLO, Argentina's Alberto Fernández, Peru's Pedro Castillo (overthrown in 2022), Chile's Boric, Colombia's Gustavo Petro, Brazil's Lula returning in 2023, and Honduras's Xiomara Castro. The second wave has been more electorally fragile and more constrained by global conditions than the first.

The Pink Tide demonstrates several things. Democratic-left governments can substantially reduce inequality through redistributive policy, but that redistribution depends on resource revenues tied to global commodity cycles, so without restructuring the underlying economic model away from commodity dependence, the gains are vulnerable to global downturns. The political institutions of Latin American democracies — narrowly contested elections, polarized civil societies, often-fragmented party systems — make sustained reform difficult.

## What Latin America teaches

**The U.S. matters.** From the 1954 CIA-organized coup against Jacobo Árbenz in Guatemala (overthrown for nationalizing United Fruit Company land), through Allende, through the contra wars of the 1980s, through ongoing tensions with Cuba and Venezuela, U.S. political and military intervention has been a structural constraint on Latin American political economy. Any honest analysis of the region's twentieth century has to integrate this fact.

**Resource nationalism is a real strategy with real limits.** Countries with valuable natural resources can use those resources to fund redistributive programs that genuinely improve mass welfare. They cannot easily escape the underlying dependence on commodity prices set in other people's markets.

**Democratic socialism has a hard road in capitalist conditions.** Allende's failure, Lula's troubles, Maduro's collapse — all illustrate the difficulty of building socialism inside the international capitalist system, with hostile great powers, financialized capital flight, and the political polarization that radical redistribution provokes. Whether the road is impossible or merely hard is, again, an open question.

## Figures to know

- **Salvador Allende** (1908–1973) — president of Chile 1970–73; died in the coup
- **Augusto Pinochet** (1915–2006) — Chilean dictator 1973–90; presided over the neoliberal transformation of the Chilean economy
- **Fidel Castro** (1926–2016) — Cuban revolutionary; head of state 1959–2008
- **Che Guevara** (1928–1967) — Argentine revolutionary; central figure in the Cuban Revolution; killed in Bolivia
- **Hugo Chávez** (1954–2013) — president of Venezuela 1999–2013; central figure of the first Pink Tide
- **Lula** (Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, b. 1945) — Brazilian Workers' Party leader; president 2003–10 and 2023–
- **Evo Morales** (b. 1959) — first Indigenous president of Bolivia; 2006–19
- **Eduardo Galeano** (1940–2015) — Uruguayan writer; *The Open Veins of Latin America* (1971), the foundational text of Latin American dependency theory
- **Andre Gunder Frank** (1929–2005) — German-American economist; *Capitalism and Underdevelopment in Latin America* (1967); dependency theory
- **Raúl Prebisch** (1901–1986) — Argentine economist; UN Economic Commission for Latin America; the *Prebisch-Singer* thesis on declining terms of trade for primary commodity exporters
