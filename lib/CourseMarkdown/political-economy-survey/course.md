This is a long study. Not a podcast summary, not a Twitter explainer — a forty-hour walk through the entire arc of how human beings have organized economies at scale, from the first agrarian empires to the platforms that now mediate most of your waking hours.

The course is built for someone starting from zero. No background in economics or political theory is assumed. By the end you will read Adam Smith, Karl Marx, John Maynard Keynes, Friedrich Hayek, and Milton Friedman without footnotes — and, more importantly, you will know what they were arguing against, what they got right, what they got catastrophically wrong, and what the world did with their ideas.

The stance is neutral and comparative. Capitalism, socialism, feudalism, communism, mercantilism, and neoliberalism each get the same treatment: a clear-eyed look at how they functioned, who they served, how they were defended by their proponents, and how they collapsed or transformed. You do not need to arrive with a political position. You only need to be willing to look.

The course is organized into seven units of roughly equal weight. Each module is between thirty minutes and ninety minutes of work, mixing reading with short video lessons recorded for the WithDepth channel, reflection prompts, and end-of-unit quizzes. Two essays and a capstone project run alongside the modules.

What you will not find here: a single hot take, a definitive verdict, or a person trying to convert you. What you will find: the receipts, the citations, and enough vocabulary to read the news for the rest of your life and know what is actually being discussed.

This is the work — the looking, not the complaining.
