The communist regimes that emerged after 1917 absorbed most of the rhetorical attention paid to socialism in the twentieth century. But socialism had another path — slower, more piecemeal, less ambitious, more successful by most measures. It is the tradition of social democracy: democratic-electoral parties of the working class winning power in advanced industrial economies and using it to build comprehensive welfare states, strong labor protections, mixed-economy industrial coordination, and substantial redistribution, while leaving private property in the means of production largely intact.

By any measure that matters — population health, life expectancy, child welfare, equality, civic trust, sustained democratic stability — the social democratic countries are among the most successful societies in human history. This module covers the model, with focus on Sweden, Germany, and (as a more radical variant) Yugoslav self-management.

## The Swedish model

Sweden in 1900 was a poor agrarian country on the European periphery, with one of the highest emigration rates in Europe — roughly a quarter of its population had left for America between 1850 and 1930. By 2020, it was consistently ranked among the wealthiest, longest-lived, and most egalitarian societies on earth.

The political shift that produced this transformation began in 1932, when the *Sveriges socialdemokratiska arbetareparti* (Swedish Social Democratic Party, or SAP) won government and proceeded to hold it, almost continuously, for the next forty-four years.

The Swedish model rested on a particular institutional bargain known as the *Saltsjöbaden Agreement* of 1938. The major employer federation (SAF) and the major trade union confederation (LO) negotiated directly with each other over wages, working conditions, and industrial restructuring, with the state acting as backstop rather than primary regulator. This *Swedish corporatism* — peak-level bargaining between organized capital and organized labor — produced predictable, low-conflict industrial relations for decades.

The post-war Swedish model added several layers:

**Universalist welfare state.** Family benefits, healthcare, education, pensions, and unemployment insurance available to all citizens regardless of income — not means-tested anti-poverty programs but universal entitlements. The political logic was deliberate: middle-class taxpayers who receive benefits will defend the system, while means-tested programs eventually become politically vulnerable.

**Active labor market policy.** The state spent (and still spends) substantially on retraining, relocation assistance, and direct employment for workers displaced by economic change. The bargain was: workers accept industrial restructuring because the state catches them when they fall.

**Compressed wage structure.** The *Rehn-Meidner model*, developed by trade union economists Gösta Rehn and Rudolf Meidner in the late 1940s, deliberately compressed wages across firms. Profitable firms paid the same union scale as marginally profitable firms; the latter were squeezed out and their workers retrained. The model accelerated structural change while maintaining low income inequality.

**Public ownership of strategic infrastructure.** Banking was substantially state-influenced; transport, energy, and parts of heavy industry were publicly owned. Most consumer industries and most manufacturing remained in private hands.

The Swedish model began to come under pressure from the 1970s onward. The oil crises, slowing global growth, and rising public-sector budgets strained the system. The Meidner Plan of 1976 — a proposal to gradually transfer corporate ownership to union-controlled wage-earner funds — would have shifted Sweden toward democratic socialism in the medium term. It was politically watered down and never substantially implemented; the SAP lost the 1976 election partly over the controversy. By the 1990s, Sweden had retreated somewhat from the most ambitious model — partial privatization of pensions, school vouchers, somewhat looser labor markets — though it remains, by international standards, a very strong social democracy.

## The German model

Germany after 1945 produced a different variety of social democracy known as *the social market economy* (*soziale Marktwirtschaft*). The model was developed by *Ordoliberal* economists at the University of Freiburg — Walter Eucken, Wilhelm Röpke, Alexander Rüstow — and implemented under Ludwig Erhard, Konrad Adenauer's economics minister and later chancellor.

Ordoliberalism was distinct from both Keynesian social democracy and from Hayekian neoliberalism. Its central claim: markets work, but only inside a strong institutional framework that prevents monopoly, maintains stable money, and provides for those the market would otherwise destroy. The state's job is to create and maintain the rules of the game, not to engage in detailed economic intervention.

The German model added:

**Mitbestimmung (co-determination).** German law requires that large corporations have worker representatives on their supervisory boards. In firms with more than 2,000 employees, half the supervisory board seats go to workers (with management retaining tie-breaking authority through the chairman). Workers also elect *works councils* with rights to information, consultation, and in some cases co-determination on operational decisions. This is the most ambitious system of formal worker participation in any G7 country.

**Strong labor union role in vocational training.** The German *dual system* combines work-based apprenticeships with formal classroom training, run cooperatively by employer associations, unions, and the state. Roughly half of German young people enter the labor market through apprenticeships rather than university. The system produces some of the world's most skilled industrial workforces.

**Comprehensive social insurance.** Health, pensions, unemployment, accident, and (from 1994) long-term care insurance, financed primarily through employer-employee contributions managed by tripartite bodies including state, employers, and unions.

**Concerted regional development.** A federal system that systematically redistributes resources between rich and poor states (*Länderfinanzausgleich*), preventing the regional polarization that has affected, for example, the United States and the United Kingdom.

Germany sustained this model through reunification in 1990 — at substantial cost — and through the eurozone crisis of 2010–12. The 2003–05 *Hartz reforms* under Chancellor Gerhard Schröder loosened the labor market somewhat and have been credited (and blamed) for Germany's strong export performance in the 2010s. Wage stagnation among lower-income German workers since the Hartz reforms is one of the defining features of contemporary German political economy and has fed both the far right (AfD) and the left (Die Linke).

## Yugoslav self-management — the road not taken

Among twentieth-century socialist experiments, Yugoslavia under Josip Broz Tito (1892–1980) deserves more attention than it usually gets, because it tried something genuinely different from both Soviet planning and Western social democracy.

Yugoslavia had broken with the Soviet Union in 1948 — Tito refused to accept Stalin's authority — and was expelled from the Comintern. Forced to develop its own model, the Yugoslav party under the theorist Edvard Kardelj developed *workers' self-management*: state-owned enterprises managed not by central planners and not by Communist Party appointees but by elected workers' councils inside each firm.

The Yugoslav system worked like this:

- All firms above a certain size were socially owned (not state-owned in the Soviet sense, but owned collectively by their workforces, who could neither sell nor inherit their stake)
- Each firm elected a workers' council from among its employees
- The workers' council appointed and could dismiss the firm's management
- The council made major decisions about investment, product mix, wages, and distribution of profit
- Firms competed with each other in domestic and international markets
- Banking, foreign trade, and a small number of strategic sectors remained state-controlled
- Investment was partially planned but largely the result of decentralized firm-level decisions

This was the most serious attempt at *market socialism* ever conducted at national scale. The economic results were mixed. Yugoslavia grew rapidly through the 1950s and 1960s — average annual growth of over 6 percent — and built a substantial industrial base. But the system produced characteristic dysfunctions: firms tended to favor existing workers over hiring new ones (since adding workers diluted the existing share of profits), interregional inequality between richer northern and poorer southern republics grew, and the system became increasingly dependent on Western foreign borrowing in the 1970s.

The Yugoslav economy entered serious crisis in the late 1970s, and when Tito died in 1980, the system began to fragment along national lines without his unifying authority. By the late 1980s, Yugoslavia was in economic and political crisis, and from 1991 it disintegrated catastrophically into the Yugoslav Wars — among the bloodiest European conflicts since 1945.

The collapse of Yugoslavia is sometimes used to dismiss self-management as a model. This is too quick. Yugoslavia held together for over forty years, demonstrated that workers' self-management could function as the basis of a national economy, and achieved substantial development. Its collapse was driven primarily by national-political dynamics — the unraveling of the multinational federation Tito had held together — not primarily by the failure of self-management as an economic mechanism. The lesson is that political institutions matter as much as economic ones.

Self-management as a model survives in pieces. The Mondragón cooperative federation in the Basque Country, founded in 1956, is the largest and most successful workers'-cooperative complex in the world, employing over 80,000 people in industry, retail, finance, and education. The Argentine *empresas recuperadas* — factories occupied and run by their workers after their owners closed them during the 2001 crisis — number several hundred firms employing thousands of workers. The contemporary Italian *Marcora Law* allows workers to use their unemployment benefits to buy out failing companies, producing a continuing flow of new worker cooperatives. The self-management tradition is not dead — patchy and unfinished, but present.

## What social democracy teaches

**It works.** By any measure that political economy can apply — population health, longevity, equality, mobility, civic trust, democratic stability — the social-democratic countries of northern Europe consistently rank near the top of every global index. This is not propaganda. It is what the data show. The model has produced the best collective outcomes of any large-scale political-economic system in modern history.

**It depends on conditions that may not be transferable.** Social democracy emerged in small-to-medium-sized, relatively homogeneous, industrially advanced countries with strong labor movements and pre-existing traditions of cross-class compromise. Whether the model can be reproduced in larger, more diverse countries with weaker labor movements — like the United States — is one of the central political questions of our time. The American failure to build a comparable system, despite many attempts since the New Deal, is one of the most important comparative facts in modern political economy.

**It is under sustained pressure.** Since roughly 1980, social-democratic countries have faced the same neoliberal pressures as everyone else — capital mobility, tax competition, financialization, deregulation, declining union density. They have done better than other rich countries at resisting these pressures, but they have not been immune. The Swedish model of 2026 is not the Swedish model of 1976. Whether the social-democratic equilibrium is sustainable in a globalized, financialized world is, again, an open question.

## Other figures and movements worth knowing

A few names beyond the major ones:

**Tage Erlander** (1901–1985) — SAP prime minister of Sweden 1946–1969; the architect of the mature welfare state
**Olof Palme** (1927–1986) — SAP prime minister 1969–76 and 1982–86; pushed the Swedish model leftward; assassinated on a Stockholm street in 1986 in an unsolved murder
**Rudolf Meidner** (1914–2005) — Austrian-Swedish economist; co-author of the Rehn-Meidner model and the Meidner Plan
**Willy Brandt** (1913–1992) — SPD chancellor of West Germany 1969–1974; *Ostpolitik* (engagement with East Germany); pushed for codetermination expansion
**Edvard Kardelj** (1910–1979) — Yugoslav theorist; principal architect of self-management
**José María Arizmendiarrieta** (1915–1976) — Basque priest; founder of the Mondragón cooperatives in 1956
**Walter Eucken** (1891–1950) — Freiburg School Ordoliberal; intellectual architect of the German social market economy
