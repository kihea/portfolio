Karl Marx (1818–1883) was a German philosopher, journalist, historian, and economist who spent most of his adult life in exile in London. He produced one of the most influential bodies of work in human intellectual history. He was also disorganized, chronically broke, frequently bedridden with liver disease and skin disorders, and often supported financially by his friend Friedrich Engels, the textile-factory heir who became his lifelong collaborator.

Marx's work is often presented as a single political program, but it was developed over forty years, in stages, and the early work is recognizably different from the late work. This module covers the *young Marx* — the writings of the late 1830s and the 1840s — and the philosophical inheritance from Hegel that shaped everything he later did.

## Hegel: the inescapable background

You cannot read Marx without some understanding of Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831). Marx was trained as a Hegelian, broke with Hegel, and spent the rest of his life rewriting Hegel into materialist terms. The break was real; the inheritance was permanent.

Hegel's central idea, in the *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807) and the *Science of Logic* (1812–1816), is that history is the process by which *Geist* — Spirit, or roughly, collective human consciousness — comes to know itself. Reality is not a static thing but a dynamic process. Concepts develop through internal contradiction: an idea (the *thesis*) generates its opposite (the *antithesis*), and the conflict between them produces a higher synthesis that contains and supersedes both. This is the *dialectic*.

Hegel's master-slave dialectic — from the *Phenomenology* — is the passage Marx would later transform. Two self-consciousnesses meet. Each demands recognition from the other. They fight. One submits rather than die. The victor becomes the master; the defeated becomes the slave. But the master, in becoming dependent on the slave's labor, ends up less free than the slave, who in working on the world develops self-consciousness through making. The slave, through labor, becomes a self; the master, through enjoyment, atrophies. The dialectic produces an inversion of the apparent power relation.

This is the structure Marx will carry into his analysis of capitalism. The worker, through labor, makes the world that the capitalist, through ownership, consumes. The apparent relation — capitalist as agent, worker as instrument — masks an underlying truth that the dialectic will eventually reveal.

## The Young Hegelians and Feuerbach

In the 1830s and 1840s, a group of radical German intellectuals — Bruno Bauer, David Strauss, Max Stirner, Ludwig Feuerbach, and Marx himself — applied Hegel's dialectical method to religion and politics. Christianity, Feuerbach argued in *The Essence of Christianity* (1841), was a *projection*: human beings took their own best qualities — love, creativity, justice — and projected them onto an imaginary God, then bowed down to the projection. The cure was atheist humanism: to *take back* the qualities humanity had alienated to the divine.

Feuerbach's structure — that human beings create a thing and then experience it as if it were an alien power ruling over them — became the template for Marx's analysis of capital. Where Feuerbach said *God is the alienated essence of humanity*, Marx would say *capital is the alienated product of human labor*. The structure of the argument is the same; the content is different.

Marx broke with Feuerbach by 1845. The famous *Theses on Feuerbach* — eleven short notebook entries Marx wrote that year, published only after his death by Engels — concluded with the line that became Marx's most quoted sentence:

> "The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it."

Where the young Hegelians had been content to criticize religion, Marx would extend the critique to the actual material conditions that produced it — and then act to change them.

## The 1844 Manuscripts: alienation

The *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844* — sometimes called the *Paris Manuscripts* — were notebooks Marx wrote during his year in Paris in 1844. They were not published in his lifetime. They were rediscovered in the Soviet archives in 1932 and published in German that year, in English in 1959. The discovery transformed twentieth-century Marx scholarship, because the manuscripts present a *humanist* Marx focused on the experiential degradation of labor under capitalism — a Marx very different from the systematic theorist of *Capital*.

The central concept is *alienation* (German *Entfremdung*). Marx identifies four ways in which labor under capitalism alienates the worker:

**From the product of labor.** The worker produces things that do not belong to him. The shoes he makes belong to the factory owner. The more he produces, the more the owner accumulates, the poorer the worker becomes relatively. The worker faces his own product as a hostile, separate thing.

**From the activity of labor.** Work itself becomes external — something done not because the worker wills it but because he must do it to live. "His labor is therefore not voluntary but forced; it is forced labor. It is not the satisfaction of a need but a mere means to satisfy needs outside itself." The worker, Marx writes, is at home only when he is not working — eating, sleeping, drinking, procreating. When he works, he is something else.

**From species-being.** Marx's term for what is distinctively human about humanity — the capacity to consciously transform the world through labor. Animals do not produce; they instinctively act on their environment. Humans, in producing freely and intentionally, realize their distinctive nature. Capitalism reduces human labor to animal labor — repetitive, instrumental, not freely chosen. The worker is therefore alienated from his own humanity.

**From other workers.** Capitalist competition pits worker against worker. Solidarity is undercut. Each worker confronts the others as rivals for scarce wages. Human relations are reorganized around the cash nexus.

Notice that none of this is about poverty. Marx is not arguing that workers under capitalism are *poor* (though many are). He is arguing that something *qualitatively wrong* happens to human life when it is reorganized around the wage relation, even when the wage rises. The cure for alienation, in the young Marx, is not higher wages. It is the abolition of the conditions that produce alienation — the abolition of private property in the means of production.

This is the most enduring critique of capitalism Marx ever produced. You can dispute the economics of the later work. You can dispute the historical predictions. But the question — *what does the wage relation do to a person's relationship to their own life?* — has not gone away. Every subsequent critic of work, from Bertrand Russell to David Graeber, has been working within Marx's frame.

## Materialism

By 1845, Marx had begun to develop the framework he would later call *historical materialism*. The basic claim: human societies should be understood not by their ideas, but by their *modes of production* — the technologies and social relations by which they secure their material existence. Ideas — religious, philosophical, legal — emerge from material conditions, not the other way around. Hegel had said spirit produces history; Marx said history produces spirit.

This is the move Marx and Engels made in *The German Ideology* (1845–6, unpublished in their lifetime). The book is partly a polemic against the Young Hegelians and partly the first systematic statement of historical materialism. The famous summary:

> "In direct contrast to German philosophy, which descends from heaven to earth, here it is a matter of ascending from earth to heaven. ... Consciousness does not determine life, but life determines consciousness."

This is Marx's *inversion* of Hegel. Hegel had treated material reality as a manifestation of underlying Spirit. Marx treats Spirit as a manifestation of underlying material relations. The dialectic stays; what it is the dialectic *of* changes from ideas to social production.

## What the young Marx looks like

To bring this together, here is the young Marx in 1845, age twenty-seven:

- Trained in Hegel; broken with the Young Hegelians
- Convinced that philosophy must become political action
- Convinced that capitalism *qualitatively* degrades human life through alienation, not just quantitatively through poverty
- Convinced that the dynamics of history are driven by material conditions of production, not by ideas
- Convinced that the proletariat — the new industrial working class — will eventually overthrow the capitalist order and produce a non-alienated, classless society
- About to begin a forty-year project of working out, in detail, how all of this would happen

He has not yet written the *Manifesto*. He has not yet read most of the British economists in their original languages. He has not yet sat in the British Museum Reading Room for the decades it will take to write *Capital*. But the framework is in place.

## Why the young Marx still matters

Twentieth-century Marxism split into two camps about the relationship between the young and the mature Marx. The *humanist Marxists* — Erich Fromm, Herbert Marcuse, the early Frankfurt School, the young Sartre — emphasized the 1844 *Manuscripts* and saw alienation as the central category. The *structuralist Marxists* — most prominently Louis Althusser in *For Marx* (1965) — argued that there was an *epistemological break* between the young and old Marx and that the mature Marx of *Capital* abandoned the humanist framework in favor of a structural science of capitalism.

The argument has not been settled. But for someone learning Marx for the first time, the young Marx is more accessible, more emotionally legible, and more obviously connected to the things that bother people about modern work. It is a useful entry point. The mature Marx is the harder and more rewarding study, and the next several modules are about him.

## Figures to know

- **G. W. F. Hegel** (1770–1831) — the master-slave dialectic; the philosophical inheritance Marx never escaped.
- **Ludwig Feuerbach** (1804–1872) — *The Essence of Christianity*; the structure of "projection" that Marx applied to capital.
- **Bruno Bauer** (1809–1882) — Young Hegelian; Marx's collaborator and then target in *The Holy Family* (1844) and *The German Ideology*.
- **Max Stirner** (1806–1856) — anarchist Young Hegelian; *The Ego and Its Own* (1844); attacked by Marx in *The German Ideology*.
- **Friedrich Engels** (1820–1895) — Marx's lifelong collaborator; *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845), the empirical study that shaped Marx's understanding of industrial capitalism.

## Reflection prompt

Marx's concept of alienation describes a qualitative wrongness of wage labor that does not disappear when wages rise. Pick a job you have had (or that someone close to you has had) and identify whether and how the four forms of alienation Marx describes — from product, activity, species-being, and others — are present in that job. Be honest about both directions: where alienation is real, and where Marx's framework overshoots.
