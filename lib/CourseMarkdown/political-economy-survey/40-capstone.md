You have completed thirty-nine modules. The work that remains is the capstone — your own piece of political-economic analysis, applying the framework you have spent forty hours building to a phenomenon you choose. The deliverable is 4,000 words of written work, or a twenty-minute video essay with full transcript.

## The capstone assignment

**Prompt.**
Choose a contemporary economic phenomenon. The choice is yours, but it must be large enough to admit serious analysis — not "my coffee order" but something like:

- The U.S. housing crisis since 2008
- Gig work and the contested status of platform workers
- The Chinese state's relationship to its largest firms
- AI's effect on white-collar labor
- The climate transition and its industrial-policy demands
- Healthcare financing in a country of your choice
- The European energy crisis since 2022
- The American opioid epidemic as a political-economic phenomenon
- The crisis of higher education debt
- The collapse of local journalism
- Cryptocurrency and its political-economic significance
- Any other phenomenon large enough to argue about

Apply the full toolkit from this course to read it. The deliverable must do three things, each of which will be explicitly evaluated in the rubric.

**One: name your inherited categories.** Identify the analytical frame you are using. Are you working in the Smithian tradition? Marxian? Polanyian? Keynesian? Hayekian? Some hybrid? Name it. Acknowledge what the frame is good at seeing and what it tends to miss. This is the most important move of the assignment.

**Two: describe the actual mechanism, without ideology.** Tell the reader what is happening: who works, who owns, who decides, who eats — the mechanism, the actual flow of value, the legal arrangements, the institutional infrastructure, the history of how it came to be this way. This section should be as descriptive as a piece of journalism and as careful as a research paper. Cite. Use primary sources where possible. Avoid the use of slogans on either side.

**Three: name what each major tradition in this course would say about it.** A serious political-economic analysis does not pretend to be the only possible reading. Present what the major traditions you have studied would say:
- The classical liberal reading (Smith, Mill, Friedman)
- The Marxian reading (alienation, surplus value, commodity fetishism)
- The institutional/Polanyian reading (fictitious commodities, the embedded economy)
- The Keynesian reading (effective demand, expectations, regulation)
- Any other framework specifically relevant to your phenomenon

Then state your own view. Defend it. Acknowledge what could prove you wrong. Name the alternatives you considered and rejected.

**Requirements:**

- 4,000 words of polished prose, or a twenty-minute video essay with full transcript
- At least five primary sources (texts by people from the period or institutions involved, government documents, corporate filings, contemporary news, etc.)
- At least five secondary sources (recent scholarly or journalistic analyses)
- Citations in a consistent format
- No slogans as substitutes for analysis
- No appeal to a thinker's authority without engaging with the actual argument
- Honest about its own position

The rubric awards points for clarity of mechanism description, plurality of perspective, intellectual honesty, source quality, and depth of analysis. It does not award points for political conclusions in any direction. A capstone that ends "and therefore capitalism must be abolished" is graded the same way as one that ends "and therefore the market must be left alone." Both are political conclusions, both admissible if defended carefully, and both penalized if they substitute for analysis.

## A recommended process

The capstone is hard, and you will need most of a month to do it well. Here is a recommended sequence.

**Week 1: Phenomenon and frame.** Choose your phenomenon. Read three or four serious recent treatments of it from different political perspectives. Identify which analytical frame is most natural for you, which is most natural for the phenomenon, and which you most want to argue with. Begin writing your "named frame" section.

**Week 2: Mechanism and history.** Reconstruct what is actually happening, with citations. Find the primary sources. Read corporate filings if relevant. Read government reports. Read the journalists who have done the slow, careful, on-the-ground work of documenting your phenomenon. The reading list in `/assessments/reading-list.md` includes journalistic and policy sources for many likely topics.

**Week 3: Plural perspectives.** Write the section that presents what each major tradition would say. Be fair to each. Read at least one work by someone who would defend the position you most disagree with. The strongest capstones are written by students who have understood the opposing position well enough to state it in terms its defenders would accept.

**Week 4: Your view, drafting, revision.** Write your own view. Then go back and revise everything. The first draft is for figuring out what you think. The second draft is for saying it well. A capstone that has been written and then put away for a week and reread before submission is qualitatively better than one finished on the deadline.

## A note on what the capstone is for

This is the last assignment of the course. It exists for a particular reason, and the reason is worth stating directly.

Everything you have read in the past thirty-nine modules was written by someone who had to do exactly what the capstone asks you to do. They had to choose a phenomenon, identify their analytical frame, describe the mechanism, engage with rival traditions, and stake out their own position. Smith did this with eighteenth-century commercial society. Marx did it with industrial capitalism. Polanyi did it with the construction of the market economy. Hayek did it with the post-war welfare state. Mazzucato is doing it now with platform capitalism and the entrepreneurial state. Piketty is doing it with twenty-first-century inequality.

The capstone is your turn — not a graduation exercise but the activity the figures in this course were doing throughout their careers. Doing it once, well, gives you a clearer sense of what they were doing — and a clearer sense of what you yourself believe and how to defend it.

You will not produce the next *Wealth of Nations*, nor is that the point. The point is that, by writing your own piece of careful political-economic analysis, you stop being a reader of other people's work and become a participant in a conversation that has been going on for nearly three hundred years.

## Final thoughts

If you have finished forty hours of work on political economy, you are now in a tiny minority of educated adults anywhere. Most people will spend their entire lives without ever sitting down with the actual arguments about how their economic world is organized. They will form opinions from soundbites, slogans, and partisan media. You have done something else.

What you do with what you have learned is your choice. Some readers will use it to participate more effectively in the politics of their country. Some will use it to think more carefully about the institutions they work inside. Some will use it to make better individual financial and life decisions. Some will use it to advocate for changes they now believe are necessary. All of these are legitimate uses.

What none of you should do is decide that, having learned the framework, you no longer need to keep learning. Political economy is not a doctrine to be memorized but a discipline to be practiced. The world keeps changing as the systems you live inside keep mutating, and the thinking required to stay literate about them is continuous.

The course closes here; the work does not.

Submit your capstone. Then take a long walk and decide what you want to do next.
