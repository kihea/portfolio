The *Communist Manifesto* was published in London in February 1848. It is the most read political pamphlet in history. It is also, in important ways, atypical of Marx's work — short, urgent, polemical, written for a specific audience and a specific political moment. This module reads it on its own terms.

## The commission

The Communist League was a small organization of German radical workers — perhaps a few hundred members, mostly in London, Paris, and Brussels. They had emerged out of the earlier League of the Just, an organization with religious-socialist roots. Marx and Engels joined in 1847.

The League's second congress, in late 1847, commissioned Marx and Engels to write a programmatic statement of the League's principles. Engels produced a draft in catechism form (question-and-answer) called the *Principles of Communism*. Marx then rewrote the document into a single sustained polemic. He sent the final draft to London in late January 1848. It was published in German in February.

Three weeks later, the February Revolution broke out in Paris. King Louis-Philippe abdicated. The Second Republic was declared. Revolutionary movements spread across Europe — Berlin, Vienna, Milan, Budapest, Prague, Rome. The *Manifesto* arrived in the middle of what looked like the European revolution Marx had been predicting. The timing was either luck or prescience or, more likely, both.

The 1848 revolutions failed, and by 1850 the old regimes were back in power across most of Europe; the *Manifesto* sold modestly for several decades. It became famous only after the rise of European social democracy in the 1880s and the publication of the seventh German edition in 1888 with Engels's important preface. Today it is in print in more languages than almost any book except the Bible.

## The structure

The *Manifesto* has four sections.

**Section I — Bourgeois and Proletarians.** Marx's compressed history of capitalism. The bourgeoisie rose by displacing feudal property relations; in doing so it created a revolutionary new mode of production that constantly transforms the technologies, geographies, and social relations of every society it touches. The famous passage:

> "The bourgeoisie cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production, and thereby the relations of production, and with them the whole relations of society. ... All fixed, fast-frozen relations, with their train of ancient and venerable prejudices and opinions, are swept away, all new-formed ones become antiquated before they can ossify. All that is solid melts into air, all that is holy is profaned."

The bourgeoisie, in this account, is not a conservative force. It is the most revolutionary class in history. It has created world markets, mechanized production, urbanized populations, dissolved traditional ways of life, and produced — as its own product — the proletariat: the modern industrial working class without property or customary protections. And the proletariat, Marx argues, will be the bourgeoisie's gravediggers.

**Section II — Proletarians and Communists.** What communists want. Marx distinguishes communists from other socialists. The communist platform is the proletariat's general interest, not a sectional doctrine. The most famous practical demands from this section:

> "1. Abolition of property in land and application of all rents of land to public purposes.
> 2. A heavy progressive or graduated income tax.
> 3. Abolition of all rights of inheritance.
> 4. Confiscation of the property of all emigrants and rebels.
> 5. Centralisation of credit in the hands of the State, by means of a national bank with State capital and an exclusive monopoly.
> 6. Centralisation of the means of communication and transport in the hands of the State.
> 7. Extension of factories and instruments of production owned by the State; the bringing into cultivation of waste-lands, and the improvement of the soil generally in accordance with a common plan.
> 8. Equal liability of all to labor. Establishment of industrial armies, especially for agriculture.
> 9. Combination of agriculture with manufacturing industries; gradual abolition of the distinction between town and country, by a more equable distribution of the population over the country.
> 10. Free education for all children in public schools. Abolition of children's factory labor in its present form. Combination of education with industrial production, etc., etc."

Notice that several of these — progressive income tax, public education, state ownership of communications and transport, abolition of child factory labor — became standard policy across capitalist liberal democracies within a century. The *Manifesto*'s ten-point program is not, in retrospect, as radical as the language around it suggests. Several of its demands are part of the everyday institutional landscape of advanced economies.

**Section III — Socialist and Communist Literature.** A taxonomy of rival socialisms. Marx distinguishes:

- *Feudal socialism* — backward-looking critiques of capitalism by aristocrats and reactionaries who wanted to restore pre-capitalist hierarchies.
- *Petty-bourgeois socialism* — the critique offered by small shopkeepers and artisans threatened by industrial capitalism; nostalgic for the guild order.
- *German "true" socialism* — sentimental philosophical socialism without political analysis; a target Marx had already attacked in *The German Ideology*.
- *Conservative or bourgeois socialism* — reformers who wanted to ease capitalism's worst effects without challenging its basic structure (Marx names Proudhon in this category).
- *Critical-utopian socialism and communism* — Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen; respected by Marx for their early diagnoses but criticized for offering blueprints rather than analyzing historical conditions.

We will return to the utopian socialists in Unit 4.

**Section IV — Position of the Communists in Relation to the Various Existing Opposition Parties.** Brief tactical conclusions. Communists support every revolutionary movement against the existing order. They never lose sight of the property question. The pamphlet ends with the famous lines:

> "The Communists disdain to conceal their views and aims. They openly declare that their ends can be attained only by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions. Let the ruling classes tremble at a Communistic revolution. The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win.
> Workingmen of all countries, unite!"

## What the *Manifesto* is doing

It is doing several things at once. It is:

- A historical analysis of how capitalism emerged and what its dynamics are
- A political program for the Communist League
- A polemic against rival socialisms
- A statement of internationalist solidarity
- A piece of rhetoric, with the sustained urgency of an argument written for people on the edge of action

The rhetorical mode matters. The *Manifesto* is *agitational*. It is written to be read by workers and to give them a framework for understanding what was happening to them and what to do about it. It is not a treatise. The economics is compressed; the philosophy is implied; the politics is everywhere.

This is why the *Manifesto* has been so much more widely read than Marx's other work, and why it is so easy to misread. Marx is making claims that look like predictions but are also exhortations. He is saying *this is the trajectory of history* and also *help make it so*. The two registers — descriptive and prescriptive — are not separated.

## What the *Manifesto* got right and wrong

Right.

— The bourgeoisie did, in fact, prove to be a revolutionary force, restlessly reorganizing every traditional society it touched. *All that is solid melts into air* is one of the truest sentences ever written about modernity.

— Capitalism did, in fact, become a *world* system. By 1900 it was global. The *Manifesto*'s internationalism — written in a Europe of nation-states where most people had never traveled — anticipated this.

— Several of the *Manifesto*'s practical demands — public education, the progressive income tax, the abolition of child labor, state ownership of transport infrastructure — became standard policy in liberal democracies.

— The dynamic of capitalism producing its own opposition through the concentration of the proletariat was empirically correct for much of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Industrial workers did organize, did unionize, did produce mass political parties.

Wrong.

— The proletariat did not, in the advanced capitalist countries, become the gravedigger of capitalism. Mass industrial workforces in the United States and Western Europe by the mid-twentieth century became, on the whole, defenders of capitalist social democracy, not its revolutionary opponents.

— The communist revolutions of the twentieth century happened in countries that were *not* industrially advanced (Russia, China, Cuba) — exactly contrary to Marx's expectation that revolution would happen first where capitalism had developed furthest.

— The state Marx and Engels envisioned would "wither away" after the transition to communism, in fact, expanded enormously in every communist regime. The *Manifesto*'s tenth-point program included "extension of factories and instruments of production owned by the State" — and the state proved much harder to dissolve than the *Manifesto* assumed.

## What to take from the *Manifesto*

Three things.

**The dynamic.** Capitalism is not a stable order; it constantly transforms itself. The disruption people experience under it is not a malfunction; it is the system working as intended. *All that is solid melts into air* is the basic phenomenology of modernity.

**The internationalism.** Marx insisted that the proletariat's situation was structurally similar in every country and that solidarity across national boundaries was both possible and necessary. This was the foundational insight of every twentieth-century left movement that crossed borders.

**The framework of class analysis.** Whether you accept Marx's specific predictions, the practice of asking *what class interests are being served by this policy?* is a permanent contribution to political analysis. You do not have to be a Marxist to find it useful. You do have to be willing to ask the question.

## Figures to know

- **Karl Marx** (1818–1883)
- **Friedrich Engels** (1820–1895) — co-author; bibliographer; financial supporter
- **Communist League** — the small German workers' association that commissioned the *Manifesto*
- **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** (1809–1865) — French anarchist socialist; *What Is Property?* (1840) and *The Philosophy of Poverty* (1846); attacked by Marx in *The Poverty of Philosophy* (1847)
