By 1920, it was clear to careful Marxists that the working class in advanced capitalist countries was not going to spontaneously revolt. Germany — the country with the largest, best-organized, most Marxist-educated proletariat — had voted for war in 1914 and then, when revolutionary moments came in 1918–19, mostly chose social-democratic reform over communism. The Russian Revolution had happened, but in a country Marx would have classified as not yet ready. Why?

The generation that asked this question, between roughly 1920 and 1970, transformed Marxism from a primarily economic theory into a theory of culture, ideology, and consciousness. They are sometimes called the *Western Marxists* to distinguish them from the Soviet-aligned *Marxism-Leninism* developing in parallel. The Hungarian philosopher György Lukács and the Italian theorist Antonio Gramsci were the two foundational figures. The Frankfurt School in Germany was the institutional center.

This module covers them as a tradition. The reading is dense — take notes.

## Lukács and the problem of reification

György Lukács (1885–1971) was a Hungarian-Jewish philosopher who joined the Communist Party in 1918 and served briefly as Commissar for Culture in the short-lived Hungarian Soviet Republic of 1919. He fled to Vienna after the republic collapsed. His most influential book, *History and Class Consciousness* (1923), drew together a strain of philosophical Marxism that had been latent in Marx's *Capital* and that had been largely buried under Engels's positivist scientism.

Lukács's central concept was *reification* (German *Verdinglichung*) — the way that under capitalism, social relations between people come to appear as relations between things, and human qualities (creativity, time, attention) come to appear as quantitative properties of commodities. This is Marx's commodity fetishism extended into a general account of how consciousness itself is shaped by capitalist social relations.

The implication: it is not enough for the working class to be objectively oppressed. The working class also has to *recognize* itself as a class, to see through the reified appearances of capitalist life. This recognition — *class consciousness* — does not arise automatically from the conditions of exploitation. Workers can experience exploitation and still understand themselves through the categories the system provides — as individual job-seekers in a market, as consumers of personal lifestyles, as patriots of their nation. The task of revolutionary theory is to *produce* class consciousness by showing the reified appearances for what they are.

Lukács's book was promptly denounced by the Communist International for theoretical deviationism. Lukács spent the rest of his life officially recanting parts of it (and unofficially defending other parts). But its influence on subsequent Western Marxism was foundational. Every later theorist who wrote about how culture shapes consciousness — Gramsci, Adorno, Marcuse, Althusser, Stuart Hall — was working in territory Lukács opened up.

## Gramsci and hegemony

Antonio Gramsci (1891–1937) was an Italian communist, a founding member of the Italian Communist Party in 1921, and after 1924 its leader. He was arrested by Mussolini's regime in 1926 and spent the rest of his life in fascist prison. The prosecutor at his trial said: "We must stop this brain from functioning for twenty years." It did not work. Gramsci's *Prison Notebooks* — thousands of pages of philosophical, historical, and political reflection written in prison between 1929 and 1935 — were smuggled out and published after his death. They are the foundational text of twentieth-century cultural Marxism.

Gramsci's central concept is *hegemony* — the way a dominant class secures the consent of subordinate classes to its rule through cultural, educational, and ideological institutions, rather than through direct force. The bourgeoisie does not stay in power, Gramsci argues, primarily by sending in the police. It stays in power by making its worldview seem natural — by controlling schools, newspapers, churches, popular entertainment, the academy, the institutions through which ordinary people come to understand the world. When ordinary working people understand themselves as individuals competing in a meritocratic market, as patriotic citizens of a national community, as consumers exercising free choice — they are reproducing the hegemonic worldview of capitalism in their own self-understanding.

This is *not* a theory that workers are stupid or duped. Gramsci is clear that hegemonic ideas often *describe* real features of capitalist life. Markets do provide real freedoms; nations do offer real identities; consumer choice is, in part, real. Hegemony is not a lie. It is a partial truth that organizes consciousness in ways favorable to existing power.

The political implication: revolutionary politics cannot just be a battle for state power. It has to be a *war of position* — a long, patient struggle to build counter-hegemonic institutions (workers' education, alternative newspapers, autonomous cultural organizations) that can produce a different consciousness. Only after the *war of position* has been won can a *war of maneuver* — a direct contest for state power — succeed.

Gramsci's framework has shaped left politics for a century. Every contemporary discussion of "discourse," "narrative," "framing," "the culture war," and "common sense" descends, knowingly or not, from Gramsci. Right-wing strategists too — Andrew Breitbart's famous line that "politics is downstream of culture" — were borrowing Gramsci's structure without crediting him.

## The Frankfurt Institute

The Frankfurt Institut für Sozialforschung was founded in 1923 by Felix Weil, a wealthy young Marxist who used family money to set up an independent Marxist research institute affiliated with the University of Frankfurt. The Institute became a gathering place for a generation of German-Jewish Marxist intellectuals: Max Horkheimer, Theodor Adorno, Erich Fromm, Friedrich Pollock, Walter Benjamin, Herbert Marcuse, Leo Löwenthal, Franz Neumann.

When the Nazis took power in 1933, the Institute's members fled — first to Geneva, then to New York, where they were given affiliation with Columbia University. They spent the war years in American exile, watching from California the collapse of European civilization.

The collective project of the Frankfurt School was a synthesis of Marx, Freud, and Weber: a critical theory that could explain how advanced capitalist societies — especially the totalitarian ones that emerged in the 1930s and 1940s — produced subjects who consented to their own domination. Their key works:

**Max Horkheimer and Theodor Adorno, *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (1947).** A bleak masterpiece arguing that Enlightenment rationality, which was supposed to liberate humanity from superstition, instead produced a new kind of domination — the reduction of everything (nature, other people, the self) to instrumental objects of calculation and control. The Holocaust, in this analysis, was not a regression from Enlightenment civilization but its dark extension.

**Theodor Adorno, *Minima Moralia* (1951).** Aphoristic reflections on life in late capitalism, written in California during the war. "There is no right life within the wrong one." Adorno's view of mass culture — Hollywood movies, jazz, advertising — was that it had become an *industry* whose function was to produce passive, consenting consumer-subjects. He was famously contemptuous of jazz, a position later generations of critics have judged unfair.

**Herbert Marcuse, *One-Dimensional Man* (1964).** The most accessible Frankfurt School book and the one that found a mass readership during the 1960s. Marcuse argued that advanced industrial society had developed mechanisms — consumer abundance, cultural integration, technocratic management — that absorbed dissent and produced a population whose immediate interests had been so thoroughly aligned with the system that they could not imagine alternatives. "Liberation" required first liberating the imagination.

**Erich Fromm, *Escape from Freedom* (1941).** Fromm's psychoanalytic analysis of why so many people found freedom unbearable and willingly submitted to authoritarian movements like Nazism. The book reads astonishingly well from a 2026 vantage point.

**Walter Benjamin** (not, technically, a member of the Institute but closely associated) — *The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction* (1936). On how mass-reproduced art changes the structure of cultural experience. Benjamin's *Arcades Project* — a vast unfinished work on the Paris of the nineteenth century — is one of the great unfinished books of the twentieth century. Benjamin killed himself in 1940 at the Spanish border while fleeing the Nazis.

The Frankfurt School's mood was, on the whole, despairing. They had watched a sophisticated, educated, industrially advanced European society — Germany — produce Nazism. They had escaped to America and found a different but related phenomenon: mass commercial culture, suburbia, the integrated white-collar worker. Their question, throughout the 1940s and 1950s, was whether modern industrial society of any kind — Soviet or American — was compatible with anything that could be called human freedom.

## The 1960s reception

The Frankfurt School theorists were largely unknown in America until the mid-1960s, when Marcuse's *One-Dimensional Man* and *Eros and Civilization* (1955) became foundational texts of the New Left. Students at Columbia, Berkeley, and the Sorbonne were reading Marcuse the way an earlier generation had read Marx. The student movements of 1968 in France, Italy, Germany, the United States, and Mexico were partly produced by Frankfurt-influenced thinking, although the relationship was always more complicated than simple intellectual influence.

Adorno's relationship to the 1968 student movement was famously sour. He thought the students were practicing exactly the kind of unreflective political activism — confusing immediate action with revolutionary theory — that *Dialectic of Enlightenment* had warned against. A famous incident: in 1969, female students at the University of Frankfurt bared their breasts at Adorno during a lecture to protest his refusal to support occupations. He was deeply shaken and died a few months later. The story has been told many ways.

## The second generation: Habermas

Jürgen Habermas (b. 1929) — Adorno's student and assistant — became the most important Frankfurt-tradition theorist of the late twentieth century. Habermas moved away from the pure cultural pessimism of his teachers and toward a theory of *communicative rationality*: that genuine democratic legitimacy depends on the institutional conditions for undistorted communication among free and equal citizens. His major works — *The Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* (1962), *The Theory of Communicative Action* (1981), *Between Facts and Norms* (1992) — are the contemporary continuation of critical theory.

Habermas is not primarily a political economist; he is a philosopher of democracy and law. But his influence on contemporary debates about media, public discourse, and democratic legitimacy is enormous, and his lineage runs directly through the Frankfurt School to Marx.

## What the cultural turn accomplished

Three things to take from this module.

**Consciousness is produced.** What people understand about their own situation is not given to them by the situation itself. It is mediated through schools, media, religion, and the everyday practices of cultural life. To change politics, you have to change the institutions that produce consciousness, not just the institutions that produce goods. This is the central insight Lukács and Gramsci added to Marx.

**Domination can be soft.** A society that disciplines its members through television, consumer goods, mass culture, and the soft compulsion of needing to fit in can be as effectively coercive as one that uses police and prisons. The Frankfurt School pushed this analysis further than any earlier tradition. Whether they pushed it too far — whether they ended up writing off the political agency of ordinary people — is a fair question.

**The vocabulary still matters.** Hegemony, reification, the culture industry, the public sphere, communicative rationality, the war of position — these terms are in active circulation a hundred years after Lukács and Gramsci coined them. Whatever you decide about the program, you cannot read contemporary critical thought without them.
