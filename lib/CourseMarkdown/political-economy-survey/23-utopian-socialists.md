The term *utopian socialism* was coined, somewhat unkindly, by Marx and Engels. They contrasted *utopian* socialism — which they characterized as a description of an ideal society arrived at by moral reasoning — with their own *scientific* socialism, which claimed to derive the future society from the actual contradictions of the present one. The distinction was polemical and not entirely fair. The thinkers Marx and Engels grouped as utopians were doing more than dreaming. Several of them ran actual experiments. Some of those experiments left traces still visible in modern life.

## Saint-Simon and the rule of producers

Claude Henri de Rouvroy, Comte de Saint-Simon (1760–1825), was a French aristocrat who fought in the American Revolution as a young man, lost his fortune speculating during the French Revolution, and spent the last two decades of his life writing prolifically — though often eccentrically — about a future society organized around productive work.

Saint-Simon's central insight: the most consequential conflict in modern society was not between the rich and the poor in the abstract, but between the *productive classes* — workers, engineers, scientists, industrialists, farmers — and the *idle classes* — aristocrats, courtiers, rentiers, parasitic officials who lived off others' labor. In one famous illustration (the *Parable*), Saint-Simon imagined what would happen if France suddenly lost its three thousand leading scientists, engineers, manufacturers, doctors, and skilled workers. The country, he said, would be devastated. Now imagine, he continued, that France instead lost its leading princes, ministers, marshals, cardinals, and courtiers. Nothing important would change.

The political conclusion: society should be reorganized around the productive classes. Politics — the old contest of dynasties, parties, and ideologies — should be replaced by *administration*, the rational management of human and material resources by those competent to do so. Saint-Simon called this the "rule of the producers." His later followers, the Saint-Simonians, developed this into a technocratic-socialist program emphasizing planned investment, mass education, public works, and the abolition of inheritance.

The Saint-Simonians did not produce a successful political movement, but their ideas had enormous practical effects. The engineers and bankers who built nineteenth-century France's railways, canals, and industrial credit institutions — especially the Crédit Mobilier, founded by Émile and Isaac Pereire — were almost all former Saint-Simonians. The Suez Canal was built by Ferdinand de Lesseps, raised in the Saint-Simonian movement. The Panama Canal, the French rail network, the Bank of France's mid-century expansion: all of these were Saint-Simonian projects in everything but name.

The Saint-Simonian tradition is also the ancestor of twentieth-century technocratic governance — the idea that complex social problems can be solved by experts using rational planning. The Soviet five-year plans, the postwar French dirigiste planning system, the European Commission's regulatory technocracy: all have Saint-Simonian DNA. Whether you regard this lineage with admiration or alarm, it is one of the most consequential undercurrents in modern political economy.

## Robert Owen and the cooperative tradition

Robert Owen (1771–1858) was a self-made Welsh industrialist who became, by his late twenties, one of the largest cotton-mill owners in Britain. He bought the New Lanark mills near Glasgow in 1799 and proceeded to run them according to principles that contradicted everything in the industrial common sense of his time. He shortened working hours. He paid workers when the mills were closed for refits. He banned corporal punishment. He built schools for workers' children — including the first nursery school in Britain — and stocked them with toys and music instead of catechisms. He provided housing, communal kitchens, and free medical care.

The remarkable thing was that the mills made money. Productivity at New Lanark was higher than at competing mills running children for fourteen-hour shifts. By 1815, New Lanark had become a kind of pilgrimage site for European reformers, statesmen, and intellectuals. The Tsar of Russia visited. Princes and ministers visited. Owen briefly became one of the most famous men in Europe.

Owen drew the wrong conclusions from his success. He concluded that capitalism could be replaced peacefully by *communities of cooperation* — self-governing villages of 500 to 3,000 people, organized around shared property, mutual aid, and rational education. He wrote pamphlets, gave lectures, and finally — in 1825 — bought thirty thousand acres in Indiana to build an experimental community called *New Harmony*.

New Harmony failed within two years. The members fought constantly. The cooperative production failed. Owen lost a substantial part of his fortune. He returned to Britain humiliated.

But Owen's failure did not end the cooperative movement. In fact, it intensified it. In the 1830s and 1840s, working-class people in Britain — many influenced by Owen's ideas — founded their own cooperative experiments. The *Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers*, founded in 1844 in a Lancashire mill town, established a small cooperative store with twenty-eight members and a few pounds of capital. They adopted a set of principles — democratic governance (one member, one vote regardless of capital invested), open membership, distribution of surplus proportional to purchases, education for members — that became the international foundation for the cooperative movement.

The Rochdale Pioneers' store grew. Then other cooperatives followed. By 1900, there were thousands of consumer cooperatives in Britain alone. The cooperative movement spread to continental Europe, North America, India, and Japan. Today, the International Co-operative Alliance estimates that cooperative enterprises employ around 280 million people worldwide and generate over $2 trillion in annual turnover. Mondragón in the Basque Country, the largest worker cooperative federation in the world, employs over 80,000 people. American agricultural cooperatives like Land O'Lakes, Ocean Spray, and Sunkist are direct descendants of the Rochdale model.

Owen failed at the grand vision, though the pieces he left behind — the cooperative store, the credit union, the worker-owned firm — are alive a century and a half later. This is one of the more interesting patterns in political economy. Utopian dreams produce mundane institutions. The mundane institutions outlast the dreams.

## Charles Fourier and the structure of desire

Charles Fourier (1772–1837) is the strangest figure in this module and, in some ways, the most prophetic. Fourier was a French clerk who spent his life developing an elaborate scheme for a future society organized into self-sufficient communities of exactly 1,620 people, called *phalanxes*. Each phalanx would occupy a *phalanstery* — a vast palace-like building containing all the productive and residential functions of the community. People would work according to their *passions* — Fourier developed an elaborate psychology of human drives — and rotate among many short tasks rather than being chained to a single job.

Some of Fourier's specific predictions and prescriptions are eccentric to the point of comedy. He predicted that, when humanity organized itself correctly, the seas would turn to lemonade, dangerous animals would be replaced by *anti-lions* and *anti-sharks* friendly to humanity, and humans would grow long, prehensile tails ending in eyes. These passages, which fill many pages of his work, have not aged well.

But Fourier was also, on the things that mattered, ahead of his time in striking ways:

— He was one of the earliest thinkers to argue for full *gender equality*, including women's right to work in any profession and to choose their own sexual partners. The word *feminism* was coined by Fourier (in French, *féminisme*) in 1837.

— He argued that the criterion for evaluating any society was *how well it allows people to express their individual passions and develop their full natures* — a deeply un-Christian idea in 1810 that has since become a foundational liberal value.

— He critiqued the modern factory and modern marriage in parallel terms, as institutions that crushed human variety into mechanical roles.

— He recognized that desire and economy were intimately connected — that the way a society organizes work is the same as the way it organizes love, family, and pleasure. This insight would not enter mainstream Western thought again until Freud and then the New Left.

Fourier's followers did try to build phalansteries. In the United States, the most famous attempt was *Brook Farm* in West Roxbury, Massachusetts, founded in 1841 and home for a few years to Nathaniel Hawthorne, Margaret Fuller, and other Transcendentalists before its members reorganized it as a Fourierist phalanx in 1844. It burned down in 1846, and the movement faded. About two dozen American phalanxes were attempted; none succeeded for more than a few years.

Fourier's deeper influence was philosophical. Marx and Engels acknowledged him as a brilliant satirist of bourgeois society. The Surrealists rediscovered him in the 1920s. Roland Barthes wrote a remarkable book — *Sade, Fourier, Loyola* (1971) — celebrating Fourier as a great theorist of pleasure. Herbert Marcuse drew on Fourier in *Eros and Civilization* (1955). The 1968 student movements quoted him. Whenever modern thought reaches for an alternative to puritanism and instrumental rationality, Fourier shows up.

## Why Marx called them "utopian"

Marx and Engels's critique of Saint-Simon, Owen, and Fourier had two parts.

The first was *methodological*. The utopians, Marx argued, derived their future society from moral reasoning — from imagining what a better society would look like. They did not derive it from analyzing the contradictions of the present society and identifying the forces that would actually carry the transition forward. They were *idealists*, in the philosophical sense — they thought the world was changed by changing ideas. Marx, as a materialist, thought the world was changed by changing the underlying structure of production, and that the consciousness changes followed.

The second was *political*. The utopians, Marx argued, addressed their proposals to humanity in general, hoping to persuade everyone — rich and poor — to adopt the new vision. They did not see that society is divided into classes with conflicting interests, and that the interests of the dominant class will block any reform that threatens its position. The transition to socialism would not come through universal persuasion. It would come through class struggle — through the proletariat winning, against the resistance of capital, the political and economic power to remake society.

This is the meaning of *scientific socialism* in Marx's vocabulary. Not "scientific" in the natural-science sense. "Scientific" in the sense of being derived from an analysis of actual conditions rather than from moral imagination.

The critique was largely correct. The utopian experiments did fail at their grand goals. But the critique also underestimated something important. The utopians, in their attempts to build small-scale alternatives — cooperative stores, worker-owned mills, phalanxes — produced institutions that *worked* and that diffused widely. The Marxian critique of building socialism in advance has a strong theoretical case but a weak empirical one. The cooperative movement, the credit unions, the worker-owned firms, the experimental communities — all of these have outlived several attempts at revolutionary socialism. They are slow, partial, and incomplete. They are also still here.

## The pre-Marxian socialist tradition more broadly

A few more figures worth knowing, beyond the three Marx and Engels singled out as "utopian."

**Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** (1809–1865) — French anarchist socialist. *What Is Property?* (1840) contained the famous line *"Property is theft."* Proudhon advocated *mutualism* — a federation of self-managed worker associations exchanging through a "people's bank" of mutual credit. He was the first thinker to call himself an *anarchist*. Marx attacked him bitterly in *The Poverty of Philosophy* (1847), and the personal hostility between Marx and Proudhon shaped the long history of the socialist-anarchist split.

**Louis Blanc** (1811–1882) — French socialist; coined the formula "From each according to his abilities, to each according to his needs" (which Marx would later adopt). Briefly part of the 1848 revolutionary government in France, where he attempted to establish state-funded *national workshops* for the unemployed. The workshops were sabotaged and then abolished by the conservative response to the revolution.

**Flora Tristan** (1803–1844) — French socialist and feminist, one of the very first thinkers to call for the international organization of the working class. Her *Workers' Union* (1843) anticipated key themes of the *Communist Manifesto* that would appear five years later. Tristan is also one of the lesser-known but historically important figures the syllabus promised to feature.

## What the utopians teach political economy

Three takeaways.

**Designed institutions can outlive their designers.** The Rochdale Pioneers' cooperative store led to a global cooperative movement that long outlasted Owen's revolutionary ambitions. Designed institutions, even when they fail at their original purpose, can leave living infrastructure behind.

**Imagination is part of political economy.** The utopians' willingness to describe a different society in detail — what work would look like, what family would look like, how desire would be organized — was a precondition for any actual change. Marx, despite his polemics, depended on the utopian tradition to give a positive content to "socialism" beyond a critique of capitalism.

**Small-scale experiments can teach what theory cannot.** New Harmony failed. The Rochdale store succeeded. The difference was not in the moral grandeur of the vision but in the institutional details — democratic governance, modest scale, members' own savings as the capital, clear property rights inside the cooperative. Theory could not have predicted which would succeed. The experiments did.

## Figures to memorize

- **Henri de Saint-Simon** (1760–1825) — productivist socialism; technocratic planning
- **Charles Fourier** (1772–1837) — passions and phalanxes; early feminism
- **Robert Owen** (1771–1858) — New Lanark; New Harmony; cooperative movement
- **Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers** (1844) — modern cooperative model
- **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** (1809–1865) — *Property is theft*; mutualism; anarchism

- **Flora Tristan** (1803–1844) — international workers' solidarity; socialist feminism
