### COLD OPEN — 0:00–1:00

[On screen: a black-and-white photograph of Magnitogorsk under construction, 1931.]

**Narrator (VO):**
In 1932, a town that had not existed three years earlier produced more steel than France. The town was called Magnitogorsk. It was built by something close to a quarter of a million workers — some volunteers, many prisoners, all working in temperatures that fell below minus forty in winter — under direct command from a central planning committee fifteen hundred kilometers away in Moscow.

A few years later, the same planning system that built Magnitogorsk would also produce cities where the bread had not arrived for three weeks, fields where the harvest rotted because no one had sent trucks, factories that produced shoes that fit no one's feet. The Soviet planned economy was, simultaneously, one of the most impressive feats of industrial mobilization in human history and one of the most chronically dysfunctional consumer economies ever built.

What was happening? Could the system have been fixed? Or was it doomed from the start?

### CHAPTER 1 — THE CALCULATION DEBATE — 1:00–6:00

[On screen: photograph of Ludwig von Mises, then dissolve to Oskar Lange.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The most important theoretical debate in twentieth-century economics began in 1920, three years after the Bolsheviks took power, when an Austrian economist named Ludwig von Mises published a paper called *Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth*.

Mises's argument was elegant and devastating. He said: a complex industrial economy is not just a question of producing goods. It is a question of *allocating resources* — deciding, for every input you have, what to use it for among the many possible uses. Should this steel go to railways or to tanks? Should this labor go to mining or to textiles? Should this hectare of land grow wheat or potatoes?

Under a market system, prices coordinate these decisions. A price tells you how much of the resource other people want and how much it costs to provide. Resources flow to their most valued uses because anyone who can produce more value per unit of input will bid more for the input. The system is decentralized; nobody has to know everything; the price signals carry the information.

Take prices away — replace them with central planners deciding allocation — and you have to recreate, by hand, what the price system did automatically. Mises argued this was impossible at the scale of a modern industrial economy. The number of allocation decisions is too large. The information required is too dispersed. The planner has no signal telling him whether he has allocated steel correctly.

This is the *socialist calculation problem* — one of the most important arguments in modern political economy, and rigorous economic theory rather than propaganda or sloganeering.

For thirty years after Mises, socialist economists tried to answer him. The most influential reply came from a Polish economist named Oskar Lange. Lange argued that planners could, in fact, simulate market prices through a process of trial and error — adjust the prices, observe whether shortages or surpluses developed, adjust again. This was the *Lange-Lerner* model, and Lange thought it had refuted Mises by the late 1930s.

Friedrich Hayek then refined Mises's argument into something more subtle. The problem, Hayek said in his 1945 paper *The Use of Knowledge in Society*, was not just about prices. It was about *tacit, local knowledge* — the kind of knowledge that lives in the heads of millions of people about their own circumstances, their own preferences, their own production possibilities. Even a perfect planner with unlimited computing power could not aggregate this dispersed knowledge into a central plan, because the knowledge is not articulated; it exists only in the act of choosing.

Hayek's reply has aged better than Lange's. The Soviet planners did not have computers in 1932, and even after they did, the calculation difficulties did not go away.

### CHAPTER 2 — WHAT HAPPENED IN PRACTICE — 6:00–11:00

[On screen: Soviet factory floor footage, 1950s.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Now let us look at what actually happened.

The Soviet planners did succeed at producing heavy industrial goods in enormous quantities. Steel. Coal. Electricity. Machine tools. Tractors. Tanks. The Soviet Union, between 1928 and 1953, built more of these things, faster, than almost any country in human history. By the 1960s, it produced more steel than the United States and more cement than anyone.

How did they do this? Two reasons.

First, the kind of planning that produces a lot of one thing — steel, say — is much easier than the kind of planning that allocates many things to many uses. You set a target, allocate inputs to it, measure output, and hit it by brute force if necessary. The Soviet planners were very good at this. The system was a kind of single-industry monoculture, scaled across many industries.

Second, the Soviet Union began the period with huge unused resources — under-employed peasants, untapped natural resources, unused industrial capacity in the cities. Any halfway competent industrialization program could have produced rapid growth by mobilizing these resources. Stalin's particular methods were not the only way to do it. The growth was real, but it was *catch-up growth*. By the 1960s, when the easy gains had been exhausted, Soviet growth slowed dramatically and never recovered.

Where the Soviet planners *failed*, consistently, was in everything that required nuanced allocation. Consumer goods. Quality. Variety. Service. Innovation in non-priority sectors. The system that built Magnitogorsk could not produce a pair of shoes that fit. It could not produce a tomato that arrived at the store before it spoiled. It could not respond to what its citizens actually wanted, because its citizens' preferences did not factor into the planning decisions.

This is not because the planners were incompetent. The Soviet Union employed some of the world's leading mathematicians and economists in its planning apparatus. The Nobel laureate Leonid Kantorovich, a Soviet mathematician, developed *linear programming* — the optimization technique now used by every major corporation — specifically to help Soviet planners allocate resources more efficiently. The planners had the brains; what they could not have was the information.

### CHAPTER 3 — THE COMPUTING ANSWER, AND WHY IT FAILED — 11:00–15:30

[On screen: photograph of a 1960s Soviet mainframe computer.]

**Narrator (VO):**
By the 1960s, Soviet economists were aware that the planning system was failing. They tried to fix it. Reform attempts in Hungary, in Czechoslovakia, in the Soviet Union itself — under Liberman in 1965, under Kosygin's brief reforms — tried to incorporate price signals, profit incentives, and decentralized decision-making.

The most interesting attempt was Project Cybersyn in Chile in 1971–73 under the Allende government, which we will cover in Module 29. Soviet planners had tried something similar through the 1960s — the OGAS project, an attempt to build a nationwide computer network that would coordinate planning in real time. OGAS was killed by political infighting before it was built.

The cybernetic dream of the late twentieth century was that, with enough computing power, central planning could finally outperform markets. Walmart, the largest single firm in the world, does in fact run an enormous internal central plan — millions of products, billions of decisions, coordinated by a centralized system that does not use internal prices. Modern logistics companies do something similar. So it is not literally true that no large system can be planned.

But — and this is the important caveat — Walmart is planning a system in which the inputs and outputs are already shaped by an external market. Walmart's planners know what a tomato should cost because there is a market for tomatoes outside Walmart. Soviet planners had no such external benchmark. They had to construct the prices themselves, and they had no signal telling them when they got it wrong.

There is a serious case that with much more advanced computing — perhaps with the kind of artificial intelligence systems we are now building — some version of central planning could outperform a market-based system. The contemporary economist Daniel Saros has made this case in *Information Technology and Socialist Construction* (2014). The argument is not crazy. It is also not yet demonstrated. The Soviet experiment ran on the technology of the 1930s through 1980s, and within those constraints, the calculation problem remained intractable.

### CHAPTER 4 — WHAT THE PLAN COULD AND COULD NOT DO — 15:30–20:00

[On screen: long shot, Moscow in winter, 1970s.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Let us summarize.

What Soviet central planning *could* do, well:
— Mobilize enormous resources for a small number of priority sectors
— Build heavy industrial infrastructure where the technology was already known
— Catch up to advanced economies when starting from a low base
— Sustain a high rate of investment relative to consumption (the Soviet investment rate was around 30 percent of GDP, against perhaps 20 percent in market economies)
— Produce universal basic services: literacy, healthcare, housing, employment

What Soviet central planning *could not* do, persistently:
— Produce consumer goods that matched what consumers wanted
— Respond to changing preferences in real time
— Innovate outside the priority sectors
— Allocate resources to thousands of competing minor uses
— Maintain quality control without market discipline
— Produce information honestly through its own statistical systems

These are not opposite verdicts but the same one: the Soviet system was good at one kind of economic activity and bad at another, and it was unable to transition from the first kind to the second.

By the 1970s, the Soviet system had largely exhausted its catch-up growth and faced an economy that needed precisely the things it was bad at: quality consumer goods, innovation outside heavy industry, responsiveness to citizen preferences. The system could not deliver. It limped on for two more decades, propped up by oil revenue, before it collapsed in 1991.

### CHAPTER 5 — THE BROADER LESSON — 20:00–24:00

[On screen: data graphs showing comparative Soviet and Western growth rates 1928–1990.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The Soviet experiment was a real experiment. It tested a specific hypothesis at enormous scale and over a long period. The hypothesis was: a complex industrial economy can be coordinated by central planning without prices and without markets.

The result was: partially, in some sectors, for some periods, with massive coercion, and with chronic dysfunction in everything outside heavy industry.

This is not the same as the result the cold-war propaganda wanted, which was that planning failed totally and that markets succeeded totally. Both pictures are too neat. The Soviet system produced real things — industries, infrastructure, basic services, scientific achievements — that pure market systems do not always produce. Market systems produce real things — consumer goods, innovation, responsiveness — that the Soviet system did not.

The honest reading is that the calculation debate has a partial answer. Pure central planning of the Soviet kind has structural limits that cannot be overcome within the system. Pure market allocation also has structural limits — the externalities, the inequality, the consumer-goods focus that crowds out long-term investment — that markets cannot overcome on their own.

Most advanced economies today are mixed systems. The state plans some sectors — healthcare in much of Europe, military procurement everywhere, infrastructure, basic research. Markets allocate others. The mix shifts over time and across countries. The cold-war framing of "planning vs. markets" obscures the more interesting question, which is: which decisions should be made how, by whom, with what accountability?

The Soviet experiment was a chapter in the answer to that question. It is not a verdict in favor of either side.
