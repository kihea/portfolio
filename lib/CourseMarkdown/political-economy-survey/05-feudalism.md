The word *feudalism* gets used loosely. People call any system they dislike "neo-feudal." This module gives you the actual mechanism — what was being exchanged, who owed what to whom, and how the system held together for roughly seven centuries across most of Europe (and, in different forms, across Japan and parts of the Middle East).

## After Rome

The collapse of the Western Roman Empire in the fifth century CE did not produce a sudden break. Roman agricultural estates persisted under their new Germanic rulers. Latin lingered as the administrative language. The Christian Church kept records, ran schools, and accumulated land. But the long-distance trade routes that had supported Roman commerce withered. Coinage thinned out and cities shrank. The state — as a centralized authority commanding armies and collecting uniform taxes — fragmented into local lordships.

What remained was land, peasants who worked it, and warriors who claimed the right to protect (or coerce) those peasants. From this raw material, between roughly 800 and 1100 CE, the social order that historians call feudalism congealed.

## The basic mechanism

At its core, European feudalism was a pyramid of obligations:

**The king** — at the top in theory, though his actual power was often modest. He claimed ultimate dominion over the land of his realm.

**The lords** — nobles who held land directly from the king (or from a more powerful noble below the king). In exchange for the right to hold the land, they owed the king *military service* — showing up at war with a specified number of armed men, for a specified number of days per year. This was the *fief* (Latin *feudum*, from which "feudal" derives). A fief was not property in the modern sense. It was a conditional grant of authority over a territory and its inhabitants, granted in exchange for service.

**Sub-lords (vassals)** — knights and lesser nobles who held smaller portions of land from a lord, owing him a similar military obligation. The system *sub-infeudated* downward, lord to vassal to vassal, sometimes through five or six layers.

**The peasants** — at the bottom, on the actual ground, growing the food. Most were not free in the modern sense. They were *serfs* — bound to the land they worked, owing the lord a portion of their crop, a number of days of labor per week on the lord's own fields (the *demesne*), and various dues at marriage, inheritance, and the use of the lord's mill, oven, and wine press.

Note what is not in this list: a market for land, a market for labor, a price system that allocates production. Feudalism was not a market economy. It was a *direct extraction* economy, in which the lord did not pay the peasant a wage and then sell what the peasant produced. The lord simply took a portion of what was produced, in kind, by customary right.

## The manor

The basic unit of feudal economy was the *manor* — an estate consisting of (a) the lord's hall and demesne fields, (b) the peasant village and the strips of land each peasant household worked, and (c) the common lands (woods, pastures, meadows) used by everyone in the village under customary rules.

A working manor produced almost everything the lord and his household needed: grain, vegetables, meat, wool, cloth, beer, ale, tools. It was largely self-sufficient. Trade existed, but was peripheral — salt, iron, luxury goods, things that could not be produced locally. The peasant who worked these lands rarely saw money in his lifetime.

The peasant's week was structured by obligation. Two or three days, depending on the manor, were spent working the lord's demesne. The remainder he worked his own strips. Custom — written and unwritten — set how many days he owed, what dues he paid at his daughter's wedding, how much grain he must grind at the lord's mill (and what cut the lord took), what penalties applied to poaching in the lord's woods. The system was not arbitrary; it was rule-bound. But the rules were made by people whose interests aligned with extraction.

## Why "feudalism" is contested

Many medieval historians dislike the word *feudalism*. Susan Reynolds, in *Fiefs and Vassals* (1994), argued that the neat pyramidal structure historians have described was largely imposed by later legal scholars trying to organize messy reality. Practices varied enormously across regions and centuries. The "feudal pyramid" in a textbook is closer to an ideal type than to actual eleventh-century Picardy.

Other historians — most influentially Marc Bloch in *Feudal Society* (1939) and Maurice Dobb in *Studies in the Development of Capitalism* (1946) — held onto the term as a useful summary of an economy in which the dominant form of surplus extraction was rent-in-kind or labor service from a peasant tied to the land of a lord whose authority derived from a chain of military obligation.

For this course, we use *feudalism* in Bloch's and Dobb's sense — as the dominant economic-political form across most of medieval Western Europe between roughly 800 and 1400 CE — while flagging that the term flattens enormous variation.

## Feudalism outside Europe

The label *feudalism* has been applied, with varying degrees of justification, to other societies. Tokugawa Japan (1603–1868) had a strikingly similar pyramidal structure of daimyo, samurai, and peasants tied to rice-producing estates. Mughal India and Safavid Persia organized landholding around grants (*jagir*, *iqta'*) that resemble fiefs in important ways. Ethiopia retained a feudal land system into the twentieth century.

The most important non-European case for political economy is China, which is *not* usefully described as feudal in the European sense. Imperial China, from the Qin (221 BCE) onward, had a centralized bureaucratic state, a functional currency, examination-based civil service, and a peasantry that — though often impoverished and rebellious — was not legally bound to the land in the European serf sense. The Chinese path to modernity ran through state structures Europe did not have, and the differences will matter later in the course.

## How feudalism ended

There is no single answer to *why* feudalism collapsed. The major mechanisms include:

**The Black Death (1347–1351)** — killed roughly one-third of Europe's population. The dramatic shortage of peasant labor that followed gave surviving peasants leverage they had never had. Wages — in places where they existed at all — rose sharply. Lords who tried to enforce old labor obligations faced revolts (the English Peasants' Revolt of 1381, the French Jacquerie of 1358). Over the next century, serfdom in Western Europe gradually dissolved, replaced by money rents and wage labor.

**The rise of towns and trade** — cities had always existed alongside feudal estates, but from the eleventh century onward they expanded rapidly, supported by long-distance trade routes that revived after the early medieval contraction. Town charters granted urban populations freedoms — "town air makes you free" was the German saying — and offered peasants a route out of serfdom. The merchant class that grew up in these towns (the *bourgeoisie*) would later become a powerful political force.

**State centralization** — kings, building tax bases and standing armies, gradually subordinated the nobility. The independent military function of the lord became less essential as kings could field their own troops. The lord's economic privileges, however, often persisted long after his political ones eroded.

**The transformation of agriculture** — in England especially, the *enclosure movement* (beginning in the late fifteenth century, accelerating in the eighteenth) converted common lands into private estates, displaced peasants from the countryside, and created the labor force that would later staff industrial factories. This was a deliberate political act. It required Parliament. We will meet it again in Unit 1, Module 7.

In Eastern Europe — Russia, Poland, Hungary — feudalism did not dissolve in the late medieval period. It intensified. The so-called *second serfdom* hardened the position of the peasantry as Eastern landowners sought to supply Western Europe's growing demand for grain. Russian serfdom was not abolished until 1861.

## What feudalism teaches political economy

Three lessons that will matter throughout the course:

**Property is not natural.** Under feudalism, no one "owned" land in the modern sense. Lords held it conditionally. Peasants worked it by custom. The Church owned vast estates as a corporate entity. Land was a bundle of overlapping rights, not a thing to be bought and sold. The idea of *absolute private property* — that an owner can do anything with land they choose — had to be invented. It is a modern construct, not a discovery.

**Markets do not necessarily allocate the central things.** For most of human history, what people ate, where they lived, and what work they did was not determined by market prices but by inherited social position. Feudalism is not a market economy in any meaningful sense, and it sustained complex civilizations for centuries.

**Surplus extraction can be direct or indirect.** The feudal lord took grain directly. The modern capitalist hires the laborer for a wage and takes the rest of what the laborer produces as profit. Both are mechanisms for extracting surplus from those who produce it. Marx will spend a great deal of energy on how these two mechanisms differ in form while resembling each other in function.

## Figures to know

- **Marc Bloch** (1886–1944) — French historian; *Feudal Society* (1939–40); a founder of the Annales school. Executed by the Gestapo for his role in the French Resistance.
- **Maurice Dobb** (1900–1976) — British Marxist economic historian; *Studies in the Development of Capitalism* (1946) reframed feudalism's decline as a question of economic structure rather than ideology.
- **Henri Pirenne** (1862–1935) — Belgian historian; argued that the rise of long-distance trade and cities, more than agricultural change, drove feudalism's transformation.
- **Susan Reynolds** (1929–2021) — British medieval historian; her *Fiefs and Vassals* (1994) is the leading critique of the textbook "feudal pyramid."
- **Robert Brenner** (b. 1943) — American historian; the *Brenner Debate* in the 1970s reignited the argument over why feudalism gave way to capitalism in England rather than elsewhere.
