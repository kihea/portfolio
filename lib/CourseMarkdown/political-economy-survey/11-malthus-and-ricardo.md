A generation after Adam Smith, two thinkers reframed political economy by introducing the assumption Smith had not made: that the new commercial system might run into limits, both natural and structural. Thomas Robert Malthus and David Ricardo are the founders of classical political economy in its mature form. Marx would later say that everything that mattered in economics had to be argued against them.

## Thomas Malthus and the population problem

The Reverend Thomas Robert Malthus (1766–1834) was a curate, then a professor of political economy at the East India Company's college at Haileybury — the first chair of political economy in Britain. His famous book, *An Essay on the Principle of Population* (1798, revised 1803), was provoked by an argument with his father, who believed that human progress would continue indefinitely.

Malthus disagreed. His central argument:

- Population, if unchecked, tends to grow *geometrically* (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 ...). Humans are biologically capable of doubling their numbers every twenty-five years or so.
- Food production, by contrast, can grow at best *arithmetically* (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 ...). The amount of arable land is finite; improvements yield diminishing returns; new acreage is harder to bring under cultivation than old.
- Therefore population *must* be checked, in every generation, by something. If it is not checked by deliberate restraint — late marriage, sexual abstinence — it will be checked by *misery* — famine, disease, war.

The implication was bleak. Wages could not rise sustainably above subsistence, because if they did, the working class would have more children, the population would grow, the demand for food would push food prices up, and real wages would fall back to subsistence. Welfare schemes, by enabling the poor to feed more children, would make the long-run situation worse, not better. Malthus opposed the Poor Laws — the English system of parish relief for the destitute — on these grounds.

The argument was empirically wrong almost immediately. Britain's population did indeed grow geometrically in the nineteenth century, but food production grew faster than arithmetically, partly because of new technology (mechanization, fertilizers, refrigerated transport) and partly because of imports from newly opened lands in the Americas and Australia. Malthus had not anticipated that the system could escape its limits by *expanding the relevant frontier* — by industrializing agriculture, opening colonies, and absorbing food from elsewhere.

But the *form* of Malthusian argument — that an exponential process running into a fixed resource produces a catastrophic check — has reappeared throughout the history of political economy and ecology. The Club of Rome's *Limits to Growth* (1972), the climate crisis debates of the twenty-first century, the demographic anxieties of post-2010 East Asia — all carry Malthusian DNA. When you hear someone say "we cannot grow forever in a finite world," you are hearing Malthus.

## Malthus and the rent problem

Malthus also made an important contribution to the theory of rent that Ricardo would systematize. Malthus argued that as a country grew, increasingly marginal land would have to be brought under cultivation to feed the population. Marginal land is, by definition, less productive than the best land. But under competition, the price of grain has to cover the cost of production on the *most* expensive land in use — otherwise that land would go out of cultivation. The owner of the best land receives a windfall: he can sell at the marginal price while producing at a much lower cost. The difference is *rent*.

This means rent rises as the economy grows. The landlord, who did not produce the increase, captures it. The growing surplus of the economy flows disproportionately to the rentier class. Malthus, an ordained clergyman who depended in part on rental income from his curacy and from his wife's estate, was not unsympathetic to landlords. But he saw the structural implication clearly. We will see Ricardo turn this insight into a sustained critique of the British landed aristocracy.

## David Ricardo: the systematic theorist

David Ricardo (1772–1823) was a London stockbroker, son of a Sephardic Jewish family, who made a fortune speculating on Napoleonic War-era bonds and retired in his early forties to study political economy. His major work, *On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817), is the most rigorous economic book of the nineteenth century. It is also the hardest classical text to read — dense, abstract, and unforgiving of inattention.

Ricardo's project was to take Smith's framework and tighten it into a deductive system. Where Smith mixed historical anecdote, moral observation, and economic analysis, Ricardo abstracted away from the historical material and tried to derive consequences from a few core assumptions. Modern economics is much more Ricardian in its method than Smithian.

The core Ricardian claims:

**The labor theory of value.** Ricardo took up Smith's suggestion that the value of a commodity is fundamentally determined by the labor required to produce it. He sharpened this into a more precise doctrine: the *relative* prices of commodities, in long-run equilibrium, are proportional to the *relative quantities of labor* embodied in them. If a coat takes twice as many hours to make as a pair of shoes, it will (in long-run equilibrium, accounting for capital) tend to sell for twice as much. This is the labor theory of value in its classical form. Marx would adopt it (with crucial modifications) and turn it into the basis of his critique of capitalism.

**Differential rent.** Ricardo formalized Malthus's insight. Land of varying quality is brought into cultivation as demand grows. The marginal land — the least productive currently in use — yields no rent. All better land yields rent proportional to its superiority over the marginal land. As population grows and marginal land becomes worse, rent rises on the better land. This is *not* a moral judgment about landlords — it is a structural feature of the system. The result is that landed property captures an increasing share of the social surplus as the economy expands.

**The iron law of wages.** Combining Malthus and the labor theory of value, Ricardo argued that the natural wage of labor was the subsistence wage — what it cost to feed, clothe, and house a worker and replace him with the next generation. Wages above subsistence would cause population to grow, increasing labor supply and pushing wages back down. The mechanism, again, was structural — not a condemnation of workers but a feature of any labor market governed by population dynamics.

**The theory of comparative advantage.** Ricardo's most famous contribution. Even if one country is better at producing everything than another country, both countries will gain by specializing in the goods in which they have a *comparative* advantage (the goods they produce at the lowest opportunity cost) and trading. The standard illustration: Portugal can produce both wine and cloth more efficiently than England, but if Portugal is much more efficient at wine than at cloth, both countries are better off if Portugal specializes in wine and England in cloth. This is the theoretical foundation of free-trade doctrine, and it is taught in every introductory economics course today.

**The tendency of profit to fall.** This is the dynamic that Ricardo most worried about. As the economy grows, population grows, marginal land must be brought in, rent rises, the cost of feeding workers rises, wages must therefore rise, and profits — what is left after rent and wages — must therefore fall. Eventually profit falls so low that capital accumulation stops, and growth ends in a *stationary state*. Ricardo thought this stationary state was distant but inevitable.

## The political stakes of Ricardian theory

Ricardo's economics had immediate political consequences. The British Corn Laws of 1815 imposed high tariffs on imported grain, protecting domestic landlords from cheaper continental wheat. Ricardo, a member of Parliament from 1819 to 1823, opposed the Corn Laws fiercely. By raising the price of grain, the Corn Laws raised the cost of subsistence, which raised wages, which lowered profits, which slowed industrial growth. The landlords benefited; the industrial capitalists and the working class suffered.

The argument structured British politics for thirty years. The Corn Laws were finally repealed in 1846 under Robert Peel's Conservative government, after the Irish Potato Famine made the contradiction unbearable. The Anti-Corn Law League, led by Richard Cobden and John Bright, made Ricardian theory into a mass political movement. This was the moment when classical political economy stopped being just an academic discipline and became — for better and worse — the operative ideology of the British state.

## Ricardo and the labor theory of value: what Marx took

Two things to flag for later modules.

First, Ricardo's labor theory of value is a long-run, equilibrium claim. It is not a claim that every market price always equals labor content. It is a claim that *underlying* relative prices, in long-run equilibrium, are governed by relative labor inputs. Short-run prices fluctuate around this underlying value due to supply and demand. Marx would adopt this distinction (he called the underlying values *prices of production*) and use it to argue that profit must come from somewhere other than market fluctuation.

Second, Ricardo did not draw the radical conclusion Marx would later draw. Ricardo accepted that the laborer received the value of his labor (the subsistence wage) and that the capitalist received the value of his capital (the profit). He did not see that this could be a problem. Marx would argue: if labor is the source of value, and the worker receives only subsistence, the surplus must come from somewhere — and the somewhere is the *unpaid* portion of the worker's labor. We will return to this in Unit 3.

## What to take from Malthus and Ricardo

Three things.

**The form of structural argument.** Malthus and Ricardo introduced the idea that economic outcomes are determined by *system properties* — diminishing returns, marginal pricing, structural distribution between classes — rather than by individual choices or moral failings. This is a foundational move.

**The dismal science.** Thomas Carlyle, in a different context, gave classical economics its nickname. Malthusian limits, the iron law of wages, the falling rate of profit — the whole picture was one in which the economy was running toward some kind of limit. The optimism of Smith was over.

**The class structure of distribution.** Ricardo's framework identified three social classes — landlords, capitalists, workers — each receiving a distinct income (rent, profit, wages) and each with a distinct dynamic interest. Rents tend to rise; profits tend to fall; wages tend to subsistence. This three-class structure of distribution would be central to Marx and central to twentieth-century debates about who really benefits from economic growth.

## Figures to know

- **Thomas Malthus** (1766–1834) — *Essay on the Principle of Population* (1798).
- **David Ricardo** (1772–1823) — *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817).
- **Jean-Baptiste Say** (1767–1832) — French economist; "Say's Law" — supply creates its own demand — covered in Module 13.
- **Robert Malthus's debate with Ricardo** — they corresponded extensively about gluts and crises; Malthus thought general overproduction was possible; Ricardo and Say denied it; Marx and later Keynes returned to Malthus's side of this argument.
- **Richard Cobden** (1804–1865) and **John Bright** (1811–1889) — the Anti-Corn Law League leaders who translated Ricardian theory into mass politics.
