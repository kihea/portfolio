Before the history, the vocabulary. The terms in this module will appear in every unit of the course. Learn them once and you will save yourself thirty hours.

The plan is to introduce the words in plain English, give the standard definitions, and flag the places where major thinkers have disagreed about what the words actually mean. Where definitions are contested, that fact is itself a clue — disagreements about a word are usually disagreements about a system.

## The four motions of any economy

Every economy, from a hunter-gatherer band to twenty-first-century global finance, can be described in terms of four motions:

**Production** — making things or providing services that other people will use. This includes growing food, building shelter, mining minerals, writing software, teaching children. Production answers the question: *what is made, by whom, and how?*

**Distribution** — moving what has been produced to the people who will use it. Distribution answers: *who gets what, in what amount, and on what conditions?*

**Exchange** — the specific mechanism of transfer. Gift, barter, sale, tribute, theft, allocation by central plan. Exchange answers: *by what method does a thing move from one person to another?*

**Consumption** — using up what was produced. Eating the bread, wearing out the shoes, driving the car. Consumption answers: *what happens to the product, and what does its use cost?*

If you can describe an economy in those four motions, you can ask the four questions that matter: *who controls each one, and on what terms?* Every political economist in this course is, in the end, answering a version of those four questions.

## The factors of production

Classical political economy identified three "factors of production" — the inputs without which nothing could be made:

**Land** — natural resources. Soil, water, minerals, climate, location. Land is non-reproducible. You cannot make more of it. (You can drain a swamp; you cannot manufacture a continent.)

**Labor** — human work. Physical, mental, skilled, unskilled. Labor is reproducible — children grow up, populations expand — but each unit of labor is a person who must eat, sleep, and consent to work.

**Capital** — produced means of production. Tools, machines, buildings, infrastructure. Capital is reproducible but only by directing labor and land toward making it rather than toward making consumption goods. To build a factory, you must take time and effort away from baking bread.

Some economists, starting in the early twentieth century, added a fourth factor:

**Entrepreneurship** — the activity of combining the other three into a productive enterprise and bearing the risk of its failure. Whether this is a separate factor or just a kind of labor is still debated. Marxists generally reject the separation. Schumpeterians insist on it. We will return to this.

Two crucial things to notice. First: the factors are not symmetric. Land cannot be created, labor cannot be created without creating people who will demand food and rights, capital can be created but only at a cost. Second: each factor has a corresponding income — land earns *rent*, labor earns *wages*, capital earns *interest* or *profit*. The question of *what rate* each one earns is the question of distribution. The question of *why* each one earns what it does is the question of value.

## Value — the oldest argument in economics

What makes a thing worth what it is worth? This is the central question that divides classical political economy from neoclassical economics.

There are three families of answers:

**Labor theories of value** — a thing's value is determined by the human labor required to produce it. This view was held in some form by Adam Smith, David Ricardo, and Karl Marx (each with important differences). The intuition: if it takes me twelve hours to grow a bushel of wheat and you twenty hours to weave a yard of cloth, and we trade, there must be some underlying equivalence between the labor in each.

**Cost-of-production theories of value** — a thing's value is determined by what it cost to produce it, where "cost" includes the inputs (land, labor, capital) at their market prices. This is roughly the position of John Stuart Mill and the late classicals.

**Subjective (or marginal) theories of value** — a thing's value is determined by how much someone wants it, at the margin. This is the view that overtook the field in the 1870s, associated with William Stanley Jevons, Carl Menger, and Léon Walras (the "marginal revolution"). It is the foundation of modern neoclassical economics. Value is in the eye of the buyer.

A modern undergraduate economics course will teach you the third view as if the first two were never serious contenders. They were, and still are. The disagreement matters because labor theories of value tend to support critiques of profit (where does it come from if labor produces the value?) while subjective theories tend to dissolve those critiques (profit is just a price, set by demand).

We will spend real time on this argument in Unit 2 and Unit 3.

## Mode of production

A *mode of production* is the way an entire society organizes the three factors. It is bigger than a "system" and bigger than an "economy" — it includes the legal arrangements, the property forms, and the social roles that hold the whole thing together.

Marx famously distinguished:

- **Primitive communism** — small, propertyless, kin-based societies
- **Slave** — labor extracted from people who are themselves property
- **Feudal** — labor extracted as rent or service from peasants tied to land owned by a noble class
- **Capitalist** — labor extracted as wages from workers who are formally free but own no means of production
- **Communist** — labor freely contributed in a society without classes or private ownership of means of production

You do not have to be a Marxist to find this scheme useful. The point is that an *economy* sits inside a wider arrangement of property, law, and force, and that arrangement can be classified.

Non-Marxist historians use different schemes — Karl Polanyi distinguished reciprocity, redistribution, householding, and market exchange; Max Weber distinguished traditional, rational-legal, and charismatic forms of authority and their economic correlates. Each scheme illuminates different things. We will use several throughout the course.

## Markets, plans, and the spectrum between

A *market* is a place — physical or notional — where people meet to exchange. The word *market* is used in two senses that are worth distinguishing:

- **Market as institution** — actual buyers and sellers actually trading. Markets in this sense have existed in almost every human society.
- **Market society** — a society in which the market is the dominant mechanism of distribution, in which land and labor are themselves traded, and in which prices set without authority govern most economic decisions.

The second sense is historically recent. Karl Polanyi argued that it had to be deliberately constructed, and that the construction required massive state violence — the enclosure of common lands, the abolition of customary protections for the poor, the legal restructuring of labor as a commodity. We return to Polanyi in Unit 1, Module 7.

The opposite of a market is a *plan* — a centrally directed allocation, where decisions about what gets produced and who gets it are made by an authority rather than by price signals. The Soviet Union ran a planned economy. So does a single corporation, internally. So does a household. The question is not whether plans exist — they always do — but at what scale and under whose control.

Most real economies are mixed. The United States, often called a market economy, allocates trillions of dollars by political plan (the military, Medicare, agricultural subsidies). The Soviet Union, often called a planned economy, retained substantial markets in consumer goods, especially in its later decades. The spectrum between pure market and pure plan is where most of political economy happens.

## A few more terms

**Capital accumulation** — the process by which capital grows over time, generally through reinvested profit. The defining motion of capitalism, in most accounts.

**Rent** — payment for the use of something non-reproducible. Traditionally, the income of land. Modern economists also speak of *rent* in a generalized sense — any income that exceeds what would be needed to keep the resource in use. Monopoly rents, patent rents, regulatory rents.

**Surplus** — what an economy produces above what is needed to reproduce its inputs. The bread the farmer does not eat, the cloth the weaver does not wear. Who gets the surplus, and on what authority, is arguably the central question of political economy.

**Exploitation** — a contested term. In Marxist usage, it has a specific technical meaning: receiving less than the value one's labor adds to the product. In everyday usage, it means unfair treatment. We will use the Marxist sense when discussing Marx and the everyday sense otherwise, and flag the difference when it matters.

**Class** — a group of people sharing a common relationship to the means of production. Workers (sell labor), capitalists (own means of production and hire labor), petty bourgeoisie (own small means of production and work them, like a sole-proprietor shopkeeper), peasants (work land they have customary rights to but may not formally own), nobility (own land and extract rent), and so on. Different traditions classify class differently; we will see Weberian and Bourdieusian variations later.

**Commodity** — anything produced for exchange rather than for direct use. A wheat plant grown to feed the farmer is not a commodity. The same wheat sold at market is. Commodification is the historical process by which more and more of life is reorganized for exchange. Pollution rights are now commodities. So is your attention, in a sense, on most websites.

## Why this vocabulary is worth memorizing

Each of these terms is a hinge on which a major argument turns. If you do not have *surplus* in your vocabulary, you cannot read Marx. If you do not have *rent* in its broad sense, you cannot read modern critiques of platform capitalism. If you do not have *factors of production*, you cannot read Smith. If you do not have *mode of production*, you cannot compare feudalism to capitalism without smuggling in one system's assumptions to describe the other.

Take the vocabulary seriously. Come back to this module when you forget. The rest of the course assumes you have it.
