China's twentieth century is the most consequential single national story in modern political economy. A country of roughly 500 million in 1949 became a country of 1.4 billion. A peasant economy at the margin of the world system became, by the 2020s, the largest manufacturing economy on earth and the second-largest economy overall. The transformation passed through three distinct phases: Mao's revolutionary state-socialism (1949–1976), Deng's reform period (1978–1992), and the contemporary system of state-managed capitalism (1992–present). Each phase made choices the others reversed. The continuities are also real.

This module covers all three, with weight on the Maoist period and the Dengist transition.

## Before 1949

China entered the twentieth century in collapse. The Qing dynasty fell in 1911 after a humiliating century of foreign invasion (the Opium Wars, 1839–60, had opened China to British opium imports backed by gunboats; the Boxer Rebellion of 1900 had been crushed by an eight-nation alliance), internal rebellion (the Taiping Rebellion of 1850–64 had killed perhaps 20 million people), and dynastic exhaustion. The Republic of China that succeeded the Qing was nominal; real power was held by regional warlords.

The Chinese Communist Party (CCP) was founded in 1921 by a small group of Marxist intellectuals — Chen Duxiu, Li Dazhao, and a young librarian named Mao Zedong — who had been radicalized by the May Fourth Movement of 1919, a mass nationalist response to the Treaty of Versailles handing former German concessions in China to Japan rather than returning them to China.

The CCP's path differed sharply from the Soviet model. The Russian Revolution had been an urban uprising. China's industrial proletariat in the 1920s was tiny. After a disastrous attempt to organize urban workers — culminating in the Shanghai Massacre of 1927, when Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalist forces turned on their Communist allies and killed thousands of organizers — the CCP retreated to the countryside. Mao, against orthodox Marxist doctrine, argued that the peasantry would be the revolutionary class in China. He developed strategies of *people's war*: small guerrilla bands operating from rural bases, building support among peasants by land reform, then expanding outward.

The CCP's Long March of 1934–35 — a 9,000-kilometer retreat from Nationalist encirclement in Jiangxi to a new base in Yan'an in the northwest — became the founding myth of the party. The march took a year; perhaps 86,000 began it, perhaps 8,000 arrived. Mao emerged from it as undisputed party leader.

The Japanese invasion of China (1937–45) reorganized the political landscape. The CCP fought a guerrilla war against the Japanese, expanded its territorial base, built mass support among peasants in occupied territories. When the Japanese surrendered in 1945, the CCP held substantial territory and an army of perhaps a million. The civil war that followed (1946–49) was won by the Communists decisively. Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalists retreated to Taiwan. Mao proclaimed the People's Republic of China in Beijing on October 1, 1949.

## The Maoist economy: phase one (1949–1957)

The first eight years of the People's Republic were broadly successful by most measures. The new state:

- Distributed land to roughly 300 million peasants — possibly the largest land reform in human history
- Stabilized the currency, ending the hyperinflation that had crippled the late Nationalist regime
- Crushed organized crime, opium addiction, and warlord remnants
- Built basic literacy programs that raised adult literacy from below 20 percent to roughly 50 percent in a decade
- Began industrialization on a Soviet model with Soviet technical assistance, including approximately 156 major industrial projects built with direct Soviet support
- Introduced rural cooperatives that brought peasants into collective production

By 1957, China had a working state, a growing industrial base, and a substantially improved standard of living for the bulk of its population. The First Five-Year Plan (1953–57) achieved its targets.

This was also the period of the *Hundred Flowers Campaign* (1956–57), in which Mao invited intellectuals to criticize the party and the state. The criticism that emerged was sharper than Mao had expected. The *Anti-Rightist Campaign* that followed (1957–59) reversed direction, persecuting hundreds of thousands of intellectuals who had spoken out, sending many to labor camps. The pattern — opening, then crackdown — would repeat throughout Mao's rule.

## The Great Leap Forward (1958–1962)

In 1958, Mao launched the *Great Leap Forward*, an attempt to leap past the Soviet model by mobilizing the entire population for simultaneous agricultural and industrial transformation. Communes of tens of thousands of peasants replaced the village-scale cooperatives. Backyard steel furnaces appeared in every village to produce steel from scrap metal (the steel was mostly useless). Agricultural targets were inflated to fantastic levels. Reports of bumper harvests were extracted from local officials terrified to report failure.

The actual harvest collapsed. The reasons were many: the destruction of skilled peasant labor (much of it diverted to backyard steel and infrastructure projects), the failure of new agricultural techniques imported from Lysenkoist Soviet biology, bad weather in several regions, and the central planning collapse that followed the reported (false) bumper harvests — grain was extracted from the countryside at levels the actual harvest could not sustain.

The resulting famine of 1959–1962 is the largest in human history. The most careful demographic studies — by historians Yang Jisheng (a Chinese journalist and Communist Party member whose father starved to death in 1959) and Frank Dikötter — estimate excess deaths in the range of 30 to 45 million.

The Great Leap was a catastrophe, after which Mao stepped back from day-to-day economic management and pragmatists — Liu Shaoqi, Zhou Enlai, Deng Xiaoping — were given the task of recovery. The economy substantially recovered between 1962 and 1965.

## The Cultural Revolution (1966–1976)

In 1966, Mao struck back. The *Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution* was, in effect, Mao's attempt to use mass mobilization to displace the party officials who had taken power back from him after the Great Leap. He called on youth — eventually known as the *Red Guards* — to attack "capitalist roaders" and "bourgeois remnants" within the party and society at large.

The result was a decade of mass political violence, factional warfare, and economic disruption. Schools and universities closed for years. The Red Guards split into rival factions and turned on each other; the army was called in to restore order in 1968 and 1969. Senior party officials — including Liu Shaoqi, who died under house arrest from neglected medical conditions — were purged. Millions of urban youth were sent to the countryside in the *Down to the Countryside Movement*, ostensibly to learn from peasants, in practice to defuse urban factional violence.

Estimates of deaths during the Cultural Revolution vary from several hundred thousand to several million, depending on what counts. The damage to Chinese institutions — universities, scientific research, the legal system, the party itself — would shape China for decades.

Mao died in September 1976, and within a month the Gang of Four — the radical clique around Mao's wife, Jiang Qing, that had run the Cultural Revolution — were arrested, ending the decade-long political nightmare abruptly.

## The Dengist turn (1978–1992)

Deng Xiaoping, twice purged during the Cultural Revolution, emerged in 1978 as the dominant leader of the CCP. The Third Plenum of the Eleventh Central Committee in December 1978 marked the official beginning of *Gaige Kaifang* — Reform and Opening Up.

The Dengist economic program had several distinctive features:

**Decollectivization of agriculture.** The commune system was gradually dismantled between 1978 and 1984. Land was leased back to peasant households under long-term contracts (the *Household Responsibility System*). Peasants kept what they produced above a state quota. Agricultural output expanded dramatically.

**Special Economic Zones.** Beginning in 1980, designated coastal cities — Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Shantou, Xiamen — were opened to foreign investment under capitalist rules. Shenzhen, a fishing village of 30,000 in 1980, would become a megalopolis of over 10 million by the 2000s.

**Township and Village Enterprises (TVEs).** Local government-owned manufacturing firms that operated under market conditions and competed with each other and with state-owned enterprises. TVEs absorbed enormous rural labor and accounted for much of the early growth.

**Gradual privatization of state-owned enterprises.** This proceeded slowly in the 1980s, accelerating in the 1990s under Zhu Rongji.

**Maintained one-party rule.** Deng was clear that economic reform did not extend to political reform. The Tiananmen Square crackdown of June 1989 — when the People's Liberation Army was used to suppress mass democracy protests, killing somewhere between several hundred and several thousand people — was the most visible demonstration. Economic liberalization without political liberalization became, and remains, the formula.

Deng's famous slogan — "It doesn't matter whether the cat is black or white, so long as it catches mice" — captured the pragmatic flexibility of the period. Another characteristic Deng line: "To get rich is glorious."

## What this transformation produced

Between 1978 and 2022, China averaged annual real GDP growth of around 9 percent for forty-four years. No other major economy has sustained this rate over such a period. Hundreds of millions of Chinese were lifted out of absolute poverty by World Bank measures — the largest reduction in absolute poverty in human history.

Income inequality, suppressed during the Maoist period, rose substantially. China's Gini coefficient rose from below 0.30 in the late 1970s to roughly 0.46 by the 2010s — comparable to the United States and substantially higher than European social democracies. A wealthy capitalist class emerged inside what remained, formally, a communist state.

The relationship between the Chinese state and the Chinese economy is unlike any prior model: the CCP holds ultimate political authority, many large firms have CCP committees inside them, and state-owned enterprises remain dominant in strategic sectors — energy, banking, telecommunications, defense. The private sector has expanded enormously but operates inside political constraints that can change without warning, as the 2020–22 crackdowns on technology, education, and real estate firms demonstrated.

What is China? The standard answer is *state capitalism* or *authoritarian capitalism* — a system that combines market mechanisms in production and consumption with state ownership of strategic sectors and political monopoly by a single party. Western observers have argued for thirty years that this system would eventually liberalize politically as it grew richer. It has not. Whether it will, whether the model is stable, and whether other developing countries can copy it are open questions.

## The lesser-known figures

A few people in this story deserve more attention than they usually get.

**Chen Yun** (1905–1995) was a senior CCP leader who advocated for what he called the "birdcage economy": the market was the bird, the plan was the cage; the bird could fly inside the cage but had to stay within its limits. Chen was a counterweight to both the Maoist radicals and the Dengist reformers. His framework — that markets are useful tools that must be politically constrained — captures much of how the contemporary Chinese state still operates.

**Zhao Ziyang** (1919–2005) was Premier and then General Secretary in the 1980s. He pushed reform further than Deng, lost, and then sympathized publicly with the Tiananmen protesters in 1989 — for which he was purged and spent the rest of his life under house arrest. His secret memoir, *Prisoner of the State* (2009), is one of the most important inside accounts of the Chinese reform period.

**Yang Jisheng** (b. 1940) is the journalist whose investigation of the Great Leap Forward famine — *Tombstone* (2008) — broke the silence inside China about the actual scale of the deaths. The book is banned in China; it is one of the most important works of twentieth-century Chinese history.

**Justin Yifu Lin** (b. 1952) is an economist who served as Chief Economist of the World Bank from 2008 to 2012 and developed *New Structural Economics*, a framework for understanding development that draws on Chinese experience. His argument: developing countries should not try to leap directly to high-tech sectors but should build comparative advantages in sectors close to their current capabilities, with the state coordinating the upgrading process. The framework is gaining influence in development economics globally.

## What China teaches political economy

**Market socialism is possible — sort of.** The Chinese model demonstrates that you can combine market mechanisms with political monopoly by a single party for at least four decades. Whether the combination is sustainable, just, or replicable is a different question, but the empirical fact is established.

**Development is possible at speed.** The orthodox view in 1978 was that developing countries faced an insurmountable middle-income trap. China's success has changed the empirical landscape and the theoretical debate. *How* China succeeded — through coordinated state investment, export-led growth, technology transfer (often coerced), and aggressive industrial policy — has reopened questions that liberal development economics had considered closed.

**One-party states have specific failure modes.** The Great Leap Forward famine, the Cultural Revolution, the Tiananmen crackdown, the Xinjiang detentions, the post-2012 contraction of civil society under Xi Jinping — all of these reflect specific features of single-party rule. The Chinese state's economic capacities are real; so are its political pathologies.

**The future is open.** The standard Western prediction that economic liberalization would produce political liberalization has not held up. The standard Chinese-government prediction that authoritarian growth could continue indefinitely is also not yet established. What happens in China over the next twenty years will decide more about the future of political economy than what happens in any other country.

## Figures to know

- **Mao Zedong** (1893–1976) — CCP leader 1935–1976
- **Zhou Enlai** (1898–1976) — Premier; the regime's chief diplomat and frequently its restraint on Maoist excess
- **Deng Xiaoping** (1904–1997) — paramount leader 1978–c. 1992; architect of reform
- **Liu Shaoqi** (1898–1969) — CCP Vice-Chairman; purged in the Cultural Revolution; rehabilitated posthumously
- **Lin Biao** (1907–1971) — Mao's designated successor in the early Cultural Revolution; killed in a mysterious plane crash after an alleged coup attempt
- **Jiang Qing** (1914–1991) — Mao's wife; leader of the Gang of Four
- **Chen Yun** (1905–1995) — "birdcage economy" theorist
- **Zhao Ziyang** (1919–2005) — reformist premier and general secretary; purged 1989
- **Zhu Rongji** (b. 1928) — Premier 1998–2003; architect of state-owned enterprise reform
- **Xi Jinping** (b. 1953) — CCP General Secretary since 2012
