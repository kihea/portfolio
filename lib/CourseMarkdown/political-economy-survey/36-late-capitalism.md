The phrase *late capitalism* originated with the German Marxist Werner Sombart in 1902 to describe what he saw as the third and final phase of capitalist development. It was given canonical Marxist treatment by Ernest Mandel in *Late Capitalism* (1972). It entered the English-language cultural lexicon through Fredric Jameson's *Postmodernism, or the Cultural Logic of Late Capitalism* (1991). By the 2010s, it had become a vague but widely circulated phrase used to describe a contemporary capitalism that seemed to have left its mid-twentieth-century form behind without having found a stable new one.

This is the longest module in the course. It covers the contemporary phenomena that are reshaping the system: platform capitalism, the rise of precarity, surveillance, the climate crisis, and the structural problems that don't have names yet.

## Platform capitalism

The economist Nick Srnicek, in his short 2017 book *Platform Capitalism*, gave the most influential typology of the digital platforms that have come to dominate large sectors of the global economy. He distinguished:

**Advertising platforms** — Google, Meta. Extract user data, sell targeted advertising space. The fundamental product is the user's attention; the actual customers are advertisers.

**Cloud platforms** — Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud. Rent out computing infrastructure, especially storage and processing. The fundamental product is the infrastructure itself; customers are other businesses.

**Industrial platforms** — Siemens, General Electric in earlier iterations, increasingly Chinese firms. Embed sensors and software in industrial equipment to extract data and sell predictive services.

**Product platforms** — Spotify, Netflix. Rent access to products that historically would have been purchased outright.

**Lean platforms** — Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash, TaskRabbit. Position themselves as intermediaries connecting service providers with customers, while owning little of the underlying capital and bearing little of the operational risk.

The defining feature of platforms is *network effects*: each new user increases the value of the platform for all other users, which produces tendencies toward monopoly. Once a critical mass of buyers and sellers is on one platform, it becomes very hard for competitors to enter, even with a technically better product — so platform markets tend toward winner-take-all dynamics.

The political-economic consequences:

**Massive accumulation of wealth in a small number of firms and individuals.** The market capitalization of the top five U.S. tech firms — Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Nvidia — exceeds the GDP of all but a handful of countries.

**Data as a new form of property.** Personal data — purchase history, location, social connections, biometrics — has become an enormously valuable asset class. The economic regulation of this asset is still being worked out. The European Union has been the most aggressive regulator through the General Data Protection Regulation (2018) and subsequent regulations. The U.S. has been substantially more permissive.

**A new kind of labor relation.** Platform workers — Uber drivers, food delivery couriers, Amazon Mechanical Turk task workers — occupy a contested status. The platforms classify them as independent contractors, entitled to no employment protections; the workers and many regulators classify them as effectively employees. The legal battles over this classification have run through every major jurisdiction.

**Surveillance.** The Harvard scholar Shoshana Zuboff, in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019), made the most influential argument that platform capitalism represents a qualitatively new economic form, organized around the extraction and commodification of human behavioral data on a scale and at a granularity unprecedented in human history. Whether or not one accepts Zuboff's specific framing, the empirical phenomenon — the enormous expansion of personal data collection and its commercial exploitation — is uncontested.

## Precarity

Through the 1960s and 1970s, the typical employment relation in advanced economies was a long-term, full-time job with a single employer, with benefits (health insurance, pension, paid leave), a clear career path, and substantial legal protection against arbitrary dismissal. The job was, in Erik Olin Wright's term, a *career*, not just employment.

Over the past forty years, this arrangement has been substantially eroded. The phenomenon is captured by the broad term *precarity*: employment that is part-time, temporary, intermittent, contingent on uncertain market conditions, or carried out under platforms or contractor arrangements that strip out the protections of standard employment.

Some indicators:

- The share of U.S. workers in "alternative work arrangements" — independent contractors, on-call workers, temporary help agency workers, contract company workers — rose from 10.7 percent in 2005 to 15.8 percent by 2015 (Katz and Krueger, 2016)
- The share of U.S. workers without employer-provided health insurance rose substantially through the 2000s and 2010s
- Defined-benefit pensions — guaranteeing retirees a specified income — have been largely replaced by defined-contribution plans (401(k)s, IRAs) that shift investment risk onto the worker
- The proportion of jobs requiring formal credentials has grown faster than the proportion that genuinely require those skills — what the economist Peter Capelli has called *credential inflation*
- Unionization rates in the U.S. private sector have fallen below 7 percent

The British sociologist Guy Standing, in *The Precariat* (2011), argued that the precarious workforce constituted a new class — not a proletariat in Marx's classical sense, because precarious workers have no stable identity as workers in a particular industry, and not a middle class, because their economic position is fundamentally insecure. Standing's framework has been influential and contested. The British anthropologist David Graeber's *Bullshit Jobs* (2018) added another dimension: a large share of contemporary white-collar jobs are, by their own holders' admission, socially pointless — managerial, administrative, and clerical positions that produce nothing the workers themselves regard as useful.

The political consequences of widespread precarity are still being worked out. The political theorist Wolfgang Streeck has argued that precarity disorganizes the working class politically, making collective action harder and democratic political voice weaker. The contemporary unionization drives at Amazon, Starbucks, and Apple in the United States, and the broader resurgence of union organizing internationally, suggest the conclusion is not foregone.

## The climate crisis

If late capitalism has one defining contradiction, it is the climate crisis. The basic facts:

- Atmospheric CO2 concentrations have risen from roughly 280 parts per million in 1850 to over 420 ppm in 2024
- Global average surface temperatures have risen by approximately 1.2°C above the pre-industrial baseline
- Current trajectories, even with announced national commitments, point to warming of 2.4–2.9°C by 2100
- The economic damage from climate change is large and accumulating, with estimates ranging widely depending on warming pathway

The political-economic implications:

**The market does not price externalities.** Carbon emissions impose costs on the global future that are not reflected in current market prices. The standard economic solution — a carbon tax that internalizes the externality — has been politically difficult to implement at scales sufficient to address the problem. Where it has been implemented (the European Emissions Trading System, British Columbia's carbon tax), the politics have been difficult and the prices generally too low.

**State action is necessary, but states are constrained.** A serious climate transition requires sustained, large-scale state action: industrial policy directing investment toward decarbonized energy, transportation, and buildings; regulation phasing out fossil fuel use; international coordination to avoid free-rider problems. The neoliberal program of the past forty years has weakened the state's capacity for exactly this kind of action.

**The polluter-pays problem.** Fossil fuel companies — Exxon, Chevron, BP, Shell, Saudi Aramco, the Chinese state oil companies — have produced enormous historical emissions while accumulating enormous profits. Forcing them to internalize the historical costs would substantially reduce their valuations, with knock-on effects on the financial system that has those valuations on its books.

**The just transition problem.** Phasing out fossil fuels would devastate communities and workers in extractive industries. A just transition would compensate them and reorient them toward replacement employment, but the political mechanisms for delivering on this commitment have been weak.

**The geopolitical complication.** Climate policy intersects with great-power competition. The U.S. Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, which committed roughly $370 billion to clean energy investment, was framed as much as competition with China as as climate policy. The European Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, beginning in 2026, will impose carbon prices on imports — a measure that some developing countries view as protectionist.

Several theorists have argued that climate change requires a fundamental rethinking of political economy. *Degrowth* — associated with thinkers like Jason Hickel, Giorgos Kallis, and Kohei Saito — argues that continued GDP growth is incompatible with planetary boundaries and that advanced economies need to plan for stable or declining production while maintaining welfare. *Eco-socialism* combines socialist economic restructuring with climate priorities. *Green New Deal* programs combine industrial policy with social provision in the tradition of the original New Deal.

Whether any of these frameworks can produce political traction at the scale required is the central political question of the next two decades. The 2024 election cycle in the United States substantially set back the federal climate program; whether other countries can pick up the slack is open.

## What contemporary political economy is asking

A few of the major analytical projects active in the 2020s:

**Mariana Mazzucato** and the entrepreneurial state. *The Entrepreneurial State* (2013) documented the substantial state role in funding the basic research underlying every major modern technology — the iPhone's touchscreen, the internet itself, GPS, voice recognition. Mazzucato argues for a more assertive state role in directing investment toward public missions (climate, health, equitable growth) and for capturing a fair share of the returns for the public.

**Brett Christophers** and rentier capitalism. *Rentier Capitalism* (2020) and subsequent works document how an increasing share of corporate profits in advanced economies derives from rent — on real estate, on intellectual property, on platforms, on regulatory privileges — rather than from productive investment. The analysis updates Ricardo's nineteenth-century theory of rent for the twenty-first-century economy.

**Thomas Piketty** and inequality. *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2013) established that the share of wealth held by the very top has risen substantially since the 1980s and is approaching levels not seen since the Belle Époque. *Capital and Ideology* (2019) extended the historical sweep. Piketty's empirical work has reshaped the global debate on inequality.

**Wendy Brown** and the de-democratization of economic decision-making. *Undoing the Demos* (2015) argues that neoliberalism has progressively transferred economic decisions out of democratic politics and into the hands of technocratic and market institutions, with substantial corrosive effects on democratic life.

**Cédric Durand** and digital feudalism. *Techno-féodalisme* (2020) and *Fictitious Capital* (2014) argue that contemporary platform capitalism is starting to resemble feudal relations in important ways — rents extracted by platform owners from dependent users, monopoly enforcement, weakened mobility. The "tech-feudalism" framework has been adopted by several other contemporary theorists.

**Yanis Varoufakis** and *Technofeudalism* (2023) makes a similar argument from a different angle: that the giant tech platforms represent a transition out of capitalism into something new and worse — a system organized around digital rents rather than productive investment.

**Daniela Gabor** and the *critical macrofinance* tradition. Documents how central bank policy, especially since 2008, has progressively become the foundation of asset valuations across the global economy, and what this means for democratic governance and financial stability.

## Some questions political economy does not yet have answers to

A few of the questions political economy in the 2020s is still working on:

- What is the political-economic significance of artificial intelligence? Does it represent a continuation of the labor-displacing technologies that capitalism has produced throughout its history, or something qualitatively new?
- Can advanced economies sustain their current levels of welfare provision under low population growth and aging demographics?
- Is the U.S. dollar's role as the global reserve currency stable, and what are the consequences of its decline?
- Is the Chinese model — state-managed capitalism with a single-party state — a viable long-term alternative to liberal-democratic capitalism, or a transitional form that will face its own crises?
- Can democratic political institutions, designed for nineteenth- and twentieth-century industrial economies, adequately govern twenty-first-century financialized, globalized, platformed economies?
- What happens to political economy when the climate transformation makes some current economic activities untenable and requires new ones at scale?
- Is the historical pattern of capitalist crisis followed by reform (1873, 1929, 2008) repeating, or has the political response capacity been so eroded that the next crisis will produce simply collapse rather than reform?

These are not questions this course can answer — they are questions you should be able to formulate clearly, which is what "near-expert" means at the close of this unit.
