In October 1917 (November by the Western calendar), a small revolutionary party called the Bolsheviks seized state power in Petrograd. Within five years, it controlled the largest country on earth. Within thirty years, its model — a one-party state, central planning, collectivized agriculture, mass industrialization — had been imposed on Eastern Europe, exported to China, and become the alternative reference point for every political and economic argument on earth.

This module covers the Russian Revolution and the early Soviet state up to roughly Lenin's death in 1924. The next module covers Stalin and the planned economy. The structure matters: Lenin's revolution and Stalin's state are continuous in some ways and sharply discontinuous in others, and you should know which is which.

## Russia in 1917

Russia in 1917 was, by Marxist standards, not a candidate for proletarian revolution. It was a vast agrarian empire. Around eighty percent of its population were peasants. Its industrial sector was modern in scale but concentrated in a few cities — Petrograd, Moscow, the Donbass, Baku. Industrial workers numbered perhaps three to four million out of a total population of around 175 million. Literacy was below thirty percent. Most of the country lived under conditions that had not changed substantially since serfdom was abolished in 1861.

Russia was, however, a country in catastrophe. The First World War had been disastrous for Russia. Roughly two million soldiers were dead by 1917. Several million more were prisoners or deserting. The army was disintegrating. Food shortages in the cities had produced bread riots. The Tsarist regime, exhausted and incompetent, had lost the support of nearly every important constituency.

In February 1917 (March by the Western calendar), a spontaneous uprising in Petrograd — beginning with women textile workers protesting bread queues and quickly joined by mutinous troops — toppled the Tsar within five days. Nicholas II abdicated. A Provisional Government was formed by liberal and moderate-socialist parties. Power was theoretically held by this government but was, in practice, divided with the *Soviets* — workers' and soldiers' councils that had sprung up in Petrograd, Moscow, and other cities. The Soviet of Workers' and Soldiers' Deputies in Petrograd had effective veto power over the government's decisions.

This dual-power arrangement lasted from February to October. The Provisional Government attempted to continue the war and to defer land reform until after the war. Both decisions destroyed it. The army would not fight. The peasants would not wait. By the autumn, the Provisional Government had lost control of the country.

## Lenin's framework

V. I. Lenin (1870–1924) was the leader of the Bolsheviks — the more radical faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, which had split in 1903 over a question that looked technical at the time (the criteria for party membership) but turned out to be foundational. The Bolsheviks (the "majority," though they were not always the actual majority) favored a small, disciplined, professional revolutionary party. The Mensheviks (the "minority") favored a broader, more democratic mass party along Western European social-democratic lines.

Lenin's theoretical contributions to Marxism were substantial:

**What Is to Be Done? (1902)** — argued that the working class, left to itself, would only develop *trade union consciousness* — a desire for better wages and conditions — not revolutionary consciousness. Revolutionary politics required a *vanguard party* of professional revolutionaries to bring revolutionary theory to the workers from outside. This was a controversial doctrine within Marxism. Critics (including Luxemburg) argued that it set up the conditions for an authoritarian party-state to substitute itself for the working class. The criticism aged well.

**Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism (1916)** — built on Hilferding and Hobson; argued that the war then raging was a structural consequence of capitalism in its monopoly phase, as advanced capitalist countries fought over markets, colonies, and raw materials. Lenin's analysis treated the war not as a moral failure of European socialism but as the predictable result of capitalist contradictions reaching a global scale.

**State and Revolution (1917)** — written during Lenin's brief exile in Finland in summer 1917; argued that the revolution must not merely capture the existing state but *smash* it and replace it with new institutions of working-class self-government, based on the Paris Commune model. This was Lenin's most radically democratic text, written just before he took state power. The Lenin who actually ran Russia after October would govern in ways that contradicted much of *State and Revolution*.

**April Theses (1917)** — Lenin's program when he returned to Russia from Swiss exile in April 1917: all power to the Soviets, immediate peace with Germany, distribution of land to the peasants, nationalization of banking. The slogan "Peace, Land, Bread" captured the program.

The Bolsheviks were a small party in February 1917 — perhaps 24,000 members in a country of 175 million. By October they had grown to several hundred thousand and held majorities in the Soviets of Petrograd and Moscow. The political situation had radicalized in their favor. The Provisional Government's failure to end the war, distribute land, or feed the cities had discredited every other left option.

## October 1917

On October 25 (November 7), 1917, the Bolsheviks staged an armed uprising in Petrograd, occupied government buildings, and arrested the ministers of the Provisional Government. The Second All-Russian Congress of Soviets, meeting that night, ratified the action and declared Soviet power.

The actual seizure was almost bloodless in Petrograd. The Provisional Government had no defenders. But the consolidation of Bolshevik power across Russia took years. The Constituent Assembly — elected in November 1917 in the freest election in Russian history to that point — gave the Bolsheviks only twenty-four percent of the vote (the peasant Socialist Revolutionaries won outright). When the Assembly met in January 1918, the Bolsheviks dispersed it by force after a single day. From that point forward, Soviet Russia was a one-party state.

The civil war that followed (1918–1922) was the formative experience of the Soviet regime. White Armies funded and supplied by Britain, France, the United States, Japan, and others fought to restore the old order. Foreign intervention forces landed in Murmansk, Arkhangelsk, Vladivostok, and the Caucasus. The Bolsheviks built the Red Army from scratch under Trotsky and won. The cost was enormous: perhaps seven to twelve million deaths from fighting, disease, and famine; the destruction of Russian industry and agriculture; the political habits of secrecy, central command, and ruthless internal discipline that would become defining features of the Soviet state.

## War Communism

During the civil war, the Bolsheviks improvised an emergency economic system that became known as *War Communism*. Its main features:

- Nationalization of all industry, large and small
- Forced grain requisitioning from peasants (*prodrazverstka*) — peasants surrendered all surplus grain to the state at zero or near-zero prices
- Suppression of private trade — markets were illegal
- Allocation of consumer goods to urban populations through state-managed ration cards
- Mass mobilization of labor through "labor armies" under military discipline

War Communism was an economic disaster. Industrial production collapsed to a fraction of its prewar level. Famine ravaged the countryside — partly from drought, mostly from the destruction of agriculture under requisitioning. By 1921, a major peasant uprising had spread across the Tambov region and a sailors' revolt at the Kronstadt naval base demanded "Soviets without Communists." The sailors, who had been the Bolsheviks' strongest supporters in 1917, were suppressed with massive force.

Lenin recognized the crisis. At the Tenth Party Congress in March 1921 — even as Kronstadt was being assaulted — he introduced the *New Economic Policy* (NEP).

## The New Economic Policy

NEP was a retreat. Forced grain requisitioning was replaced by a tax in kind; peasants could sell surplus grain on a restored market. Small private businesses — shops, small workshops, cafés — were legalized again. The state retained the "commanding heights" — heavy industry, banking, foreign trade, transport — but allowed a substantial private sector to operate at the small-scale level.

NEP worked. By 1926–27, Soviet agricultural and industrial production had recovered to roughly pre-war levels. Famine receded. A cautious normalization began. The Russian intelligentsia, the urban middle class, and the peasantry breathed.

NEP also produced a new class of small entrepreneurs — *Nepmen* — and a recovering peasantry, especially the more prosperous farmers known as *kulaks*. From the Bolshevik perspective, this was a problem: the revolutionary state was now coexisting with a partial capitalist restoration. Lenin himself, before his death in January 1924, called NEP a "strategic retreat" and remained unclear about its long-term place.

## Lenin's death and the succession

Lenin suffered a series of strokes in 1922 and 1923 and was effectively incapacitated for the last year and a half of his life. Before he died in January 1924, he dictated several letters to the party congress (the "Lenin Testament") in which he assessed the leading Bolsheviks. The assessment was unsparing.

About Joseph Stalin, the party's General Secretary, Lenin wrote:

> "Comrade Stalin, having become Secretary-General, has unlimited authority concentrated in his hands, and I am not sure whether he will always be capable of using that authority with sufficient caution. ... Stalin is too rude, and this fault, although tolerable in dealings among us Communists, becomes intolerable in a Secretary-General. That is why I suggest the comrades think about a way to remove Stalin from that post."

The Testament was suppressed. Stalin remained in office. He outmaneuvered every rival — Trotsky, Zinoviev, Kamenev, Bukharin — over the next five years. By 1929, he had consolidated unchallenged power.

The Lenin who died in 1924 had presided over the founding of a workers' state through revolution and civil war, the brutal expediencies of War Communism, and the partial retreat of NEP. The Lenin remembered in Soviet propaganda was the iconic founder of a doctrine. The Lenin of the historical record was a more complicated figure — brilliant, ruthless, theoretically agile, often wrong, and only sporadically in control of the events his party set in motion.

## What the Russian Revolution accomplished

By 1924, the Soviet state had:

- Survived the most concerted foreign and civil-war assault any new regime has ever faced
- Distributed land to roughly 100 million peasants — one of the largest land redistributions in history
- Nationalized industry, banking, and foreign trade
- Established universal literacy programs that would lift literacy from below 30 percent in 1917 to roughly 80 percent by 1939
- Granted formal equal rights to women in marriage, divorce, abortion, and the workplace — far ahead of any Western country
- Built a one-party state with a secret police (the Cheka, later GPU, later NKVD, later KGB) that had already, in its founding years, executed tens of thousands of opponents

The combination was unstable. The egalitarian and emancipatory dimensions sat in increasing tension with the authoritarian and centralizing ones — a tension Lenin had managed through a combination of theoretical agility and personal authority that had no obvious successor, and that the next decade would resolve in the most catastrophic possible direction.

## Figures to know

- **V. I. Lenin** (Vladimir Ilyich Ulyanov, 1870–1924) — Bolshevik leader
- **Leon Trotsky** (Lev Davidovich Bronstein, 1879–1940) — Commissar of Foreign Affairs; commander of the Red Army; later Stalin's defeated rival; assassinated in Mexico in 1940
- **Nadezhda Krupskaya** (1869–1939) — revolutionary; Lenin's wife; commissar of education; the architect of Soviet mass literacy programs
- **Alexandra Kollontai** (1872–1952) — Commissar of Social Welfare; the only woman in Lenin's first cabinet; pioneering theorist of socialist feminism, family, and sexuality
- **Nikolai Bukharin** (1888–1938) — Bolshevik theorist; major advocate of NEP; executed in Stalin's purges
- **Felix Dzerzhinsky** (1877–1926) — founder and head of the Cheka (the original Soviet secret police)
- **Mensheviks** — the moderate-socialist wing of Russian social democracy that opposed the Bolshevik seizure of power
- **Socialist Revolutionaries** — the agrarian socialist party that received the largest single share of votes in the November 1917 Constituent Assembly election

## Reflection prompt

The Bolsheviks dispersed the Constituent Assembly in January 1918 after losing the election. Marx had assumed that proletarian revolution would occur in countries where the proletariat formed the majority. Russia in 1917 was overwhelmingly peasant. How does this fact change the moral and political assessment of the Bolshevik action? Write a paragraph defending and a paragraph attacking the dispersal of the Constituent Assembly.
