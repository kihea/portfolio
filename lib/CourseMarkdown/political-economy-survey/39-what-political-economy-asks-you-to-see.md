### CHAPTER 1 — THE FOUR QUESTIONS — 1:00–5:00

[On screen: a clean stack of four lines of text appearing one at a time.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Everything in the course can be reduced to four questions. Memorize them — they are everything.

*Who works?* The first question is the easiest. Who labors to produce the food, the goods, the services, the care that the society depends on? In a feudal manor, the serfs work. In a Roman *latifundium*, the slaves work. In a modern economy, the wage workers work — at all levels of the wage scale. The question is more interesting than it sounds, because much of what is needed for a society to function — child care, elder care, domestic labor, voluntary community work — happens outside the wage economy and is therefore often invisible in the statistics.

*Who owns?* The second question. Who has legally recognized claim over the means of production — the land, the buildings, the machinery, the data, the platforms? In a feudal society, the lords own. In a capitalist society, the capitalists own. In a Soviet society, the state owns (and the party controls the state). The form of ownership shapes the form of distribution.

*Who decides?* The third question. Who decides what gets produced, how, where, in what quantities, for whose benefit? Markets distribute these decisions through prices. Plans concentrate them in administrators. Custom locks them into inherited routines. Almost every economy combines all three; the question is *which decisions get made how*.

*Who eats?* The final question, and the harshest. Who gets the food, the housing, the medical care, the education, the security? Who gets a lot? Who gets enough? Who gets too little? Who gets none at all? The distribution does not have to match production or ownership. It frequently does not.

You ask these four questions and you have political economy. Everything else is detail.

### CHAPTER 2 — WHAT THE COURSE WAS REALLY ABOUT — 5:00–10:00

[On screen: dissolves between course images — a Mesopotamian tablet, a feudal manor, an industrial mill, a Soviet poster, a glass financial tower, a present-day delivery worker.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The course had a structure. We started before capitalism — with hunter-gatherer subsistence, with agrarian tribute, with feudalism. We did this not to romanticize earlier systems but to demonstrate that capitalism is not the natural state of human economic life. It is a specific historical formation with a beginning, with conditions of possibility, with rules that had to be enforced, with people who had to be displaced. It can in principle be replaced by something else, though what replaces it has historically not been an improvement until the change is consolidated.

Then classical political economy — Smith, Malthus, Ricardo, Mill — the first sustained attempt to describe what capitalism was and how it worked. These thinkers got some things spectacularly right (the division of labor, comparative advantage, the rent mechanism) and some things spectacularly wrong (the iron law of wages, the universal applicability of free trade, the morality of treating land and labor as commodities). What they all had in common was that they took the system seriously as an object of study — which is the move political economy makes.

Then Marx. The most consequential critique of capitalism ever written. You do not have to be a Marxist to find his categories useful. The wage relation, surplus value, alienation, commodity fetishism, the falling rate of profit, primitive accumulation — every one of these concepts illuminates something you can still see in contemporary life, and most of them have not been improved upon by Marx's intellectual opponents in the past hundred and fifty years.

Then the twentieth century — the experiments. The Soviet Union, China under Mao, Yugoslavia under Tito, Cuba under Castro, the Nordic countries, Allende's Chile, Mao's China after Deng. These were real attempts to organize national economies on principles other than capitalist private accumulation. Some failed catastrophically. Some succeeded in important measures of human welfare. None produced a stable alternative that could outlive its founders or escape the gravitational pull of the global capitalist system.

Then the neoliberal counter-revolution. Hayek, Friedman, Thatcher, Reagan, Volcker, Pinochet's Chicago Boys, the Washington Consensus. A coherent, well-funded, internationally coordinated movement that dismantled much of the post-war social-democratic compromise and built the financialized, globalized, deregulated, unequal world most readers of this course were born into.

Then 2008. Then late capitalism — platforms, precarity, climate, the surveillance economy, the deaths of despair, the political fragmentation that has followed.

This is the arc, and you now know it — though you also know it has not finished. You are still inside the story.

### CHAPTER 3 — WHAT WE DID NOT COVER — 10:00–13:30

[On screen: world map with regions and subjects briefly highlighted.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Forty hours is a lot. It is also not enough. Some honest acknowledgments of what we did not cover.

We did not cover the Islamic economic tradition — *al-mudaraba* contracts, *waqf* endowments, the medieval Cairo geniza records, the modern Islamic banking experiments. There is a substantial literature here, and you should read into it if you have any interest in the Middle East and North Africa, contemporary or historical.

We did not cover indigenous economies in depth — the gift economies of the Pacific Northwest, the redistributive systems of the Inca, the kin-based agriculture of the Iroquois, the Haida potlatch traditions. Each deserves more than the paragraph we gave them.

We did not cover sub-Saharan African political economy — the precolonial trading kingdoms (Mali, Songhai, Ashanti), the colonial extractive economies, the post-independence developmental states, the resource curse, the contemporary tensions between Chinese investment and Western conditionality. This is a major omission.

We did not cover South Asian political economy in detail — the Mughal land system, the East India Company, the colonial drain, the Nehruvian planning experiment, the post-1991 liberalization, the contemporary Indian state-capital-labor relationship. India is now the world's most populous country and will increasingly be central to global political economy.

We did not give Japan and Korea, the East Asian developmental states, the depth they deserve. The model that produced South Korea's transformation from one of the world's poorest countries in 1960 to a high-income economy by 2000 is not a Soviet-style plan and not an Anglo-American market and deserves its own treatment.

We did not give feminist political economy and the analysis of unpaid reproductive labor anywhere near the space it deserves. Silvia Federici, Selma James, Mariarosa Dalla Costa, Maria Mies, Marilyn Waring — there is a body of work here that revises classical, Marxist, and mainstream economics in important ways, and one paragraph in this course does not do it justice.

We did not give ecological economics, beyond the climate discussion. Herman Daly, Kate Raworth, the degrowth tradition, indigenous land-relationship frameworks — there is a different way of doing political economy that takes the biosphere as a fundamental constraint, and we did not develop it.

If you have the appetite for more, all of these are open doors.

### CHAPTER 4 — WHAT TO TAKE WITH YOU — 13:30–18:30

[On screen: clean text statements appearing in sequence.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Five things to take with you.

**One.** The economy is not natural. Every system you have studied was made — by laws, by force, by ideas, by accidents of history. Some parts of it look natural because they are very old or because they affect you below the threshold of conscious notice — but they are still made, and they can be remade.

**Two.** Every economy has a distribution problem. The question of who gets the surplus is the central political question of every society of more than a few hundred people. The answers societies have given to this question have varied enormously and have shaped almost everything else. If a society's answer to the distribution question is *the people who already have the most should also get the new growth*, you have one kind of society. If the answer is *the people who do the work should share in the rewards*, you have another. The answer is not given by nature but by politics.

**Three.** Theories serve interests. This is not the same as saying every theory is propaganda. Adam Smith was a serious thinker; so was Marx; so was Hayek. Each was also doing work for a particular position in the political-economic struggle of his time. When you read economic argument, ask not only *is the argument correct?* but also *what would be true about the world if the argument is taken up by policy?* Whose interests does the argument serve? Whose are subordinated? The argument may still be correct. But you should not be surprised when the people who advance it most aggressively are the people whose interests it most clearly serves.

**Four.** Watch what people do, not what they say. Friedman's libertarian rhetoric coexisted with his consulting for the Pinochet dictatorship. The Soviet rhetoric of workers' liberation coexisted with a state that murdered millions of workers. The neoliberal rhetoric of free markets has coexisted with the use of state power to defend incumbent firms, suppress unions, and bail out failed banks. The official story is one form of evidence; behavior is another. Where the two diverge, behavior wins.

**Five.** You are inside the system. You are not a neutral observer. Whatever you read about political economy is being read by someone with a job, a debt structure, a relationship to property, a position in the international hierarchy, a stake in current arrangements. The intellectual honesty of any good political economist is to be aware of where they are standing. Be aware of where you are standing. Then make your judgments anyway.

### CHAPTER 5 — WHAT HAPPENS NEXT — 18:30–22:00

[On screen: long shot, daylight, a present-day city street.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The course closes here, but you are not done. The capstone project waits in the next module.

The world you are walking back into has problems this course has equipped you to read. The hollowing-out of middle-class jobs in advanced economies. The platform economy's restructuring of work and information. The climate transition that has barely begun. The Chinese-American competition that will shape the international economy for the next several decades. The artificial intelligence transformation whose political economy is still being made. The aging populations of every wealthy country. The continuing housing crisis. The opioid crisis. The mental health crisis. The trust crisis. Every one of these has economic and political dimensions you can now see.

You will not be able to fix everything — nobody can. What you can do — and what the discipline asks of you — is *not be fooled*. About what is happening. About who is doing it. About why. About what alternatives have been rejected and on what grounds. About what the systems you live inside are actually for.

That is the equipment — it does not solve anything, but it changes what you are able to say and, eventually, what you are able to do. The history of political economy is partly a history of people who were not fooled, who said so publicly, who built movements out of the saying, who shifted the world a little, and who left the equipment in writing for the people who came next.

You are now one of those people. Whether you build anything with the equipment is your choice — and it is yours.

**Narrator (VO):**
A final word.

This was a long study, and you finished it — most people will not; most people will go through their lives without ever sitting down for forty hours with the actual arguments about how their world is organized. The frame is in your head now and will operate quietly. You will read a news story and see things the casual reader cannot. You will hear a political speech and recognize the arguments inside it from chapters you read last month. You will look at a paycheck and see a wage relation, not just a number. You will look at a shopping cart and see a commodity, not just a thing.

The reading is over. The seeing has begun. Now begin the capstone.
