The period between roughly 1500 and 1800 is the bridge. Feudalism had ended (in Western Europe; in the East it had hardened); industrial capitalism had not yet arrived. What filled the middle was a long, messy, state-driven economic order historians call *mercantilism*. By the end of it, the institutional skeleton of modern capitalism — wage labor, private property in land, joint-stock corporations, central banks, public debt — was in place. The factories came afterward.

## What "mercantilism" means

The word *mercantilism* was coined retrospectively, by Adam Smith in 1776, to describe a set of policies he wanted to discredit. Earlier writers had not called themselves mercantilists. They were merchants, statesmen, and pamphleteers — Thomas Mun, William Petty, Jean-Baptiste Colbert, Friedrich List — arguing for specific policies in specific national contexts.

What unified them, in retrospect, was a set of beliefs:

- **National wealth is measured in bullion** — gold and silver. A country grows richer by acquiring more of it.
- **Trade is zero-sum**. If France gains, England loses. Trade should therefore be conducted so that more bullion flows in than out.
- **State power and economic policy are inseparable**. The army, the navy, and the merchant fleet serve the same purpose: making the realm rich and strong.
- **Manufactures matter more than raw materials**. A country that exports finished cloth and imports raw wool grows rich; a country that exports raw wool and imports finished cloth grows poor. (Friedrich List, writing in the 1840s, will later turn this into a developmental doctrine for Germany.)
- **Domestic production should be protected** — by tariffs, by monopolies granted to favored companies, by colonial restrictions binding overseas territories to the home country's market.

Mercantilist policy made certain countries rich. Britain ran the system most successfully and emerged from the eighteenth century with a global empire and the world's most productive economy. Spain, which had begun with the largest bullion inflows in human history from the conquests of Mexico and Peru, spent it on wars and finished the period bankrupt. Mercantilism was a real political economy, with winners and losers.

## The enclosures

Of all the institutional changes that prepared the ground for capitalism, the *enclosures* in England matter most.

Recall from Module 5 that medieval peasants had customary rights to common land — pastures, woods, waste, meadows — used collectively under village rules. Beginning in the late fifteenth century and accelerating through the seventeenth and eighteenth, English landowners began consolidating these common lands into private estates, enclosed with hedges and fences, and converting them from mixed peasant agriculture to sheep pasture or to large commercial farms.

The process happened in two waves. The first, roughly 1450–1600, was driven by individual lords and was often illegal under existing law — common law protected customary rights. Sir Thomas More, in *Utopia* (1516), described the resulting displacement with a famous line: "Your sheep, that were wont to be so meek and tame, and so small eaters, now, as I hear say, be become so great devourers, and so wild, that they eat up and swallow down the very men themselves."

The second wave, roughly 1750–1850, was carried out by *Acts of Enclosure* — laws passed by Parliament, which by this point was dominated by exactly the landowners who stood to benefit. Between 1760 and 1830, more than four thousand parliamentary enclosure acts privatized roughly six million acres of English common land. Peasants who had held customary rights for generations were dispossessed. Some received small parcels in compensation; most did not. Those who refused to leave could be jailed.

The displaced peasantry had to go somewhere — and they went to the cities. The labor force that would staff the textile factories of Manchester, Birmingham, and Leeds was created, in large part, by this dispossession.

This is one of the most important facts in the history of political economy, and it is often left out of textbook accounts. *Capitalism's labor market was deliberately manufactured.* The "free" worker who voluntarily accepts a wage in nineteenth-century England was free in a very specific sense: free of land, free of customary rights, free of subsistence, and therefore *compelled* by the threat of starvation to accept whatever wage the factory owner offered.

Karl Polanyi, whom we have already met, made this the centerpiece of *The Great Transformation*:

> "Such precisely was the arrangement under a market system. Man under the name of labor, nature under the name of land, were made available for sale; the use of labor power could be universally bought and sold at a price called wages, and the use of land could be negotiated for a price called rent."

The making of these "fictitious commodities" required laws, courts, and police. It did not happen by itself.

## The colonial system

The other engine of the mercantilist period was the European overseas empire.

Beginning with the Portuguese exploration of the African coast in the fifteenth century and the Spanish conquest of the Americas in the sixteenth, European states extracted enormous wealth from the Americas, Africa, and Asia. The mechanisms varied:

**Bullion extraction** — Spanish silver mined from Potosí (in modern Bolivia) and Zacatecas (in modern Mexico) flowed across the Atlantic in such volumes that it triggered the *Price Revolution* in Europe — a century of sustained inflation as the gold and silver supply expanded faster than the goods it chased. Between 1500 and 1650, prices in Europe rose roughly fivefold.

**Plantation slavery** — sugar, tobacco, cotton, and coffee, grown for European consumption on plantations worked by enslaved Africans. The transatlantic slave trade forcibly transported an estimated 12.5 million people across the Atlantic between 1500 and 1866. Slavery in the Americas was different from Roman slavery in important ways: it was *racial* (legally restricted to people of African descent), it was *hereditary* (the children of slaves were born slaves), and it was *chattel* (slaves were legally classified as movable property, like livestock, with no recognized kinship rights).

**Joint-stock companies** — the Dutch East India Company (founded 1602) and the English East India Company (founded 1600) were quasi-public corporations granted state-backed monopolies over trade with particular regions. They had their own armies, conducted their own wars, and ruled their own territories. The English East India Company effectively governed Bengal from 1757 until 1858. These companies were the prototypes of the modern multinational corporation.

**Triangular trade** — manufactured goods from Europe to Africa, enslaved people from Africa to the Americas, and raw plantation goods from the Americas back to Europe. Each leg generated profit. The system tied together the European bourgeoisie, the African slave-trading kingdoms (often coastal states that warred with their interior neighbors to capture slaves for sale), and the Americas plantations.

Eric Williams, in *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944), made the case that the profits from the Atlantic slave trade were a major source of capital for the British Industrial Revolution. The Williams thesis has been debated by economic historians for eight decades — some have argued the profits were smaller than Williams estimated — but recent quantitative work has tended to support his basic claim. Whatever the exact share, slavery and the slave trade were not incidental to capitalism's origin. They were one of its formative conditions.

## The financial revolution

A third set of institutional changes in the same period laid the groundwork for capitalist finance.

**The Bank of England** was founded in 1694 to lend money to William III's government, which needed cash to fight France. The bank received the right to issue paper notes redeemable in gold, the right to manage the government's debt, and a profitable position at the center of London's financial markets. It became the prototype of every later central bank.

**Public debt** at scale — the British state began routinely borrowing from the bond market, using the secure tax base of an increasingly commercial empire as collateral. This was a financial innovation: it allowed the state to spend more than it taxed in any given year, paying back over time. The British Empire was, among other things, a fiscal-military experiment that succeeded because Britain could borrow on better terms than France or Spain. Wars are expensive, which is why the country that can borrow cheaply wins them.

**Stock exchanges** — the Amsterdam stock exchange opened in 1602 to trade shares of the Dutch East India Company. The London exchange grew up in coffeehouses in the late seventeenth century. Speculative bubbles — the Dutch tulip mania of 1637, the South Sea Bubble of 1720 — were already part of the new financial landscape.

**Insurance** — Lloyd's of London originated in a coffeehouse in 1688 as a place where merchants could insure shipping against loss. Marine insurance allowed long-distance trade to scale by spreading risk across many investors.

By 1750, the basic financial infrastructure of modern capitalism existed in London and Amsterdam: a central bank, a national debt market, a stock exchange, joint-stock corporations, marine insurance, and bills of exchange that allowed merchants to transfer credit across continents. Industrial production had not yet exploded, but the machinery for organizing it had.

## What the period prepared

By the time Adam Smith published *The Wealth of Nations* in 1776, the institutional architecture of capitalism was largely in place:

- A landless wage-labor force (created by the enclosures)
- Private property in land, defended by the state
- Long-distance trade networks operated by joint-stock corporations
- A financial system capable of mobilizing capital at scale
- Colonial markets for finished goods and sources of raw materials
- A state apparatus willing to coordinate all of the above

What was *not* yet in place was the steam engine, the factory, the railway, and the urban industrial proletariat at scale. Those would arrive over the next eighty years. They arrived, however, into a world that had been remade to receive them.

## Polanyi's central claim, once more

This module is largely a sustained argument for one of Karl Polanyi's central claims: that the market economy was not a natural development but a political construction, requiring sustained state action, displacement of customary rights, and frequently violence. The "free market" did not free itself — it was freed, for capital, from the constraints of common land, custom, and corporate guild restrictions — by states acting on behalf of the rising commercial classes.

You need only acknowledge what the historical record shows to find this useful. Adam Smith, in 1776, will write as if the system he describes is the natural state of things. The next module reads him carefully. But you should now be able to ask the right question while reading: *what state action was required to make this "natural" system possible, and who paid for it?*

## Figures to know

- **Karl Polanyi** (1886–1964) — *The Great Transformation* (1944). The central argument that this module rests on.
- **Eric Williams** (1911–1981) — Trinidadian historian and later prime minister of Trinidad and Tobago; *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944).
- **Jean-Baptiste Colbert** (1619–1683) — Louis XIV's finance minister; the most famous architect of practical mercantilist policy.
- **Friedrich List** (1789–1846) — German economist; argued that protectionist mercantilism was the right path for developing economies, against Smith's free-trade orthodoxy.
- **Sir Thomas More** (1478–1535) — *Utopia*, the early protest against the enclosures.
- **Sven Beckert** (b. 1965) — historian; *Empire of Cotton* (2014), the leading recent work on how the cotton trade tied slavery, colonial labor, and industrial capitalism into a single global system.
