Marx died in 1883. Engels lived twelve more years, editing *Capital* Volumes II and III from Marx's notebooks, and serving as the elder statesman of the international socialist movement. After Engels died in 1895, leadership of the movement passed to a generation of theorists and political organizers who had to translate Marx's framework into a political program for actual workers' parties in actual European countries. The arguments they had with each other shaped twentieth-century left politics more than the arguments Marx had with the bourgeois economists.

This module covers the post-Marx generation up to roughly 1920. The next module covers the Frankfurt School and the cultural turn in Western Marxism between the wars.

## Engels: the indispensable second author

A note about Engels before we move on. Friedrich Engels (1820–1895) was not, as he is sometimes presented, a junior partner who happened to be in the right place to fund Marx's work. He was a co-author, a major theorist in his own right, and the editor without whom the bulk of Marx's writing would not exist as we have it.

Engels's *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845) was a foundational empirical study of industrial Manchester that shaped Marx's understanding of capitalism long before Marx had developed the theory of *Capital*. Engels's *Anti-Dühring* (1878) was the most read introduction to Marxism in the late nineteenth century. *The Origin of the Family, Private Property, and the State* (1884) extended historical materialism to the analysis of family forms and gender — a starting point for socialist feminist analysis. *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* (1880) defined the difference between the early socialists and the Marxian tradition.

Engels also did the unglamorous work. After Marx's death, he spent ten years deciphering Marx's nearly illegible handwriting and reconstructing *Capital* Volumes II and III from notebooks Marx had left in various states of disorder. The published versions of those volumes are largely Engels's editorial labor. Without him, we would have only Volume I.

For some twentieth-century Marxists, this matters because they argue Engels misrepresented Marx — that Engels's positivist scientism (treating Marxism as a "scientific" theory analogous to physics) flattens out the dialectical complexity of Marx's actual thinking. The Marxian scholar Maximilien Rubel made this case in the 1950s, and the so-called *Marx-Engels question* has been revisited many times since. The argument is real. For an introduction, however, what matters is that Engels was a serious thinker in his own right, and that the version of Marxism that entered twentieth-century politics was significantly Engels's reconstruction.

## The Second International

After Marx's death, the international socialist movement reconstituted itself as the Second International — a loose federation of national workers' parties that met periodically and tried to coordinate political strategy. The dominant party was the *Sozialdemokratische Partei Deutschlands* (SPD), the German Social Democratic Party. By 1912, the SPD was the largest party in the German Reichstag, with over four million voters and a sophisticated network of newspapers, schools, libraries, cooperatives, and labor unions.

The SPD was officially Marxist. Its 1891 Erfurt Program, drafted by Karl Kautsky, was a statement of orthodox Marxism. The Erfurt Program had two parts: a maximum program (the eventual transition to socialism through the overthrow of capitalism) and a minimum program (concrete reforms the party would pursue immediately — universal suffrage, an eight-hour day, free public education, abolition of standing armies, taxation reform).

The relationship between the maximum and minimum programs would become the central question for the SPD and for European socialism more broadly. Was the minimum program a stepping stone to revolution, or was it the whole program — and did revolution itself recede into a vague horizon?

## Eduard Bernstein and revisionism

The most consequential theoretical challenge to orthodox Marxism came from within the SPD itself. Eduard Bernstein (1850–1932) was a senior party intellectual and, for many years, Engels's literary executor. Between 1896 and 1898 he wrote a series of essays — published in book form as *Evolutionary Socialism* (German: *Die Voraussetzungen des Sozialismus*, 1899) — that argued Marx's predictions had not, in fact, come true.

Bernstein's specific claims:

— Capitalism had not concentrated wealth in fewer and fewer hands. The number of small property holders was *increasing*, not decreasing.

— The working class was not becoming uniformly poorer. Real wages were rising in industrialized countries; living standards were improving.

— Crises were not becoming more severe. The credit system and modern corporate organization were stabilizing capitalism, not destabilizing it.

— Therefore: the revolutionary collapse of capitalism that Marx had predicted was not arriving. Socialism would have to be achieved through *gradual reform* — winning elections, building unions, expanding the welfare state, democratizing the economy step by step.

— "The final goal," Bernstein wrote, "whatever it may be, is nothing; the movement is everything."

Bernstein's argument was an empirical one. He had looked at the German economy and the actual trajectory of working-class life over twenty-five years and concluded that the Marxian script was not playing out. He wanted to align the party's theory with what was actually happening, which was the slow growth of trade unions, cooperatives, social insurance, and the welfare state. He did not want to abandon socialism; he wanted to redefine the path to it.

The orthodox Marxists in the SPD — led by Karl Kautsky and the Russian exile Georgi Plekhanov — denounced Bernstein as a "revisionist." Bernstein's analysis, they argued, mistook a temporary capitalist stabilization for a permanent one. Crises would return. Concentration would resume. The reformist path was a trap that would absorb the workers' movement into the existing order without changing it.

Kautsky's response to Bernstein, the *Erfurt Program* commentary expanded in *The Class Struggle* and other works, attempted to hold a middle position: maintain the revolutionary maximum program while pursuing reform in the short term. This *centrist Marxism* was the official position of the SPD up to the First World War.

## Rosa Luxemburg: revolution from the left

The most powerful critique of Bernstein from the left came from Rosa Luxemburg (1871–1919), a Polish-born Jewish socialist who had moved to Germany to participate in the SPD. Luxemburg's *Reform or Revolution?* (1900) was a direct response to Bernstein.

Luxemburg argued that the reforms Bernstein was building toward could not, in themselves, transform capitalism. Trade unions, welfare benefits, electoral participation — these were genuine gains for the working class, but they were *defensive*. They mitigated the worst effects of capitalism without challenging its underlying structure. A socialism built only of reforms, Luxemburg argued, would simply be a more humane capitalism. The revolutionary transformation of property relations required revolutionary politics — the seizure of state power by the proletariat in a moment of crisis.

Luxemburg's broader contributions to Marxist theory were considerable. *The Accumulation of Capital* (1913) was an attempt to explain why capitalism required imperialism — the constant search for new markets, raw materials, and labor in non-capitalist regions. Capitalism, Luxemburg argued, could not be a closed system; it required an outside, a periphery to extract from. The expansion of European empires into Africa and Asia in the late nineteenth century was not an aberration; it was a structural requirement of the system.

Luxemburg was murdered in 1919 by right-wing paramilitaries during the failed Spartacus uprising in Berlin. Her body was thrown into the Landwehr Canal. She was forty-seven. She is one of the most important political theorists the twentieth century produced and one of its most consequential martyrs.

A Luxemburg sentence often quoted: "Freedom is always, exclusively, the freedom of the one who thinks differently." She was writing about the early Soviet state. The line was directed at Lenin.

## Karl Kautsky: the centrist

Karl Kautsky (1854–1938) was for decades the most influential Marxist theorist in Europe — Engels's chosen successor, editor of *Die Neue Zeit*, the leading socialist theoretical journal, and the principal author of the SPD's program. Lenin in his early career called Kautsky a "renegade" only after they fell out over the war and the Russian Revolution; before that, Kautsky was the gold standard of Marxist orthodoxy.

Kautsky's position was complicated. He defended orthodox Marxism against Bernstein's revisionism. He also defended *parliamentary democracy* against the Bolshevik program of one-party rule. After the Russian Revolution, Kautsky published *The Dictatorship of the Proletariat* (1918), criticizing Lenin for what he saw as the betrayal of democratic socialism in favor of a Jacobin minority dictatorship. Lenin responded with *The Proletarian Revolution and the Renegade Kautsky* (1918), one of the most savage polemics in modern political writing.

The Kautsky–Lenin split was the moment European socialism broke in two. The social-democratic wing — Kautsky, Bernstein eventually, the SPD majority — pursued parliamentary socialism through reform. The revolutionary wing — Lenin, Luxemburg, the new communist parties forming after 1918 — pursued the seizure of state power. The two wings would fight each other, sometimes physically, for the next seventy years.

## The First World War and the collapse of internationalism

The Second International had committed itself to internationalism. Working-class solidarity, the official line ran, would transcend national loyalty. If a war between European powers were to break out, the workers' parties would refuse to fight each other and would call general strikes against the war.

In August 1914, war broke out. The SPD, in the German Reichstag, voted unanimously to approve war credits — to fund the German army's attack on France, Belgium, and Russia. The French Socialist Party voted to support the French government. Workers across Europe enlisted, by the millions, to kill each other.

International socialism collapsed in those weeks — the Second International dissolved with it. Lenin, in Switzerland, watched the news and concluded that European socialism had betrayed itself permanently and that a new International — built only of revolutionary parties — was required. The Third (Communist) International was founded in Moscow in 1919.

The First World War is the hinge on which the entire twentieth century turns. We will return to it in Unit 4 when we cover the Russian Revolution.

## Other figures who matter

A short list of post-Marx theorists worth knowing:

**Antonio Labriola** (1843–1904) — Italian Marxist; first systematic Italian interpreter of Marx; major influence on Gramsci.

**Rudolf Hilferding** (1877–1941) — Austrian-German Marxist; *Finance Capital* (1910) is the canonical Marxian analysis of how finance and industry merged into a new dominant form of capitalism. He served as German finance minister in the Weimar Republic; he was murdered by the Gestapo in 1941.

**Otto Bauer** (1881–1938) — Austrian socialist; *Austro-Marxism* tradition; *The Question of Nationalities and Social Democracy* (1907), an attempt to integrate national identity into Marxist analysis.

**Plekhanov** (1856–1918) — "The father of Russian Marxism"; mentor and later opponent of Lenin; defended a more orthodox parliamentary line against Lenin's revolutionary one.

**Karl Korsch** (1886–1961) — German Marxist; *Marxism and Philosophy* (1923); together with Lukács, the founder of "Western Marxism" — the more philosophical, less party-bound tradition we will encounter in the next module.

## What this period accomplished

By 1920, Marxism had:

- Become a major political force, with mass parties in every European country
- Split between social-democratic reformists and revolutionary communists
- Been forced to confront the failure of its prediction that capitalism would collapse
- Developed new tools — theories of imperialism (Luxemburg, Hilferding, Hobson, Lenin), theories of finance capital, theories of the national question, theories of mass political organization

What it had not done: produced a successful workers' revolution in any of the advanced capitalist countries. The Russian Revolution of 1917 happened, but Russia was — by any classical Marxist standard — *not* an advanced capitalist economy. It was an agrarian, semi-feudal country with a small industrial proletariat in a few cities. That revolution would be one of the central events of the twentieth century. It is the subject of Unit 4, Modules 24 and 25.

For now: the post-Marx generation tried to translate the master's theory into practical politics, failed in some ways, succeeded in others, and bequeathed to the next generation a movement that was both larger and more divided than the one they had inherited.
