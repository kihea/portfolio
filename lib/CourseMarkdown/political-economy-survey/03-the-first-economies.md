The story most modern economics textbooks tell about the origin of money goes like this: once upon a time, people bartered. A farmer with eggs and a weaver with cloth would meet in a village square and trade. Eventually, because of the inconvenience of barter — what if the weaver does not want eggs today? — humans invented money to lubricate the exchange. Markets grew out of barter. The state came later, to protect the markets that already existed.

This story is wrong in almost every detail. It was invented by Adam Smith in 1776, repeated for two centuries, and decisively refuted by anthropologists. We start here because the wrongness matters: the false origin story is doing work in the rest of economic theory, and you cannot read Smith carefully until you can see the work it does.

## What anthropologists actually found

When ethnographers spread out across the colonial world in the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries, they looked for the barter society Smith had imagined. They did not find it. As Caroline Humphrey, an anthropologist at Cambridge, put it in 1985: "No example of a barter economy, pure and simple, has ever been described, let alone the emergence from it of money; all available ethnography suggests that there never has been such a thing."

What they found instead was a variety of arrangements that mostly fell into three patterns:

**Gift** — exchanges in which the timing, amount, and direct equivalence of return are deliberately blurred. You give your neighbor a fish today. She gives you a basket of berries next month. You give her son a knife at his wedding. None of these transactions are tallied, and to ask for an immediate return would be a profound insult. The relationship matters more than the accounting.

**Reciprocity** — patterned exchange between groups or kin, in which what circulates is roughly tracked but the obligation to give and receive is morally enforced rather than contractual. The Trobriand Islanders studied by Bronisław Malinowski in the 1910s practiced *kula* — a ceremonial exchange of necklaces and armbands that circulated for generations across hundreds of miles of ocean.

**Subsistence** — direct production by a household or kin group of most of what it consumes, with little exchange at all. The economy is the household. What is not consumed is stored or given away.

In all three patterns, what we call "the economy" is not separable from what we call "social relations." A gift creates an obligation. A refusal to give insults a person. A theft is an attack not on property but on the relationship that the property represented.

## The "original affluent society"

In a 1968 essay, the anthropologist Marshall Sahlins coined the phrase *the original affluent society* to describe hunter-gatherer life. The phrase was provocative because earlier writers — including Hobbes, who described pre-state life as "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — had assumed that pre-agricultural humans lived on the edge of starvation.

The actual evidence Sahlins compiled, drawing on field studies of the Hadza, the !Kung San, and others, told a different story. Hunter-gatherers in moderately fertile environments worked, on average, fifteen to twenty hours a week to feed and clothe themselves. The rest of the time was spent socializing, sleeping, telling stories, and visiting. Their material wants were modest because they could be met without strain.

Sahlins's claim is not that hunter-gatherer life was a paradise. Infant mortality was high. Violence was real. Some bands faced regular hunger. But the assumption that hunter-gatherers were on the brink of starvation — and therefore, by implication, that civilization rescued them — is not supported.

This matters for political economy because Smith and his successors built their theory of capitalism partly on the assumption that pre-agricultural humans had been desperately poor and that markets were a great liberation. If they were not, the liberation story is harder to tell.

## David Graeber and the deep history of debt

The late anthropologist David Graeber, in his 2011 book *Debt: The First 5,000 Years*, made the case for a still more radical reading. Graeber argued that the earliest measurements of credit and debt — found on cuneiform tablets in ancient Sumer dated to around 3500 BCE — predate the earliest coins by thousands of years. Long before money existed, people kept tallies of who owed whom.

In Graeber's reconstruction, the order is:

1. **Credit** comes first. People keep track of obligations in their heads and on tablets.
2. **States** emerge and centralize debt-keeping, often through temple complexes that doubled as economic accounting centers.
3. **Coinage** is invented much later — around 600 BCE in Lydia (modern Turkey) — largely to pay soldiers in mobile armies, where credit relationships could not be maintained.
4. **Markets**, in the sense of widespread anonymous trade priced in money, are a still later development, often driven and maintained by state authority.

Graeber's broader thesis is contested — historians and economists have pushed back on parts of it — but the basic chronology is now widely accepted: credit predates coinage, coinage predates markets in the modern sense, and the state was there for all of it.

The story Smith told had it backwards.

## Karl Polanyi and the place of the economy

The Hungarian economic historian Karl Polanyi, writing in the 1940s, drew the lesson out. In *The Great Transformation* (1944), Polanyi argued that in every known society before modern Europe, the economy was *embedded* in social relations. Economic activity served social, religious, and political ends. Land and labor were not commodities. Markets, where they existed, were peripheral — they served as occasional meeting places for traders, not as the central allocative mechanism of society.

What was historically unique about modern capitalism, in Polanyi's reading, was the *disembedding* of the economy: the construction of a society in which social relations served economic ends, rather than the other way around. Land and labor were turned into commodities — what Polanyi called "fictitious commodities," since neither was originally produced for sale. This transformation, he argued, did not happen naturally. It had to be imposed. We will return to Polanyi in detail in Module 7.

For now, the takeaway: before there were markets in our sense, there were economies. They worked, sustaining complex societies for millennia on a logic that was not the logic of price.

## Why the origin story matters

The classical economists who wrote between 1776 and 1850 made an assumption about pre-capitalist life that determined the rest of their theory. They assumed that humans had always traded for advantage, that money was a natural development, and that markets were the spontaneous form economic life would take if not interfered with by states.

If that assumption is wrong — if markets were a deliberate political construction, if pre-market economies functioned by gift and obligation, if money was invented by states to pay soldiers and only later flowed into everyday transactions — then a lot of what comes after needs rereading. Smith's "invisible hand" looks different if the hand was that of the British Parliament forcibly removing peasants from common land. Friedman's case for "free markets" looks different if free markets do not, historically, emerge without coercion.

You do not have to agree with this critique. You do have to know it exists, because every classical theorist we will read either assumed Smith's origin story or was reacting against it.

## A short list of figures from this module

- **Marshall Sahlins** (1930–2021) — American anthropologist; *Stone Age Economics* (1972), the source of the "original affluent society" thesis.
- **Bronisław Malinowski** (1884–1942) — Polish-British anthropologist; *Argonauts of the Western Pacific* (1922), the foundational study of *kula* exchange.
- **Marcel Mauss** (1872–1950) — French sociologist; *The Gift* (1925), a foundational text on the obligation structure of gift-giving across cultures.
- **Karl Polanyi** (1886–1964) — Hungarian-Austrian economic historian; *The Great Transformation* (1944), arguing that the market economy is a recent and constructed social form.
- **David Graeber** (1961–2020) — American anthropologist; *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011), reconstructing the deep history of credit and money.
