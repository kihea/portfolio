The institutional architecture of post-war Western capitalism — strong unions, high marginal tax rates, capital controls, full-employment macroeconomic policy, expanding welfare states — held together for roughly thirty years. It came apart in the 1970s under pressure from oil shocks, stagflation, declining productivity, and a falling rate of profit. It was replaced, in the years 1979–1984, by a different system whose basic features have held for forty years and structure most of the political-economic arguments in advanced economies today. This module covers the pivot.

## The crisis of the 1970s

The post-war Keynesian consensus — sometimes called the *embedded liberalism* of the Bretton Woods era — depended on conditions that all began to fail in the late 1960s.

**Productivity growth slowed.** Through the 1950s and 1960s, productivity in the U.S. and Western Europe had grown at roughly 3 percent per year. After 1973, growth slowed to roughly 1 percent and never fully recovered. Why is debated; some combination of the end of catch-up growth, the energy shocks, slowing technological innovation, and the costs of pollution and resource depletion.

**Inflation rose.** Average annual inflation in the United States rose from around 1–2 percent in the early 1960s to 6–7 percent by the early 1970s and peaked at 13.5 percent in 1980. The combination of rising inflation and stagnant growth — *stagflation* — was not supposed to be possible in the textbook Keynesian framework, which expected inflation to fall when unemployment rose.

**The Bretton Woods system collapsed.** The international monetary architecture established in 1944 had pegged most major currencies to the U.S. dollar, which was in turn pegged to gold at $35 per ounce. By the late 1960s, the United States no longer had enough gold to honor the convertibility commitment if foreign holders called for it. In August 1971, President Nixon ended dollar-gold convertibility. The system gave way to floating exchange rates and the eventual liberalization of capital flows.

**Oil prices exploded.** The 1973 OAPEC embargo, imposed in response to U.S. and Western support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War, quadrupled oil prices from roughly $3 to $12 per barrel. The 1979 Iranian Revolution doubled them again, to $30. The energy-intensive industrial economies of the West were not built for these prices, and the inflationary shock fed into general price increases.

**The wage-profit balance shifted.** The post-war labor compact had delivered rising real wages alongside rising profits. In the 1970s, this began to break down. Workers, with full employment behind them and strong union contracts in place, pushed for wages that compensated them for inflation, so profits fell and the conflict over the distribution of a slowing-growth pie became sharper.

By the late 1970s, the political center across the West was visibly losing confidence in the Keynesian framework. Center-left governments tried wage-price controls, incomes policies, and tripartite bargaining; they did not work. The intellectual ground had been prepared, over thirty years, by the Mont Pelerin Society and its institutional descendants. The political moment came in 1979.

## Margaret Thatcher (1979–1990)

Margaret Thatcher (1925–2013) became Conservative Party leader in 1975 and prime minister in May 1979. She was the first woman to hold either position and, intellectually, the first prime minister fully formed in the neoliberal movement. Her education in economics had come from the Centre for Policy Studies and from her advisor Keith Joseph, who had said in 1974 that the post-war Conservative governments had collaborated in a "ratchet to the left" and that the party needed to break with the consensus.

Thatcher's program in office had several elements:

**Monetary tightening to break inflation.** Thatcher's government adopted explicit monetary targets. The Bank of England, under government direction, raised interest rates sharply. Inflation fell from over 18 percent in 1980 to under 4 percent by 1983. The cost was a recession sharper than anything Britain had seen since the 1930s; manufacturing output fell by 17 percent in two years; unemployment rose from 1.4 million to over 3 million.

**Privatization of state-owned enterprises.** British Telecom (1984), British Gas (1986), British Steel (1988), British Airways (1987), British Petroleum, the water authorities, the electricity boards — Thatcher sold them all, in some cases at deliberately low prices to build a constituency of small shareholders. The privatization program raised tens of billions of pounds for the treasury and reshaped British industry. It also produced substantial windfall profits for senior executives and institutional investors and, in some cases (water, energy), set up oligopolistic private utilities that have been politically contentious ever since.

**Breaking the trade unions.** The decisive moment was the 1984–85 miners' strike. Arthur Scargill, the leader of the National Union of Mineworkers, led a strike to prevent the closure of allegedly uneconomic coal pits. Thatcher prepared for the confrontation: coal stockpiles, police mobilization, legal restrictions on union activity. The strike collapsed after a year. Mining communities across northern England, Wales, and Scotland were devastated. Trade union density in Britain, which had peaked at over 50 percent in 1979, declined steadily over the following two decades.

**Council house sales.** Thatcher's *Right to Buy* policy, enacted in 1980, allowed tenants of public housing to buy their homes at heavily discounted prices. The policy was politically masterful — it created a new constituency of homeowners with a stake in property values — and economically consequential. The public housing stock was substantially reduced; the bulk of the eventual capital gains went to former tenants (and increasingly to their inheritors); a long-run housing affordability crisis began.

**Financial deregulation.** The "Big Bang" of 1986 deregulated the London Stock Exchange, allowed banks to merge their commercial and investment functions, and opened the City of London to American and Japanese banks. London became one of the two global financial centers. Manufacturing's share of British output continued to decline; finance's share rose.

By 1990, when Thatcher was forced from office by her own party over the deeply unpopular poll tax, Britain was a substantially different country. The economy had been restructured toward services and finance; unions were weakened; inequality was higher; homeownership was higher; manufacturing employment had collapsed. The "Thatcher era" — defended by some as the necessary rejuvenation of a sclerotic economy, denounced by others as the wholesale dismantling of post-war social democracy — had set the pattern for political-economic transformation across the West.

## Ronald Reagan (1981–1989)

Ronald Reagan (1911–2004) came to the presidency in January 1981. He had been a Hollywood actor, an FBI informant during the post-war Hollywood communist scare, the Republican governor of California (1967–75), and the long-shot 1976 conservative challenger to Gerald Ford. He had spoken at Goldwater rallies in 1964 and been the most consistent national exponent of the neoliberal program throughout the 1970s.

Reagan's economic program was branded *supply-side economics*. Its main features:

**Tax cuts.** The Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981 cut the top marginal individual income tax rate from 70 percent to 50 percent (a further cut to 28 percent followed in 1986). The corporate tax rate was reduced. Capital gains taxes were cut. The theory — articulated most prominently by the economist Arthur Laffer — was that lower tax rates would so stimulate productive activity that revenues would actually rise. They did not. The federal deficit ballooned.

**Defense spending increases.** Reagan dramatically expanded the U.S. defense budget — from $158 billion in 1981 to $300 billion in 1989, in nominal terms. The increased spending, financed largely by deficits, may have contributed to the Soviet Union's strategic exhaustion; it certainly added enormously to U.S. national debt.

**Deregulation.** Banking, savings and loan associations, airlines (begun under Carter), trucking, telecommunications, and oil were deregulated. The deregulation of savings and loan associations led directly to the S&L crisis of the late 1980s, a major financial collapse that cost taxpayers roughly $124 billion.

**Hostility to organized labor.** Reagan's defining early act was firing 11,345 striking air traffic controllers in August 1981 and banning them from federal employment for life. The PATCO firings sent a signal to private-sector employers that aggressive anti-union action would not be politically penalized. The era of mass union-busting in the United States began.

**Welfare reform and retrenchment.** Cuts to federal social programs, tightening of eligibility, transfer of authority to states — Reagan's domestic program reversed the trajectory of welfare expansion that had been underway since the 1930s.

Reagan was succeeded by George H. W. Bush (1989–1993), who modulated some of Reagan's program but did not reverse it. The center-left Democrats under Bill Clinton (1993–2001) accepted most of the neoliberal framework — deregulation, free trade, welfare reform, balanced budgets — and added some adjustments around the edges. The new political center had moved.

## Paul Volcker and the monetary turn

The third figure who reshaped the global economy in this period was Paul Volcker (1927–2019), who chaired the U.S. Federal Reserve from August 1979 to August 1987. Appointed by Jimmy Carter and reappointed by Reagan, Volcker was a Democrat and a career central banker who arrived at the Fed with one priority: breaking inflation.

The methods were ferocious. Volcker raised the federal funds rate to over 20 percent by 1981; mortgage rates exceeded 18 percent. The U.S. economy entered the deepest recession since the 1930s: unemployment rose to 10.8 percent in late 1982, construction collapsed, and manufacturing employment fell by 2.5 million.

Inflation also collapsed — from 13.5 percent in 1980 to 3.2 percent by 1983. By the end of Volcker's tenure, inflation expectations had been substantially reset, and the U.S. economy entered a long period of stable prices that would last for thirty years.

The *Volcker shock* had several effects beyond U.S. inflation:

**The Latin American debt crisis.** Many developing countries, especially in Latin America, had borrowed heavily in U.S. dollars during the 1970s when interest rates were low. When the Volcker Fed pushed rates above 15 percent, debt service costs for these countries exploded. Mexico defaulted in August 1982; the crisis spread across Latin America. The "lost decade" of the 1980s — during which Latin American GDP per capita actually fell — was a direct consequence of the Volcker shock.

**The empowerment of capital relative to labor.** High interest rates and tight money disciplined wage demands and weakened unions, even where (as in the U.S. and U.K.) the central bank was nominally independent of partisan politics. Workers fighting for wage increases in a recession had little leverage.

**Globalization and capital mobility.** Volcker's tight money, combined with deregulation of capital flows, attracted enormous inflows of foreign capital into U.S. financial markets. The U.S. dollar appreciated substantially against other major currencies. Manufacturing exports became uncompetitive; the U.S. trade deficit widened. The era of large U.S. current account deficits financed by foreign capital inflows began.

**Institutional independence of central banks.** Volcker demonstrated that an independent central bank, willing to inflict short-term recession pain, could break entrenched inflation. The lesson was learned. Through the 1980s and 1990s, country after country granted formal independence to its central bank and set price stability as the primary mandate. The European Central Bank, founded in 1998, was modeled on this template. Modern central banks operate with substantial insulation from democratic political pressure — a deliberate institutional design intended to permit them to administer painful corrective policy when needed. Whether this insulation has been used well, or whether it has produced an undemocratic concentration of economic power, is one of the open debates of contemporary political economy.

## The Washington Consensus

By the late 1980s, the program that Thatcher, Reagan, and Volcker had implemented had crystallized into a set of policy prescriptions that the economist John Williamson, writing in 1989, summarized as the *Washington Consensus*. The ten points:

1. Fiscal discipline
2. Reorientation of public expenditures toward growth-promoting areas
3. Tax reform (broadening the base, cutting marginal rates)
4. Interest rate liberalization
5. Competitive exchange rates
6. Trade liberalization
7. Liberalization of inward foreign direct investment
8. Privatization of state-owned enterprises
9. Deregulation
10. Secure property rights

These prescriptions were applied — sometimes through choice, often through pressure from the IMF and World Bank in moments of crisis — to dozens of developing and post-communist countries through the 1990s. The results were mixed. Some countries (Poland, Chile post-Pinochet) achieved sustained growth. Others (Russia in the 1990s, Argentina, many African economies) experienced catastrophic outcomes. The "shock therapy" applied to the former Soviet bloc produced one of the largest peacetime economic collapses in history.

By 2000, the Washington Consensus had begun to lose intellectual credibility. The Asian financial crisis of 1997–98 had demonstrated the dangers of liberalized capital flows; the Russian collapse had shown the fragility of overnight market construction; the Argentine crisis of 2001 had shown the limits of currency-board pegs to the dollar. Even the IMF, by the mid-2000s, had begun to qualify its earlier prescriptions.

- **John Williamson** (1937–2021) — coined "Washington Consensus"
