The neoliberal turn of the late 1970s did more than change government policy. It restructured the relationship between finance, production, and labor across the entire global economy. Two trends define the period: *financialization* — the rising share of the economy organized around financial assets, debt, and rentier income — and *globalization* — the deepening integration of national economies through trade, capital flows, and supply chains. This module covers both.

## What financialization is

The American economist Gerald Epstein has offered a useful working definition: *financialization* is "the increasing role of financial motives, financial markets, financial actors, and financial institutions in the operation of the domestic and international economies."

Some specific empirical signs:

**Rising financial sector share of profits.** In the United States, the financial sector's share of total corporate profits rose from roughly 10 percent in the early 1970s to a peak of over 40 percent in the early 2000s. By any historical measure, this was extraordinary.

**Rising household debt.** U.S. household debt as a percentage of GDP rose from around 50 percent in 1980 to over 100 percent by 2008. The composition shifted, too — mortgage debt in particular ballooned during the housing bubble of the 2000s.

**Rising corporate non-financial firms' financial activity.** Industrial corporations increasingly derived a significant share of their profits not from their core productive activity but from financial operations — pension fund management, in-house financial services, supply-chain finance, currency hedging, share buybacks. General Electric in the 1990s and 2000s made more money from its financial subsidiary (GE Capital) than from making appliances.

**Shareholder value as the dominant corporate norm.** Through the post-war period, American corporations had generally operated under a "retain and reinvest" model — retaining earnings, reinvesting them in productive capacity. From the 1980s onward, the new norm was "downsize and distribute" — minimizing headcount and reinvestment, distributing earnings to shareholders through dividends and share buybacks. The economist William Lazonick has documented this shift in extensive detail.

**The growth of asset management.** The post-1980 period produced the rise of giant institutional asset managers — Vanguard, BlackRock, State Street, Fidelity — that now collectively own substantial blocks of every major public corporation in the United States. BlackRock alone manages over $10 trillion. The shareholding pattern of the global economy has become extraordinarily concentrated in a handful of asset management firms.

## What globalization is

The globalization of the late twentieth century had several specific dimensions:

**Trade liberalization.** Tariffs and trade barriers fell substantially. The Uruguay Round of GATT negotiations (1986–1994) created the World Trade Organization in 1995. China joined the WTO in 2001 — a singular event in modern economic history, because it integrated 1.3 billion low-wage workers into the global manufacturing system. By 2020, world merchandise trade was roughly six times its 1990 level.

**Capital account liberalization.** Capital controls — restrictions on the movement of money across national borders — were progressively dismantled across the OECD through the 1980s and across developing countries through the 1990s. By 2007, most of the world's major economies had largely open capital accounts. The IMF, which had originally defended capital controls under the Bretton Woods framework, became (in the 1990s) a strong advocate of liberalization.

**Manufacturing offshoring.** Lower trade barriers, falling shipping costs (containerization), and the integration of China and Eastern Europe into the global production system shifted manufacturing decisively from high-wage to low-wage countries. U.S. manufacturing employment fell from over 19 million in 1979 to roughly 12 million by 2020. Western European manufacturing employment fell in similar proportions. Chinese manufacturing employment rose from roughly 90 million in 1990 to over 200 million by 2014.

**Financial globalization.** Cross-border capital flows expanded enormously. Foreign direct investment, portfolio investment, and lending all rose multiple times relative to GDP between 1980 and 2007. International banks operated through complex networks of subsidiaries; cross-border claims by global banks reached roughly $30 trillion at the 2008 peak.

**Supply chain integration.** Manufacturing increasingly happened not in one country but across many. An iPhone was designed in California, manufactured with components from a dozen countries, assembled in China. The economist Richard Baldwin has called this the *second unbundling* — first goods were unbundled from where they were consumed; now production processes were unbundled across countries.

## Who won and who lost

The political-economic effects of financialization and globalization were unevenly distributed.

**Major winners.** Capital owners in advanced economies. The financial sector. China's coastal urban workforce. Educated professionals globally. The very rich everywhere.

**Major losers.** Lower-skill workers in advanced-economy manufacturing. African subsistence agriculture (whose products competed against subsidized U.S. and European exports). Many developing countries that liberalized their capital accounts and were then hit by sudden capital flight (Mexico 1994, Asia 1997, Russia 1998, Argentina 2001).

**Ambiguous.** The middle classes of advanced economies; lower-middle-income workers in developing countries.

The economist Branko Milanovic's *elephant chart* — published in his 2016 book *Global Inequality* — captured this distribution graphically. It showed, on a global scale, that real income gains between 1988 and 2008 had been very large for the global top 1 percent and for the world's emerging middle classes (mostly Asian, especially Chinese), and very small for the working classes of advanced economies. The shape of the chart looked like an elephant in profile, with a high "back" of emerging-economy workers and a high "trunk" of the global rich, separated by a depressed "head" of advanced-economy lower-middle classes.

This pattern has political consequences. The political backlash of the 2010s — Brexit, Trump, the European populist right, the gilets jaunes in France — drew much of its energy from people whose real incomes had been depressed by exactly the trade and capital flows that had lifted hundreds of millions in Asia out of poverty. The standard liberal response — *globalization is, on net, a positive-sum game; we should compensate the losers* — was correct as a description but politically inadequate as a program. The compensation, in practice, did not happen.

## Important theorists of this period

**Robert Brenner** (b. 1943) — American historian and political economist; *The Boom and the Bubble* (2002), *The Economics of Global Turbulence* (2006). The most detailed empirical analysis of why the global rate of profit declined from the 1970s and why financialization compensated.

**Giovanni Arrighi** (1937–2009) — Italian historical sociologist; *The Long Twentieth Century* (1994). Argued that financialization is a recurring feature of the hegemonic transitions in capitalist history — Genoese, Dutch, British, American — and that the late-twentieth-century U.S. shift to finance signaled the autumn of American hegemony.

**Robert Wade** (b. 1944) — British development economist; *Governing the Market* (1990); the leading critic of the Washington Consensus from a developmental state perspective.

**Joseph Stiglitz** (b. 1943) — American economist; Nobel laureate 2001; former chief economist at the World Bank; *Globalization and Its Discontents* (2002) was the first major insider critique of the IMF's role in the late-1990s crises.

**Hyman Minsky** (1919–1996) — American economist; the foundational theorist of *financial instability*. His *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy* (1986) developed the *financial instability hypothesis* — that prolonged stability in financial markets produces increasingly risky behavior that eventually destabilizes the system. Minsky was largely ignored during his lifetime; the 2008 crisis was widely described as a *Minsky moment*.

**Susan Strange** (1923–1998) — British international political economist; *Casino Capitalism* (1986), *Mad Money* (1998). Argued that the post-Bretton Woods financial system functioned more like a casino than a productive financial system, with consequences that should have been anticipated.

**Michael Hudson** (b. 1939) — American economist; *Killing the Host* (2015), among many other works; one of the most consistent contemporary critics of financialization, drawing explicitly on the classical economists' analysis of rent.

**Wolfgang Streeck** (b. 1946) — German sociologist; *Buying Time* (2014), *How Will Capitalism End?* (2016). Argued that the financialization-and-debt regime was a way for late capitalism to "buy time" against its own structural contradictions, and that the time was running out.

**Quinn Slobodian** (b. 1978) — Canadian historian; *Globalists* (2018), *Crack-Up Capitalism* (2023). The leading recent historian of how the neoliberal movement deliberately constructed international institutions to insulate global capital from democratic control.

## The Asian crisis as a cautionary tale

The Asian financial crisis of 1997–98 is worth dwelling on briefly, because it demonstrated the specific danger of liberalized capital flows in developing economies.

Through the 1990s, the East Asian "tiger" economies — Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, South Korea, the Philippines — had attracted enormous capital inflows from American, European, and Japanese banks. Borrowing was in foreign currency, especially U.S. dollars; the receiving banks and corporations onlent in domestic currency. The system worked as long as exchange rates were stable.

In July 1997, speculation against the Thai baht forced its devaluation. Within months, the speculative attack had spread to every major East Asian currency; capital fled, and banks and corporations that had borrowed in dollars and onlent in domestic currency found themselves unable to repay. GDP in Indonesia fell by 13 percent in 1998; Thailand by 10 percent; South Korea by 7 percent. Tens of millions were thrown back into poverty.

The IMF's response — austerity, structural adjustment, high interest rates, fiscal contraction — deepened the crisis in the countries it was supposed to be helping. Joseph Stiglitz, then chief economist at the World Bank, denounced the IMF's response publicly and was forced out of his position. The crisis substantially damaged the credibility of the Washington Consensus and pushed many emerging economies (especially in Asia) to accumulate enormous foreign exchange reserves as self-insurance — a development with major implications for global finance over the next two decades.

## What financialization and globalization built

By 2007, the global economic system that the neoliberal turn had constructed looked, on the surface, remarkably stable and impressively productive. The Great Moderation in advanced economies had produced two decades of stable, low inflation and modest but steady growth; hundreds of millions in Asia had escaped absolute poverty; trade volumes were at historic highs. Capital was free, technology was advancing, and financial innovation seemed to have made the system more resilient.

The system was also profoundly fragile in ways that were not, at the time, widely recognized. Household debt was at historic highs. Financial leverage in major banks was enormous (some U.S. investment banks were operating at 30:1 leverage). The shadow banking system — money market funds, securitization vehicles, repo markets — had grown to roughly the size of the regulated banking system and was substantially unregulated. The complex new financial instruments that the system had produced — collateralized debt obligations, structured investment vehicles, credit default swaps — were poorly understood even by their creators.

The first cracks appeared in August 2007; by September 2008, the system was in full collapse. The next module covers what happened.
