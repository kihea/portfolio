In September 2008, the global financial system came closer to total collapse than at any point since 1931. The investment bank Lehman Brothers went bankrupt on September 15; the world's interbank lending markets froze; money market funds began breaking the buck. Major banks across the United States and Europe were within days of failing without massive government intervention. For approximately seventy-two hours, the architecture of the global economy was visibly unraveling.

It was saved. By 2010 the immediate emergency was over. The deeper consequences — political, economic, ideological — are still unfolding sixteen years later. This module covers the crisis and the long aftermath that produced the contemporary world.

## What happened

The proximate cause was the collapse of the U.S. subprime mortgage market. Between 2000 and 2006, U.S. housing prices had risen at roughly 9 percent per year, far above any historical norm. Mortgages — including a growing volume to borrowers with little ability to repay (*subprime* and *Alt-A* loans) — were issued at scale and immediately sold to investment banks, which bundled them into mortgage-backed securities, which were further bundled into collateralized debt obligations, which were sold to investors around the world as low-risk, high-yield investments.

The credit rating agencies — Moody's, S&P, Fitch — rated the senior tranches of these instruments as AAA, the highest possible rating. The underlying mortgages, it turned out, were not. As housing prices peaked in 2006 and began to fall, mortgage defaults rose. The complex instruments began to lose value. The investment banks that had created them held large inventories that were suddenly worth far less than the banks had paid for them.

The losses, in absolute terms, were not initially enormous. Total subprime losses were estimated at around $500 billion — a substantial sum but a small fraction of the U.S. economy. What made the crisis systemic was the *leverage*. Major investment banks were operating at leverage ratios of 30:1 or higher; small losses on assets produced large losses on equity. The interconnections among major institutions, through derivatives and short-term funding markets, meant that one failure threatened all the others.

By mid-2008, Bear Stearns had been rescued by JPMorgan with Federal Reserve support. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the U.S. government-sponsored mortgage agencies, were taken into federal conservatorship in September. Lehman Brothers went bankrupt on September 15. AIG, the world's largest insurance company, was nationalized two days later to prevent its credit default swap obligations from triggering further cascading failures.

The Federal Reserve, under Ben Bernanke, deployed emergency powers that exceeded anything in its history. The Treasury, under Henry Paulson, persuaded Congress to authorize the $700 billion Troubled Asset Relief Program. The European Central Bank, the Bank of England, and other major central banks coordinated emergency liquidity provisions. By early 2009, the immediate crisis had been contained.

The recession that followed was the deepest since the 1930s. U.S. unemployment peaked at 10 percent. European unemployment, especially in southern Europe, was much higher (Spain reached 26 percent; Greece, in the eurozone crisis that followed, reached 27 percent). Tens of millions of jobs were lost. Trillions of dollars of household wealth, much of it concentrated in middle-class home equity, evaporated.

## The political economy of the response

The official response to the crisis had a particular shape that mattered enormously for what came after.

**Banks were bailed out; mortgage holders were not.** The U.S. TARP program provided massive support to major financial institutions, most of which survived and were profitable again within a few years. Programs aimed at helping individual mortgage holders avoid foreclosure — most prominently the Home Affordable Modification Program (HAMP) — were chronically underfunded, badly designed, and slow to deploy. Roughly 6 million American households lost their homes to foreclosure between 2007 and 2014.

**No senior executives were prosecuted.** Despite extensive evidence of fraud, mortgage misrepresentation, and other crimes in the lead-up to the crisis, the U.S. Justice Department under Attorney General Eric Holder did not prosecute any senior executive at any major financial institution. The economist Jed Rakoff, a federal judge, has written extensively about why this is. The contrast with the savings and loan crisis of the late 1980s — when roughly 800 bankers were jailed — is striking.

**Inequality widened.** The Federal Reserve's response to the crisis included unprecedented quantitative easing — direct purchase of financial assets to expand the money supply. The asset price inflation that followed disproportionately benefited those who already held financial assets, primarily the wealthy. By 2020, the share of U.S. wealth held by the top 1 percent had reached its highest level since the 1920s.

**Austerity followed in Europe.** The eurozone crisis of 2010–12 produced a different policy response: massive fiscal contraction imposed on countries — Greece, Ireland, Portugal, Spain, and Italy — by the IMF, the European Central Bank, and the European Commission. The Greek economy contracted by roughly 25 percent between 2010 and 2014, suicide rates rose, and public services collapsed. The Greek experience would be one of the defining experiences of European politics for the next decade.

The economist Adam Tooze's *Crashed* (2018) is the most thorough single account of the crisis and its global aftermath. The book's argument: the crisis was a transatlantic financial crisis, not the discrete national crises the various official accounts often suggested. The interlocking dollar funding markets, the shadow banking system, the cross-border bank balance sheets had produced a fundamentally global system whose collapse required a global response — which arrived, but in deeply uneven distribution.

## The Great Recession and after

The decade after 2008 was, in advanced economies, a period of unusually slow growth, persistent low inflation, near-zero interest rates, and rising political volatility.

**Slow growth.** GDP growth in the U.S. averaged around 2 percent annually between 2009 and 2019 — well below pre-crisis trends. European growth was slower still. Larry Summers and others described this as *secular stagnation* — a long-run shortfall of investment demand relative to savings, producing chronically low interest rates and low growth.

**Wage stagnation.** Real median wages in the U.S. continued the pattern that had begun in the 1970s — minimal growth despite continued productivity gains. The share of national income going to wages continued to fall; the share going to capital and to the very high-earner share of wages continued to rise.

**Rising deaths of despair.** The economists Anne Case and Angus Deaton documented a striking phenomenon in 2015: U.S. mortality rates among middle-aged white non-college-educated Americans were rising, driven by drug overdoses, alcohol-related liver disease, and suicide. The pattern was unprecedented in an advanced industrial economy outside of war or famine. Case and Deaton tied it directly to the long-term economic decline of working-class communities. Their 2020 book *Deaths of Despair and the Future of Capitalism* extended the analysis.

**The opioid epidemic.** Closely linked. Beginning in the late 1990s and accelerating through the 2000s and 2010s, prescription opioid addiction — driven heavily by aggressive marketing by Purdue Pharma and other manufacturers — produced over half a million U.S. overdose deaths by 2023. The epidemic was concentrated in deindustrialized communities and rural areas where the working-class economy had collapsed.

**Political backlash.** The 2010s produced a wave of anti-establishment political movements across the West — the Tea Party and then Trump in the U.S., Brexit and the rise of UKIP/Reform in the U.K., the Alternative für Deutschland in Germany, the Front National (later Rassemblement National) in France, the Five Star Movement and Lega in Italy, PiS in Poland, Fidesz in Hungary, Bolsonaro in Brazil. From the left, the response was Bernie Sanders in the U.S., Jeremy Corbyn in the U.K., Podemos in Spain, Syriza in Greece. Most of the left's responses lost; most of the right's responses won, at least once.

The historian Tony Judt, who died in 2010, had argued in his final book *Ill Fares the Land* (2010) that the social-democratic consensus of the post-war period had been deliberately dismantled and that the result was a population alienated from politics, vulnerable to demagoguery, and unable to imagine alternatives. The political trajectory of the 2010s and 2020s has largely vindicated Judt's diagnosis.

## What the crisis did not change

For all the disruption, the deep architecture of the post-1980 system remained substantially intact.

- The major banks were preserved as private entities, returning to record profitability within a few years.
- The basic framework of capital mobility, free trade (until 2018), and central bank independence held.
- The neoliberal program was modified at the edges (some financial regulation through Dodd-Frank, some retreat from peak globalization) but not fundamentally altered.
- The professional class that had managed the pre-crisis economy continued to manage it — with the same people, the same institutions, the same intellectual framework, minus some technical adjustments.

The most consequential political shifts have come not from the center — which has largely held to the post-1980 framework — but from the populist right, which has rejected free trade and free immigration without offering a coherent alternative economic model, and from the social-democratic left, which has been politically marginal in most major countries.

The world the crisis produced was not the world the crisis revealed — which was a financialized, deeply unequal, politically fragile economic order. The world it produced was the same order, now openly contested, with no clear alternative on offer.

## Modern Monetary Theory and the fiscal turn

One intellectual development of the post-crisis period deserves brief mention. *Modern Monetary Theory* (MMT) — associated with the economists Stephanie Kelton, L. Randall Wray, Warren Mosler, and Bill Mitchell — has argued that the conventional concern about government deficits is misplaced for countries that issue their own currency. Such governments can never run out of their own currency; the real constraint on government spending is inflation, not deficits. The 2008 crisis and its response, in which central banks created trillions of dollars in new money with no inflationary consequences (until 2021), seemed to support parts of the MMT argument.

MMT has been controversial. Critics — including most mainstream Keynesians — argue that the framework underestimates inflationary risk and the political difficulty of cutting spending or raising taxes when inflation does emerge. The 2021–23 inflation surge has substantially tempered the MMT-influenced enthusiasm for large fiscal stimulus that briefly dominated progressive economic policy circles. Whether the framework has lasting analytic value or whether it was a moment of post-crisis intellectual excitement is, again, a question still being argued.

## What to take from this module

Three things.

**The 2008 crisis was a real refutation of significant parts of the neoliberal claim**. The assertion that liberalized financial markets are self-regulating and self-stabilizing was decisively falsified. The Federal Reserve, the Treasury, and Congress — the most prominent partisans of self-regulating markets — discovered when the crisis came that they had to nationalize major firms, guarantee creditors, and inject trillions of dollars of public money to save the system from itself.

**The political response did not match the intellectual refutation**. The crisis produced no comparable shift in the operating logic of the economy. The political class that had managed the pre-crisis system continued to manage the post-crisis system. The major changes since 2008 have come from outside the political mainstream — from right-wing populism, from a brief socialist surge, from technological transformations, from the climate crisis — not from a renewed and coherent policy program.

**The shape of what comes next is unclear**. We are sixteen years past the crisis. The system that produced the crisis is still substantially in place. Whether what eventually replaces it is a renewed social democracy, an authoritarian capitalism on the Chinese or Russian models, a fragmented multipolar economic order, or something we cannot yet name is an open question. The final module of Unit 5 takes up the contemporary phenomena — platforms, precarity, climate — that are reshaping the system whether or not coherent political responses emerge.

## Figures to know

- **Ben Bernanke** (b. 1953) — Federal Reserve chair 2006–2014; presided over the crisis response
- **Henry Paulson** (b. 1946) — U.S. Treasury Secretary 2006–2009; designed the TARP
- **Timothy Geithner** (b. 1961) — New York Fed president, then Obama Treasury Secretary; criticized for protecting bank executives
- **Mario Draghi** (b. 1947) — ECB president 2011–2019; famously committed to do "whatever it takes" to save the euro in July 2012
- **Adam Tooze** (b. 1967) — *Crashed* (2018), the leading historical account of the crisis
- **Stephanie Kelton** (b. 1969) — MMT economist; *The Deficit Myth* (2020)
- **Anne Case** (b. 1958) and **Angus Deaton** (b. 1945) — *Deaths of Despair and the Future of Capitalism* (2020)
- **Thomas Piketty** (b. 1971) — *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2013); reshaped the empirical study of inequality
- **Mariana Mazzucato** (b. 1968) — *The Value of Everything* (2018); critique of rentier economy
