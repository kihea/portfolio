The word *economy* in something like its modern sense — meaning a self-contained sphere of national life, with its own laws, that can be studied separately from politics, religion, and morality — is younger than most students assume. The historian Margaret Schabas, in *The Natural Origins of Economics* (2005), tracks the shift across the eighteenth century. Before roughly 1750, *economy* meant household management or, by analogy, the careful arrangement of any complex thing. After roughly 1850, it meant what we mean today.

This module is about that shift. Not because the dictionary entry is interesting, but because the moment "the economy" became an object of study determined the questions that political economy would ask and the categories it would use. To understand Adam Smith, you have to know what he was inventing.

## Before political economy was a discipline

Pre-modern thinkers wrote about economic matters constantly — they just did not think of those matters as a separate domain. Aristotle, in the *Politics*, distinguished *oikonomikē* (the management of a household, including its productive estate) from *chrēmatistikē* (the unnatural pursuit of unlimited wealth). Money-lending at interest, in Aristotle's view, was inherently corrupt — money was supposed to be a means of exchange, not a thing that grew of itself. This argument echoed through Christian thought for the next two thousand years. Aquinas, writing in the thirteenth century, treated *usury* (lending at interest) as a sin because it sought a return where no productive labor had occurred.

Medieval Christian thought, in fact, had a sophisticated economic ethics. The *just price* doctrine held that the morally correct price for a good was one that allowed the seller to maintain his customary social position without taking advantage of the buyer's necessity. Markets existed, but were morally regulated. To raise prices during a famine was sinful. To pay a craftsman less than a customary wage was sinful. The medieval scholastics, far from being economically naive, produced an ethical framework far more elaborate than anything that followed.

What changed?

## The Reformation and the rehabilitation of profit

The Protestant Reformation, especially in its Calvinist branch, accomplished a slow ethical revolution. Max Weber, in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905), made the most famous argument: that Calvinist theology, by elevating diligent work in a worldly calling to religious significance and by treating worldly success as a possible sign of divine election, produced a culture in which the accumulation of wealth through methodical labor could be morally praised rather than merely tolerated.

Weber's thesis is contested. Many historians point out that Catholic Europe had its own commercial dynamism (Venice, Florence, Antwerp) and that Calvinist Geneva was no economic powerhouse. R. H. Tawney, in *Religion and the Rise of Capitalism* (1926), shifted the emphasis from theology to the broader cultural and institutional shifts the Reformation accompanied. The basic insight, however, has stuck. By 1700, the moral suspicion that had hung over commerce and money-lending for two millennia had thinned considerably in Protestant Europe. To make money was no longer a guilty pleasure. It was, increasingly, evidence of virtue.

## The scientific revolution and the search for natural laws

A parallel intellectual shift mattered. The seventeenth century produced Galileo, Newton, Descartes — and with them, a model of knowledge in which the world was governed by natural laws expressible in mathematical terms. Thinkers in many fields — biology, medicine, politics — tried to find the equivalents of Newton's laws in their own domains.

In economic thought, this produced the *Physiocrats* — a school of French thinkers in the mid-eighteenth century, led by François Quesnay, who argued that wealth ultimately came from land and agriculture (the only sphere where humans produced a genuine *net product* exceeding inputs) and that the economy followed a natural order which good policy should respect rather than disturb. The Physiocrats popularized the phrase *laissez-faire, laissez-passer* — "let it be, let it pass." They were, in important ways, the first systematic thinkers of capitalism, before Smith.

Quesnay's most famous work was the *Tableau Économique* (1758), an attempt to depict the circular flow of wealth through an economy. It is the first thing in the history of economics that looks remotely like a modern macroeconomic model. Marx, a century later, would call it a work of genius.

The Physiocrats were wrong about land being the only source of net wealth (industry would soon prove otherwise), and wrong about taxation — they recommended that all taxes fall on land, which produced political trouble. But they established two ideas that would shape everything after: that the economy had its own internal laws, and that those laws could be studied scientifically.

## The Scottish Enlightenment

The third stream feeding into the invention of political economy came from Scotland. Edinburgh, Glasgow, and Aberdeen between roughly 1740 and 1790 produced a remarkable cluster of thinkers — David Hume, Adam Ferguson, Lord Kames, Francis Hutcheson, and Adam Smith — who shared an interest in the historical development of human societies, the moral foundations of social order, and the workings of commerce.

Adam Ferguson's *Essay on the History of Civil Society* (1767) sketched a four-stage theory of social evolution — hunters, shepherds, agriculturalists, commercial society — that Smith and many later thinkers would use. Each stage had its own characteristic morality, politics, and forms of property. Commercial society, in this view, was a recent development, with specific virtues (freedom from feudal dependence, technological progress, individual liberty) and specific dangers (atomization, loss of civic virtue, dependence on the market).

Hume, in his *Political Discourses* (1752), wrote some of the earliest sophisticated economic essays — on money, interest, the balance of trade, public credit. Hume was already attacking the mercantilist obsession with bullion: a country that imported more than it exported would simply see its prices fall, making its goods more competitive, which would correct the trade balance. The *price-specie-flow mechanism*, as economists later called it, was a Humean discovery.

By the time Adam Smith published *The Wealth of Nations* in 1776, the intellectual ground had been prepared. There was a moral framework in which the pursuit of wealth could be defended. There was a scientific model in which the economy could be treated as a lawful system. There was a sociological tradition in which commercial society could be analyzed as one stage in human development.

Smith would synthesize these strands into the founding statement of classical political economy.

## What "the economy" became

Once political economy was established as a field, certain consequences followed.

First, it became possible to ask questions about *the economy* as such — its growth, its productivity, its stability, its fairness. Statistics on national output (what we now call GDP) would not be systematically calculated until the twentieth century, but the concept of a national economic aggregate begins here.

Second, certain things became invisible. The work done in households — overwhelmingly by women — was not counted as economic activity, because it was not paid. The labor of slaves was counted as the product of their owners. The unpaid reproductive labor that made wage labor possible was treated as belonging to nature, like rainfall. Marilyn Waring, in *If Women Counted* (1988), demonstrated how durable these omissions became and how they continue to distort economic measurement.

Third, the moral framework that had previously governed economic life — the just price, the prohibition of usury, the duties of charity — was relegated to private ethics. The economy became, in Polanyi's later phrase, *disembedded* from social relations.

Fourth, the economy became something policy could *target*. The mercantilists had managed trade and currency. The classical political economists would argue, instead, that the best policy was usually to leave the market alone. But both positions presumed that "the economy" was now a thing that policy was *about*.

## Figures to know

- **François Quesnay** (1694–1774) — court physician to Louis XV; founder of the Physiocrats; author of the *Tableau Économique*.
- **Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot** (1727–1781) — Physiocrat, briefly French finance minister; tried to abolish guilds and free the grain trade; failed and was dismissed; an important influence on Smith.
- **David Hume** (1711–1776) — Scottish philosopher; close friend of Smith; his economic essays anticipate several of Smith's positions.
- **Adam Ferguson** (1723–1816) — Scottish historian and moral philosopher; the four-stages theory.
- **Max Weber** (1864–1920) — German sociologist; *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*.
- **R. H. Tawney** (1880–1962) — British economic historian; *Religion and the Rise of Capitalism*.
- **Margaret Schabas** (b. 1954) — historian of economic thought; *The Natural Origins of Economics*.

## Reflection prompt

Before 1750, "economy" mostly meant household management. After 1850, it meant a separate national sphere with its own laws. What is gained — and what is lost — when an economic system becomes a thing that can be studied separately from politics, religion, and moral life?
