The intellectual movement that came to be called *neoliberalism* did not appear from nowhere in the 1970s. It was built — by a small network of intellectuals working over three decades, organized initially through a Swiss-registered association called the Mont Pelerin Society, funded by sympathetic businessmen and conservative foundations, propagated through think tanks, journals, and university appointments, and finally adopted by political leaders who used it to transform their countries' economies. This module covers the intellectual movement up to roughly 1979. The next two modules cover the political transition.

## The 1947 founding

In April 1947, thirty-nine intellectuals gathered at the Hôtel du Parc on Mont Pelerin, a mountain above Lake Geneva. The convener was Friedrich Hayek, an Austrian economist then teaching at the London School of Economics. The attendees included Ludwig von Mises, Milton Friedman, Frank Knight, George Stigler, Karl Popper, Michael Polanyi, Lionel Robbins, Bertrand de Jouvenel, and Wilhelm Röpke. The funding came principally from Swiss business interests and from the William Volker Fund in the United States.

The founding statement of the Mont Pelerin Society opened: "The central values of civilization are in danger." It identified two threats: the totalitarianisms of the recent past (fascism, Soviet communism) and the rising welfare states and Keynesian managed economies of the post-war West. The first was largely defeated; the second, in the founders' view, was the new and more insidious danger.

The Society's purpose, as articulated in the founding statement, was to redefine the function of the state, to defend the rule of law and private property, to oppose central planning and unrestricted state power, and to study the historical and political relationships between freedom and the market.

The Society did not have a single doctrine. Its members included pure libertarian anti-statists (Mises), constitutional liberals (Hayek), Catholic-conservative ordo-liberals (Röpke), and pragmatic Chicago-school empiricists (Friedman). What they shared was the belief that the post-war drift toward managed economies, welfare states, and Keynesian macroeconomic policy was a dangerous mistake, and that an organized intellectual counter-movement was needed to reverse it.

The Mont Pelerin Society still exists. It now has hundreds of members across dozens of countries and has held annual meetings continuously since 1947. Eight of its members have received the Nobel Prize in Economics (Hayek, Friedman, Stigler, Buchanan, Becker, Coase, North, Vernon Smith). Its institutional offspring — the American Enterprise Institute, the Heritage Foundation, the Cato Institute, the Institute of Economic Affairs in London, the Adam Smith Institute, the Atlas Network of think tanks worldwide — became some of the most influential policy organizations in modern history.

## Hayek's economic and political theory

Friedrich August von Hayek (1899–1992) was the central theorist of the movement. His most important works for political economy:

**The Road to Serfdom (1944).** Written during the Second World War, addressed to "the socialists of all parties." The argument: central economic planning, even when motivated by good intentions, leads inevitably to political tyranny. The planner has to make decisions about what gets produced and who gets what; these decisions cannot be made democratically (there are too many of them, and people disagree); they will therefore be made by an elite that gradually accumulates the power to suppress dissent. *Road to Serfdom* was condensed in *Reader's Digest* and sold widely in the United States and Britain. It is the most influential popular argument for the position.

**The Use of Knowledge in Society (1945).** A more technical essay that we have already met. Hayek's central argument: the information needed to allocate economic resources is dispersed across millions of individuals, each holding fragmentary local knowledge that no central authority can ever aggregate. Markets work not because participants are rational but because prices summarize and transmit dispersed information that would otherwise be lost. This is Hayek's epistemic argument for markets and against planning. It is one of the most important essays in modern economics.

**The Constitution of Liberty (1960).** Hayek's major statement of liberal political theory. Defends a constrained constitutional state — not the night-watchman state of strict libertarianism, but a state limited by the rule of law, protecting private property and contract, providing for a modest social safety net for those the market cannot accommodate. Hayek is sometimes misread as an anarcho-capitalist; he was not. He accepted a role for the state in education, infrastructure, basic income support, monopoly regulation, and environmental protection.

**Law, Legislation, and Liberty (3 vols., 1973–79).** Hayek's late synthesis. The book elaborates the distinction between *taxis* — deliberate human design — and *kosmos* — the spontaneous order that emerges from the interactions of many actors following abstract rules. Markets, languages, and common law are spontaneous orders that cannot be improved by deliberate central design without disrupting the dispersed information processing that makes them work.

Hayek won the Nobel Prize in 1974, in the year of stagflation that made his arguments suddenly seem newly relevant. The award marked a turning point in the intellectual standing of the movement.

## Friedman and the Chicago School

Milton Friedman (1912–2006) was the public face of the movement and the most consequential political economist of the second half of the twentieth century. His career as an academic was based at the University of Chicago, where he and George Stigler led what came to be called the *Chicago School*. His public career as a popularizer made him the most read economist of his generation.

Friedman's major theoretical contributions:

**A Monetary History of the United States, 1867–1960 (with Anna Schwartz, 1963).** A massive empirical work that reinterpreted American economic history — most importantly, the Great Depression — through a monetarist lens. Friedman and Schwartz argued that the Depression was caused not by a failure of capitalism (as Keynesian analysis had it) but by a failure of the Federal Reserve, which had allowed the money supply to contract by one-third between 1929 and 1933. If the Fed had instead expanded the money supply, the Depression could have been mitigated or avoided. This was a direct challenge to the Keynesian orthodoxy.

**Capitalism and Freedom (1962).** The popular statement of Friedman's political economy. The argument: economic freedom is a necessary (though not sufficient) condition for political freedom. Concentrating economic decisions in the state inevitably concentrates power in ways that threaten individual liberty. The state should be limited to functions where market failure is clear (defense, basic justice, externalities), and even then should be designed to minimize bureaucratic discretion.

**The negative income tax.** Friedman proposed, in *Capitalism and Freedom* and later writings, replacing the entire welfare state with a single guaranteed-income program that paid cash to people below a threshold and phased out smoothly as their earned income rose. The idea was that cash transfers were both more efficient and more dignifying than in-kind programs administered through paternalistic bureaucracies. The proposal influenced both the Nixon administration's failed Family Assistance Plan and the contemporary Universal Basic Income debate.

**The natural rate of unemployment / Phillips curve critique.** With Edmund Phelps, Friedman argued in 1968 that there is no stable long-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment. Attempts to push unemployment below its "natural rate" through expansionary monetary policy would simply produce accelerating inflation. This argument predicted stagflation before it happened and was empirically vindicated by the 1970s.

**Free to Choose (1980).** A ten-part PBS television series and accompanying book that explained Friedman's positions to a mass audience. *Free to Choose* may have been the single most influential piece of economic popularization in the twentieth century.

Friedman was also a consultant to the Pinochet government in Chile in the 1970s, advising on the design of the neoliberal program implemented after the 1973 coup. The visit became controversial and remained so throughout Friedman's career. Friedman defended his role on the grounds that economic freedom would eventually produce political freedom in Chile, a prediction that was substantially borne out only after Pinochet's dictatorship ended in 1990 and Chile transitioned back to democratic government.

## Other key figures

A short list of important neoliberal theorists beyond Hayek and Friedman:

**Ludwig von Mises** (1881–1973) — Austrian-American economist; mentor of Hayek; the most radical anti-statist of the movement; *Human Action* (1949) is the foundational text of the Austrian School of economics.

**James Buchanan** (1919–2013) — American economist; Nobel laureate 1986; founder of *public choice theory*, the application of economic methods to political behavior; argued that politicians and bureaucrats pursue their own self-interest just like everyone else, and that constitutional constraints on government were therefore necessary.

**Gary Becker** (1930–2014) — Chicago economist; Nobel laureate 1992; extended economic analysis to human behaviors (crime, family, education, discrimination) traditionally outside economics. Becker's framework became one of the most influential moves in late-twentieth-century economics, for better and worse.

**Aaron Director** (1901–2004) — Chicago professor; founder of *law and economics* as a field; argued that legal rules should be designed to mimic the outcomes a well-functioning market would produce.

**Wilhelm Röpke** (1899–1966) — German ordo-liberal; central architect of the post-war German social market economy; defender of a "third way" between Soviet planning and Anglo-American laissez-faire.

**Ronald Coase** (1910–2013) — British-American economist; *The Problem of Social Cost* (1960) introduced what became known as the *Coase Theorem*, arguing that externalities could in principle be resolved by private bargaining if transaction costs were low and property rights well-defined.

**Walter Eucken** (1891–1950) — German ordo-liberal; first generation of the Freiburg School; intellectually formed during the Weimar inflation, which shaped his commitment to strong rule-bound monetary institutions.

**Murray Rothbard** (1926–1995) — radical libertarian; pushed Austrian economics in an anarcho-capitalist direction; broke with the Mont Pelerin tradition over its acceptance of any state role.

## The infrastructure of the movement

What distinguished the neoliberal movement from other intellectual currents was its sustained, organized institutional infrastructure. By 1980, the movement included:

- The Mont Pelerin Society itself, with regional chapters and annual meetings
- A growing network of think tanks: the American Enterprise Institute (founded 1938, neoliberalized after the war), the Heritage Foundation (1973), the Cato Institute (1977), the Manhattan Institute (1978), the Hoover Institution at Stanford, the Institute of Economic Affairs in London (1955), the Centre for Policy Studies (Thatcher's brain trust, 1974), the Adam Smith Institute (1977)
- Foundations providing sustained funding: the Earhart Foundation, the John M. Olin Foundation, the Sarah Scaife Foundation, the Lynde and Harry Bradley Foundation, the Charles Koch Foundation
- Academic appointments at the University of Chicago, George Mason University, UCLA, and other strongholds
- A publishing program: the *Journal of Law and Economics*, the *Cato Journal*, the *Independent Review*, and a steady flow of trade books designed to influence policy debates
- Training programs for journalists, lawyers, and politicians, most notably the Federalist Society, founded in 1982

This infrastructure was not accidental. It was built deliberately, over decades, on the model the Mont Pelerin founders had set out in 1947. Hayek's famous 1949 essay *The Intellectuals and Socialism* had argued explicitly that ideas move from intellectuals to journalists to politicians to mass opinion, and that conservatives had to organize their intellectuals if they wanted to shift the political center over time. The movement followed his prescription.

## What the movement believed by 1979

By the end of the 1970s, the neoliberal program had cohered into a recognizable set of policy commitments:

- Deregulation of industry, especially financial services, transportation, energy, and telecommunications
- Privatization of state-owned enterprises and outsourcing of state functions
- Tax cuts, especially on capital, corporations, and high earners, on the theory that lower taxes would stimulate productive investment
- Reduction of state spending on social programs, with greater reliance on means-testing and private provision
- Weakening of organized labor: limits on the right to strike, employer protections against union activity, decline in union recognition
- Free trade and open capital markets; opposition to capital controls; gradual integration of the global economy
- Tight monetary policy aimed at controlling inflation, with central bank independence to insulate monetary decisions from democratic accountability
- Hostility to industrial policy and selective state intervention in particular sectors
- An emphasis on individual choice, market discipline, and property rights as foundational political values

This program was about to find its political moment. In 1979, Margaret Thatcher became prime minister of the United Kingdom. In 1980, Ronald Reagan was elected president of the United States. The transition is the subject of the next module.

## How to read this material

Neoliberal theorists are sometimes presented in critical literature as cartoon villains — they were not. Hayek was a careful, learned, often subtle thinker whose epistemic argument for markets is one of the most important contributions to twentieth-century social thought. Friedman was a brilliant empirical economist whose work on the Great Depression remains influential. The movement included Nobel laureates not because of conspiracy but because some of its arguments were correct.

What the movement got right: that central planning of complex economies has structural information problems; that overregulation can produce stagnation and rent-seeking; that price systems coordinate dispersed knowledge in ways that planners cannot; that inflation can become destructive if monetary policy is not credible.

What the movement got wrong: that markets, properly constructed, would produce optimal outcomes without the corrective intervention of states; that the structural inequalities markets generate would not corrode the political and social institutions on which markets themselves depend; that financial markets are self-stabilizing; that the social effects of the program — declining mobility, the hollowing-out of middle-class jobs, rising deaths of despair — were acceptable transitional costs.

The mixed verdict is, in this course, the only honest one. The movement transformed the world: some of that transformation was correction of real problems, some produced new and worse ones. The next four modules trace what happened.
