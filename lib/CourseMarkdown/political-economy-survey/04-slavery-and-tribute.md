The shift from village subsistence to civilizational scale required something new: a way to organize the labor of strangers. Three solutions emerged in roughly the same period across very different parts of the planet — slavery, tribute, and corvée (compulsory unpaid labor for the state). Each was specific, each was brutal, and each shaped political economy for the next four thousand years.

## The agrarian transition

Around 10,000 BCE, in several places independently — the Fertile Crescent, the Yangtze Valley, Mesoamerica, the Andes, the highlands of New Guinea — humans began cultivating plants and domesticating animals. This was not, recent evidence suggests, an unambiguous improvement in human welfare. Skeletons from early agrarian sites are shorter, more diseased, and show more signs of malnutrition than those from contemporaneous foragers. James C. Scott, in *Against the Grain* (2017), argues that the earliest states emerged precisely *because* concentrated grain agriculture could be taxed in a way that diverse foraging could not. The state did not invent agriculture; agriculture made the state possible.

What agriculture did unambiguously do was produce a surplus. A peasant family growing wheat in Mesopotamia could produce more grain than it could eat. The question — the political question — was what happened to the rest.

## Mesopotamia: the temple, the palace, and the tablet

The earliest known states, in southern Mesopotamia around 3500 BCE, organized their economies around twin institutions: the *temple* (religious authority) and the *palace* (political authority). Both held vast estates worked by a mix of dependents — temple slaves, debt slaves, free peasants paying rent in grain, and corvée laborers performing seasonal work in exchange for rations.

The economy was managed through a remarkable technology — cuneiform writing on clay tablets — that allowed administrators to track who owed what, who had delivered what, and how rations were to be distributed. The world's first writing was, in essence, accounting. Sumerian temple tablets recorded barley, beer, sheep, wool, and labor in standardized units. Workers were paid in fixed rations of grain and beer per day.

Slavery in Mesopotamia took several forms. Some slaves were captives of war. Some were sold by their families during famine. The most consequential category, however, was *debt slavery*. A peasant who could not repay a loan — often a loan of seed grain made by a temple or wealthy creditor — could be forced into servitude until the debt was worked off. Their wife and children could be pledged alongside. The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BCE) regulated this system, including time limits on debt slavery and protections against creditors selling debt slaves abroad.

The earliest large-scale economies, in other words, ran on *credit and debt*, and the management of debt was already a political question. Several Mesopotamian rulers issued *clean-slate* decrees — periodically erasing debts to prevent peasant immiseration from destabilizing the realm. The Hebrew Bible inherited this practice as the Jubilee year.

## Rome: the slave economy at scale

Slavery existed in many ancient societies. Rome industrialized it. By the late Republic and early Empire (roughly 100 BCE to 200 CE), perhaps a third of the population of Italy was enslaved. The proportion in some Roman provinces was higher.

Roman slavery was not racial. Slaves came from every part of the empire — Gauls, Germans, Greeks, North Africans, Britons, Jews. Most were prisoners of war. Some were the children of slaves (vernae). Some were sold into slavery by impoverished families.

The economic significance was immense. Roman agriculture, especially the large estates (*latifundia*) that emerged in the second century BCE, ran on slave labor. So did mining. So did much skilled artisanal production. Greek slaves often served as tutors, accountants, and physicians in wealthy households — a category of urban skilled slavery that has few modern parallels.

Rome's experience produced a distinct political economy:

- Massive concentration of land in the hands of senatorial families, accelerated by the displacement of smallholders into the army and then into the slums of Rome itself
- A reliance on continual military expansion to supply new slaves, which broke down once Rome stopped expanding
- A free urban population that received grain doles (the *annona*) and entertainment (gladiatorial games, chariot races) — the "bread and circuses" of which Juvenal complained
- A long structural crisis as slave supply slowed in the late empire and the *latifundia* shifted toward dependent peasant labor (the *coloni*), foreshadowing what would become medieval serfdom

The Roman slave system shaped Western political vocabulary in ways still with us. The English word *liberty* derives from the Latin *liber*, meaning "free" — but its meaning was originally defined against *servus*, "slave." Aristotle, working in a Greek slave society, argued in the *Politics* that some humans were "natural slaves" — a doctrine the Spanish empire would later use to justify the enslavement of Indigenous Americans.

## The Inca: redistribution without markets

The Inca Empire (c. 1438–1533), spanning the Andes from modern Ecuador to Chile, ran a complex economy with no money, no marketplaces, and very limited private property in land. It was, in its administrative scale, one of the most sophisticated pre-modern economies humans have built.

The system worked through *labor tribute*, called *mit'a*. Adult men in subject communities owed the state a portion of their labor each year. The labor was used to build roads, terraces, fortifications, and irrigation works, and to cultivate state lands. In exchange, the state stored grain and textiles in massive warehouses (*qollqa*) along the road network and redistributed them during famine, war, or hardship.

The Inca redistribution system used a *quipu* — a knotted-string recording device — to track tribute owed, delivered, and disbursed. The Spanish, arriving in 1532, found administrative records of remarkable accuracy. There were no markets in the European sense, no money, and very little long-distance private trade. Land was held collectively by the *ayllu* (kin group) and reallocated periodically to ensure each household had enough.

Anthropologists describe this as a *redistributive* economy — distinct from market exchange and from gift exchange, and structured around a central authority that gathered surplus and redistributed it on political and religious criteria. Karl Polanyi treated the Inca as a paradigmatic example of his thesis that the economy was embedded in social structures, not the other way around.

The Inca system supported tens of millions of people across vastly different ecological zones. The Spanish conquest dismantled it within a generation. It did not collapse because it could not feed its population; it collapsed because it could not survive smallpox, civil war over the Inca succession, and the introduction of European cavalry and steel.

## What these three systems share

Despite enormous differences — climate, religion, technology, geography — Mesopotamia, Rome, and the Inca state shared several features that mark them as something new in human history:

- **Centralized extraction of surplus from a peasantry by an elite who did not produce it**. Whether the mechanism was rent in kind, slavery, or labor tribute, the result was a flow of goods from many producers to a few non-producers.
- **Administrative literacy or its functional equivalent** (the quipu in the Inca case). Surplus extraction at scale requires record-keeping.
- **A monopoly of organized violence held by the extractive elite**. Slavery and tribute do not work without armed force backing them. Every one of these systems had standing armies, palaces, and the legal apparatus to enforce labor obligations.
- **Religious legitimation** of the extractive arrangement. Pharaohs were gods. Roman emperors were deified after death. Inca rulers were children of the Sun. The labor of peasants was framed as a religious duty, not merely a political one.

The point of this list is not to flatten three very different civilizations into one schema. The point is that, once humans crossed into agriculture at scale, certain political-economic problems became universal: how to organize surplus extraction, how to enforce it, how to justify it, and how to record it. The solutions varied; the problem did not.

## A note on what we are not covering

We are skipping a great deal — Egypt, China, Achaemenid Persia, Maurya India, the Maya, the Mexica (Aztec), African kingdoms like Mali and Songhai. Each had a sophisticated political economy worth studying. The course is structured around the lineage that leads most directly to modern capitalism — Mesopotamia and Rome for institutional inheritance, the Inca for contrast — but you should not take that focus to mean nothing else mattered.

## Figures to know

- **James C. Scott** (1936–2024) — political scientist; *Against the Grain* (2017), the case that the early state grew up around taxable grain agriculture.
- **Moses Finley** (1912–1986) — historian of antiquity; *The Ancient Economy* (1973), the standard re-reading of the Greco-Roman economy as a non-market system.
- **John Murra** (1916–2006) — anthropologist of the Andes; the leading twentieth-century scholar of the Inca economy.
- **Hammurabi** (r. c. 1792–1750 BCE) — Babylonian king; his Code is the earliest substantial legal record of debt slavery, wage labor, and price regulation.
