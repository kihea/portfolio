## VIDEO SCRIPT — "What Feudalism Was Actually Like"

**Runtime target:** 22–25 minutes
**Tone:** Documentary, plain, unrushed. Heavy use of period art, maps, manuscript pages. Citations on-screen, never spoken aloud unless attributed in dialogue.

### COLD OPEN — 0:00–0:45

[On screen: medieval illumination of peasants working a field. Slow zoom.]

**Narrator (VO):**
You have a picture in your head of a medieval peasant. It is probably wrong.

It is probably some combination of *Monty Python and the Holy Grail*, the Black Death, mud, illiteracy, and the assumption that a person from the year 1250 was a kind of half-human prototype waiting for the Enlightenment to come switch the lights on.

The actual evidence does not support this picture. The medieval peasant had a working economic system, a complex moral world, and a daily life with structure and rhythm that — once you understand it — looks less like primitive squalor and more like a coherent civilization with very different priorities than ours.

So today, we are going to look at what feudal Europe was actually like. Not the slogans. The receipts.

[Title card: WHAT FEUDALISM WAS ACTUALLY LIKE.]

---

### CHAPTER 1 — WHAT THE PEASANT OWED — 0:45–4:30

[On screen: medieval manorial roll, with labels overlaid identifying each entry as a "due."]

**Narrator (VO):**
Let us start with what we can measure. The historian Christopher Dyer, in his 1989 book *Standards of Living in the Later Middle Ages*, went through the manorial accounts of English estates between 1200 and 1500 and tried to reconstruct what a typical peasant household actually owed and actually consumed.

A serf on an English manor in the year 1250 owed the lord several things:

[Graphic: stacked list of obligations.]

— **Labor.** Roughly two to three days a week working on the lord's own land, called the demesne. More during harvest, when everyone worked from dawn to dark, often for two solid weeks.

— **Rent in kind.** A portion of what he grew, usually around a tenth, delivered to the lord's storehouse.

— **Customary dues.** Money or goods owed at specific events. The *heriot* — paid at death, often the best beast in the herd. The *merchet* — paid when his daughter married. The *banalities* — fees for using the lord's mill, oven, and wine press.

— **The tithe.** A tenth of his crop to the Church, separate from anything owed the lord.

This was real. It was also bounded. There was a customary list of what was owed, and going beyond it was actionable — peasants did, in fact, sue their lords in manor courts, and they sometimes won. The lord's authority was extensive, but it was not unlimited. It was *regulated* by something we might call constitutional custom, even though no one would have used that word.

[Cut to: re-enactment footage or animation of a manor court in session.]

The point of starting here is to dislodge the picture of arbitrary tyranny. Feudal exploitation was real. It was also rule-bound, predictable, and — in the eyes of the people inside it — *legitimate*. That last word is going to come back.

---

### CHAPTER 2 — WHAT THE PEASANT GOT — 4:30–9:00

[On screen: maps of medieval English villages with strip-field layouts.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Now flip the ledger. What did the peasant get in exchange?

— **Use of his strips.** A typical English serf farmed somewhere between 15 and 30 acres in scattered strips across the open fields. He kept what he produced, minus the dues we just listed.

— **Access to the commons.** The woods, pastures, meadows, and waste lands of the manor were not owned by anyone in the modern sense. They were used in common by the village, under detailed and ancient customary rules. A peasant could pasture his cow on the common, gather firewood from the woodland, fish in the streams, glean grain from the harvested fields. This was not charity. It was a *right*, established by use and protected — until the enclosures we will discuss next module — by the manorial court.

— **Protection.** In theory, the lord owed military protection from raiders, bandits, and rival lords. In practice, this was unreliable but real.

— **Adjudication.** Disputes between peasants were settled at the manorial court, which met regularly and ran on customary law. Most cases were not the lord against a peasant. They were peasant against peasant.

— **A floor.** This is the most important and the least obvious. The customary system bound the peasant in many ways, but it also bound the lord. A lord who tried to evict a long-standing tenant for no reason — to take his land for sheep, say — was breaking the custom of the manor and could be challenged. There was no concept yet of an "at-will" tenancy. The peasant had a kind of *security* that the modern worker, especially the modern renter, often does not.

[Graphic comparison: medieval rights vs. modern at-will employment / renting.]

The result was that a fourteenth-century English peasant — at least in the better years — ate enough calories to support hard physical labor, drank ale daily, had a few weeks of feast days off per year, and was guaranteed access to the resources he needed to feed his family. He was unfree. He was also, in certain economic respects, more secure than a twenty-first-century American with student debt and a six-month lease.

This is not romanticism. We will get to the famines, the wars, the plague, the violence. The point is that the system did something for both sides. It would not have held together for seven hundred years otherwise.

### CHAPTER 3 — TIME, WORK, AND THE LITURGICAL YEAR — 9:00–13:30

[On screen: illustrations from the *Très Riches Heures du Duc de Berry* — the famous fifteenth-century book of hours showing monthly peasant labors.]

**Narrator (VO):**
The medieval year was structured by religion. The calendar was a liturgical document. Easter, Christmas, Lent, Whitsun, Michaelmas — these were not just feast days. They were the dates around which agriculture, contracts, rents, and labor obligations were organized.

A French historian, Jacques Le Goff, made a famous distinction between *peasant time* — cyclical, governed by the seasons, religious feasts, and the natural rhythms of plant and animal life — and *merchant time* — linear, measured in increments, oriented toward profit and accumulation. The two coexisted uneasily in medieval Europe. The peasant lived in the first. The merchant in the towns lived increasingly in the second.

In peasant time, the workday was bounded by daylight, varied by the season, and punctuated by required rest. The Church mandated rest on Sundays and major feast days. Edward P. Thompson, the historian we will return to in Unit 4, calculated that the medieval English calendar may have included as many as 150 non-working days per year, between Sundays and saints' days and harvest breaks and customary observances.

This is more vacation than the modern American worker gets. Some of the historiography on this is contested — productive work could and did happen on feast days informally — but the basic picture stands. The relentless year-round labor we associate with modern industrial life was a *break* with the medieval pattern, not a continuation of it.

---

### CHAPTER 4 — THE PLAGUE THAT CHANGED EVERYTHING — 13:30–18:00

[On screen: Hans Holbein the Younger's *Dance of Death* woodcuts.]

**Narrator (VO):**
Between 1347 and 1351, somewhere between thirty and sixty percent of the population of Europe died. The Black Death — bubonic plague carried by fleas on rats, possibly with pneumonic and septicemic complications — arrived from Asia, swept through the Mediterranean, and rolled north. Whole villages disappeared. Some monasteries lost every monk.

The economic consequences were profound. Land that had been worked was now empty. Lords who had treated peasant labor as a guaranteed cheap input suddenly faced a labor shortage that gave surviving peasants something they had never had: leverage.

Wages — paid to wage laborers, who had always existed alongside the serfs — rose sharply. English laws were passed almost immediately trying to freeze them at pre-plague levels: the Ordinance of Labourers in 1349, the Statute of Labourers in 1351. They were widely ignored. Peasants left manors that paid too little to manors that paid more, despite serfdom's legal restrictions. Lords, desperate for labor, often accepted this rather than lose their tenants entirely.

When the lords tried to reassert old obligations through poll taxes in the 1370s, peasants in England exploded. The Peasants' Revolt of 1381, led by Wat Tyler and the priest John Ball, brought a peasant army into London. They burned tax records. They executed the Archbishop of Canterbury. They demanded the end of serfdom.

The revolt was crushed. But within a century, English serfdom was effectively dead. The lord's customary right to labor service was commuted to money rent. The serf became, in legal terms, a free tenant. The labor system that had supported European civilization for seven hundred years was no longer the dominant arrangement.

[On screen: John Ball's famous question: "When Adam delved and Eve span, who was then the gentleman?"]

**Narrator (VO):**
That sentence — *when Adam delved and Eve span, who was then the gentleman?* — is one of the earliest recorded English questions about the legitimacy of class hierarchy itself. It would take five more centuries before that question made its way into mass politics. It started here.

---

### CHAPTER 5 — WHY THE WORD "FEUDAL" MISLEADS — 18:00–21:30

[On screen: stack of competing historians' books on feudalism.]

**Narrator (VO):**
A note before we close.

Many medieval historians — Susan Reynolds most prominently — argue that the textbook picture of a "feudal pyramid" with neat layers of king, lord, vassal, and serf was constructed by later legal scholars trying to organize messy reality into a system. Practices varied enormously across regions and centuries. The neat hierarchy never existed exactly the way we draw it.

This is true and worth knowing. But it does not mean the underlying mechanism was an illusion. Across most of Europe between roughly 800 and 1400, the dominant arrangement for organizing rural production was a lord extracting rent — in kind, in labor, or eventually in money — from peasants who had customary rights to land they did not legally own. Whatever we call it, that was the system.

The reason this matters: when modern writers use "feudal" as an insult — calling tech monopolies neo-feudal, calling landlord rent feudal rent — they are trying to attach a specific historical mechanism to a current one. Sometimes the comparison is apt. Sometimes it dissolves on contact with the actual history. You now know enough to tell which is which.

### CLOSING — 21:30–24:00

[On screen: long shot of a present-day English field with the visible ridges of medieval strip cultivation still in the ground.]

**Narrator (VO):**
If you walk through certain parts of England today and squint at the right angle, you can still see ridges in the fields where the medieval plough turned its furrows. The system that put them there is gone, but the lines it scored into the earth are still here.

The point of this video is not to defend feudalism. It is to look at it the way historians actually look at it — as a working system, with internal logic, that did certain things for the people inside it and other things to them. That kind of looking is what political economy asks of you, regardless of which system is on the table.

Capitalism came next. It came out of feudalism's collapse, and it inherited some things from feudalism — the legal framework of property, the basic structure of rural production, the working assumption that some people give orders and others take them. But it also broke decisively with the feudal arrangement in ways the next modules will trace.

We will end this video with a short summary of what to take with you:

— Feudalism was a *direct extraction* economy, not a market economy.
— What the lord took was bounded by custom, not unlimited.
— Peasants had rights — including the commons — that the modern poor often do not.
— The Black Death broke the labor balance and ended serfdom in the West.
— The word "feudal" is contested by historians but useful as shorthand for a real mechanism.

That is the system the moderns inherited. Next time, what they did with it.
